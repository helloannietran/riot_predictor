<p>The <b>2006 Nuku<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>alofa riots</b> started on 16 November, in the <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tongan</a> capital of <a href="/wiki/Nuku%CA%BBalofa" title="Nukuʻalofa">Nuku<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>alofa</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_of_Tonga" title="Legislative Assembly of Tonga">Legislative Assembly of Tonga</a> was due to adjourn for the year and despite promises of action, had done little to advance democracy in the <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Tonga" title="Politics of Tonga">government</a>. A mixed crowd of democracy advocates took to the streets in protest. Some people in the crowd starting tipping over cars, then progressed to looting and burning buildings.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Targets_of_riots">Targets of riots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2006_Nuku%E2%80%98alofa_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Targets of riots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Riots broke out around 3:30 pm <a href="/wiki/UTC%2B13" class="mw-redirect" title="UTC+13">TOT</a> as rioters threw stones, broke windows, and looted. By about 6:00 pm, rioters started setting buildings on fire. The first targets of the rioters were government buildings. Then they attacked enterprises, including some that were leased to <a href="/wiki/Australia_and_New_Zealand_Banking_Group" title="Australia and New Zealand Banking Group">ANZ Bank</a> and those owned by the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Tonga" title="Prime Minister of Tonga">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Feleti_Sevele" title="Feleti Sevele">Feleti Sevele</a>.</p>
<p>A private shop selling mobile telephones and advertising for <a href="/wiki/Tonf%C3%B6n" title="Tonfön">Tonfön</a> (part of the Shoreline Group of Companies owned, at the time, by the royal family) was next. Rioters also attacked and burned the main office of the Shoreline Group of Companies, which was located one kilometer away from the small central business district.</p>
<p>Several of the larger Chinese shops were targeted for looting and burning. Other shops, including one owned by ethnic Indians, were burned as well, but it is not clear if they were intentionally set on fire or caught fire from surrounding buildings.</p>

Looters walking</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At about 6:00 PM TOT rioters torched the Royal Pacific hotel (owned by the Shoreline Group of Companies). The hotel was located on one of the main roads into the city. There are many commercial buildings on the thoroughfare, and the fire spread to some of those buildings as well.</p>
<p>According to an article in <i>Tonga Now</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Planet-Tonga_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Planet-Tonga-1">[1]</a></sup> normally law-abiding Tongans of both sexes and all ages were participating avidly in the looting. However, some photos<sup id="cite_ref-Planet-Tonga_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Planet-Tonga-1">[1]</a></sup> would seem to indicate that the car-tipping and arson were the work of young men. This conception would be reinforced after a destroyed Chinese shop was vandalized with graffiti.</p>
<p>At nightfall, the police and the <a href="/wiki/Tonga_Defence_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="Tonga Defence Services">Tonga Defence Services</a> regained control of the central business district and were turning away anyone who tried to enter.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2006_Nuku%E2%80%98alofa_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Estimates of the damage varied. Some estimates said that 60 to 80% of the central business district was destroyed.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dayafter.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Dayafter.jpg/300px-Dayafter.jpg" width="300" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Dayafter.jpg/450px-Dayafter.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Dayafter.jpg/600px-Dayafter.jpg 2x" data-file-width="768" data-file-height="448" /></a>

The day after, a journalist under the watchful eye of the army</div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="17_November">17 November</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2006_Nuku%E2%80%98alofa_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 17 November">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was announced that eight bodies had been found in charred ruins.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Since most of the employees of the affected businesses managed to reach safety, news reports speculated that the dead were likely looters. It is not clear if the deceased have been identified or if identified, when their names will be released.</p>
<p>The Tongan government declared a <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of emergency</a>. Only firefighters, police, utility workers, etc. were allowed inside a perimeter defined by Vuna road, <span class="unicode">ʻ</span>Alipate road, Mateialona road, and Tupoulahi road. Residents of that area could enter only after being searched. For the next month, gatherings of more than five persons were illegal in that area. Emergency laws gave security forces the right to stop and search people without a warrant.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Tongan government promised reform. Popular elections are to be held in 2008, in which a majority of the Legislative Assembly will be elected by a popular vote.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Chinese embassy chartered an airplane to evacuate Chinese nationals.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="18_November">18 November</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2006_Nuku%E2%80%98alofa_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 18 November">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>110 soldiers and 44 police officers from Australia and New Zealand arrived to help the local police to establish order. The New Zealand army was to be in charge of airport security and the police were to protect the <a href="/wiki/High_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="High Commission">High Commission</a>. The Australian contingent were to assist and relieving exhausted Tongan police.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19_November">19 November</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2006_Nuku%E2%80%98alofa_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 19 November">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A leader of the <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tongan</a> pro-democracy movement, MP <a href="/wiki/%27Akilisi_Pohiva" class="mw-redirect" title="'Akilisi Pohiva">'Akilisi Pohiva</a>, has criticised the intervention of Australian and New Zealand peacekeepers following the riots.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20_November">20 November</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2006_Nuku%E2%80%98alofa_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 20 November">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some businesses had temporarily relocated to the suburbs. Some looted items were returned. Police were guarding the telecommunications center and investigating mobile call logs. According to the <i>Matangi Tonga</i> newspaper, twenty-six arrests had been made and the number of deaths had been revised down to six.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="22_November">22 November</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2006_Nuku%E2%80%98alofa_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 22 November">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nukuʻalofa was by now largely peaceful. The town center was still cordoned off and heavily patrolled, but local shop owners and the like could easily get permission to enter the restricted area. Some Chinese shops which escaped damage were now open again. Major shops and banks, however, were still operating from temporary locations in the suburbs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1_December">1 December</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2006_Nuku%E2%80%98alofa_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1 December">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Peace was believed to be restored and foreign forces began leaving. The forbidden area in town was reduced. Police had made 571 arrests.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maintaining_the_state_of_emergency">Maintaining the state of emergency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2006_Nuku%E2%80%98alofa_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Maintaining the state of emergency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In late January 2008, the Tongan authorities renewed a Proclamation of Public Order for the sixteenth month running, a lingering aftermath of the riots. The statement reads: "It is hereby proclaimed that there continues to exist a state of danger" in central Nukuʻalofa. According to the Proclamation, the area will remain "controlled and maintained by the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tonga_Police_Force&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tonga Police Force (page does not exist)">Tonga Police Force</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tonga_Defence_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="Tonga Defence Services">Tonga Defence Services</a> for the sole purpose of maintaining public order for all people of the country".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Tongan information minister <a href="/wiki/Afualo_Matoto" class="mw-redirect" title="Afualo Matoto">Afualo Matoto</a> announced that the state of emergency would probably be maintained for another three months (i.e., until the end of April). This was criticised by Tongan pro-democracy advocate Akilisi Pohiva: "I don’t see any reason for government to continue to hold on to the emergency power."<sup id="cite_ref-RNZI_37686_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RNZI_37686-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The state of emergency was lifted in August 2008. Member of Parliament <a href="/wiki/%27Akilisi_Pohiva" class="mw-redirect" title="'Akilisi Pohiva">'Akilisi Pohiva</a> noted that the government had probably chosen to wait until after the coronation of King <a href="/wiki/George_Tupou_V" title="George Tupou V">George Tupou V</a> to put an end to it.<sup id="cite_ref-RNZI_41747_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RNZI_41747-13">[13]</a></sup> It was, however, re-imposed in September, despite the objections of pro-democracy members of Parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RNZI_41922_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RNZI_41922-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation_Kaliloa">Operation Kaliloa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2006_Nuku%E2%80%98alofa_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Operation Kaliloa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A joint investigation into the riots by the <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a> police, <a href="/wiki/Australian_Federal_Police" title="Australian Federal Police">Australian Federal Police</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Police" title="New Zealand Police">New Zealand Police</a>, codenamed <b>Operation Kaliloa</b>, has begun.</p>
<p>Tongan MP and former Police Minister <a href="/wiki/Clive_Edwards_(Tonga)" class="mw-redirect" title="Clive Edwards (Tonga)">Clive Edwards</a> claims that Tongan soldiers have beaten hundreds of people and then refused them treatment for their injuries. Edwards claims he was attacked by soldiers himself.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> An official of the Tongan Defence Force said there was no evidence to back up Edwards' claims. He would not give his name.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> On 2 February 2007, Edwards was arrested and charged with two counts of sedition arising from the riots.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The state of emergency declared on 17 November 2006 was extended several times. It was finally ended in January 2011 once the new Prime Minister took office following the <a href="/wiki/Tongan_general_election,_2010" title="Tongan general election, 2010">2010 election</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rebuilding">Rebuilding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2006_Nuku%E2%80%98alofa_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rebuilding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first business to rebuild and reopen was the Fung Shin supermarket, which opened in new premises on 19 December 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
