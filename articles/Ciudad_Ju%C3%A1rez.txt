<p><b>Ciudad Juárez</b> (<small>Spanish pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[sjuˈðað ˈxwaɾes]</a></span>, <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'w' in 'wind'">w</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'z' in 'Zion'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">WHAH</span></span></b>-rez</i></a></span>; <i>Juarez City</i>) is a city in the <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexican</a> <a href="/wiki/Mexican_state" class="mw-redirect" title="Mexican state">state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chihuahua_(state)" title="Chihuahua (state)">Chihuahua</a>. Commonly referred to by locals as simply <b>Juárez</b>, and known as <b>Paso del Norte</b> (<i>Pass of the North</i>) until 1888,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Juárez is the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Ju%C3%A1rez_(Municipality_of_Chihuahua)" class="mw-redirect" title="Juárez (Municipality of Chihuahua)">municipality of Juárez</a> with an estimated population of 1.5 million people.<sup id="cite_ref-twocities_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-twocities-4">[4]</a></sup> The city lies on the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande" title="Rio Grande">Rio Grande (Río Bravo del Norte)</a>, south of <a href="/wiki/El_Paso,_Texas" title="El Paso, Texas">El Paso, Texas</a>. Together with the surrounding areas the cities form <a href="/wiki/El_Paso%E2%80%93Ju%C3%A1rez" title="El Paso–Juárez">El Paso–Juárez</a>, the second largest bi-national metropolitan area on the <a href="/wiki/Mexico%E2%80%93United_States_border" title="Mexico–United States border">Mexico–United States border</a> (after <a href="/wiki/San_Diego%E2%80%93Tijuana" title="San Diego–Tijuana">San Diego–Tijuana</a>), with a combined population of over 2.7 million people.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Strategically placed on the <a href="/wiki/Mexico%E2%80%93United_States_border" title="Mexico–United States border">Mexico–United States border</a>, Ciudad Juárez has long been a center of <a href="/wiki/Illegal_drug_trade" title="Illegal drug trade">illegal drug trafficking</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ju%C3%A1rez_Cartel" title="Juárez Cartel">Juárez Cartel</a>'s long-standing control over the city's trafficking activities was challenged in 2007 by its former ally the <a href="/wiki/Sinaloa_Cartel" title="Sinaloa Cartel">Sinaloa Cartel</a>, leading to an exceptionally bloody <a href="/wiki/Turf_war" title="Turf war">turf war</a>. In the period from 2008 to 2012, violence between the two reached unprecedented heights as the city became known as "the most dangerous place on Earth".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> At the very height of the conflict, in 2010, there were an average 8.5 to 20 <a href="/wiki/Homicide" title="Homicide">homicides</a> (for a total of 3,766) and eight kidnappings per day. By 2012, the Sinaloa cartel had decisively gained the upper hand and violence declined sharply as a result. As of 2015 and 2016, perceptions of the city are much more positive. While killings do still occur, they do so at a much lower rate that is comparable to or lower than that in many American cities.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are four international ports of entry connecting Ciudad Juárez and El Paso, including the Bridge of the Americas, Ysleta International Bridge, Paso del Norte Bridge and Stanton Street Bridge. These combined allowed 22,958,472 crossings in 2008,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> making Ciudad Juárez a major point of entry and transportation into the U.S. for all of central northern Mexico. The city has a growing industrial center which is made up in large part by more than 300 <i><a href="/wiki/Maquiladora" title="Maquiladora">maquiladoras</a></i> (assembly plants) located in and around the city. According to a 2007 <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">New York Times</a></i> article, Ciudad Juárez "is now absorbing more new industrial real estate space than any other North American city."<sup id="cite_ref-twocities_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-twocities-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2008, <i><a href="/wiki/FDi_magazine" title="FDi magazine">fDi Magazine</a></i> designated Ciudad Juárez "The City of the Future."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez" title="Timeline of Ciudad Juárez">Timeline of Ciudad Juárez</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_El_Paso,_Texas" title="History of El Paso, Texas">History of El Paso, Texas</a></div>

<p>In 1659, as Spanish explorers sought a route through the southern <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a>, the Franciscan Friar García de San Francisco founded Ciudad Juárez as <i>Paso del Norte</i> ("North Pass"). The Misión de Nuestra Señora de Guadalupe became the first permanent Spanish development in the area. The Native American population was already located there. The <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> friars established a community that grew in importance as commerce between Santa Fe and <a href="/wiki/Chihuahua,_Chihuahua" class="mw-redirect" title="Chihuahua, Chihuahua">Chihuahua</a> passed through it. The wood for the bridge across the Rio Grande first came from <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe,_New_Mexico" title="Santa Fe, New Mexico">Santa Fe, New Mexico</a> in the 18th century. The original population of <a href="/wiki/Suma-Jumano" class="mw-redirect" title="Suma-Jumano">Suma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jumano" class="mw-redirect" title="Jumano">Jumano</a> and immigrants brought by the Spanish as slaves from Central <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">New Spain</a> grew around the mission. In 1680 during the <a href="/wiki/Pueblo_Revolt" title="Pueblo Revolt">Pueblo Revolt</a>, some members of the <a href="/wiki/Tiwa_people" title="Tiwa people">Tigua</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/Pueblo" title="Pueblo">Pueblo</a> became refugees from the conflict and a Mission was established for them in Ysleta del Paso del Norte. Other colonial era settlements included Senecú, Real de San Lorenzo, and the Presidio de San José. The population of the entire district reached some 5,000 around 1750, when the <a href="/wiki/Apache" title="Apache">Apache</a> attacked the other native towns around the missions. The 1848 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Guadalupe_Hidalgo" title="Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo">Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo</a> established the Rio Grande as the border between Mexico and the United States, separating the settlements on the north bank of the river from the rest of the town.</p>

<a href="/wiki/Benito_Juarez" class="mw-redirect" title="Benito Juarez">Benito Juarez</a> monument located in central Juarez</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Such settlements were not part of the town at that time; as the military set up its buildings the town grew around it. This would later become <a href="/wiki/El_Paso,_Texas" title="El Paso, Texas">El Paso, Texas</a>. From that time until around 1930 populations on both sides of the border could move freely across it. Ciudad Juárez and El Paso are one of the 14 pairs of <a href="/wiki/Cross-border_town_naming" title="Cross-border town naming">cross-border town naming</a> along the U.S.–Mexico border.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/French_intervention_in_Mexico" title="French intervention in Mexico">French intervention in Mexico</a> (1862–1867), El Paso del Norte served as a temporary stop for <a href="/wiki/Benito_Ju%C3%A1rez" title="Benito Juárez">Benito Juárez</a>'s republican forces until he established his <a href="/wiki/Government-in-exile" class="mw-redirect" title="Government-in-exile">government-in-exile</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chihuahua,_Chihuahua" class="mw-redirect" title="Chihuahua, Chihuahua">Chihuahua</a>. After 1882 the city grew with the arrival of the Mexican Central Railway. Banks, telegraph, telephone, and trams appeared, indicating the city's thriving commerce, in the firm control of the city's oligarchy of the Ochoa, Samaniego, Daguerre, Provencio, and Cuarón families. In 1888, El Paso del Norte was renamed in honor of Juárez.</p>
<p>The city expanded significantly thanks to <a href="/wiki/Porfirio_D%C3%ADaz" title="Porfirio Díaz">Díaz</a>'s free-trade policy, creating a new retail and service sector along the old Calle del Comercio (now Vicente Guerrero) and 16 September Avenue. A bullring opened in 1899. The Escobar brothers founded the city's first institution of higher education in 1906, the Escuela Particular de Agricultura. That same year, a series of public works are inaugurated, including the city's sewage and drainage system, as well as potable water. A public library, schools, new public market (the old Mercado Cuauhtémoc) and parks dotted the city, making it one of many Porfirian showcases.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Modern hotels and restaurants catered to the increased international railroad traffic from the 1880s on.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:199px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Presidents_Taft_and_Diaz,_Oct._1909.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/55/Presidents_Taft_and_Diaz%2C_Oct._1909.jpg" width="197" height="255" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="197" data-file-height="255" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Presidents_Taft_and_Diaz,_Oct._1909.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Taft and Díaz, historic first presidential summit, Ciudad Juárez, Mexico, 1909</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1909, Díaz and <a href="/wiki/William_Howard_Taft" title="William Howard Taft">William Howard Taft</a> planned a summit in Ciudad Juárez and El Paso, an historic first meeting between a Mexican and a U.S. president and also the first time an American president would cross the border into Mexico<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris20091_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris20091-14">[14]</a></sup> But tensions rose on both sides of the border over the disputed <a href="/wiki/Chamizal_dispute" title="Chamizal dispute">Chamizal strip</a> connecting Ciudad Juárez to El Paso, even though it would have been considered neutral territory with no flags present during the summit.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris200914_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris200914-15">[15]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Texas_Rangers_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="Texas Rangers Division">Texas Rangers</a>, 4,000 U.S. and Mexican troops, U.S. Secret Service agents, FBI agents and U.S. marshals were all called in to provide security.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris200915_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris200915-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Russell_Burnham" title="Frederick Russell Burnham">Frederick Russell Burnham</a>, the celebrated scout, was put in charge of a 250 private security detail hired by <a href="/wiki/John_Hays_Hammond" title="John Hays Hammond">John Hays Hammond</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHampton1910_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHampton1910-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDaily_Mail19097_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaily_Mail19097-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Wyk2003440.E2.80.93446_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Wyk2003440.E2.80.93446-19">[19]</a></sup> On October 16, the day of the summit, Burnham and Private C.R. Moore, a Texas Ranger, discovered a man holding a concealed <a href="/wiki/Protector_Palm_Pistol" title="Protector Palm Pistol">palm pistol</a> standing at the El Paso Chamber of Commerce building along the procession route.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris200916_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris200916-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHammond1935565-66_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHammond1935565-66-21">[21]</a></sup> Burnham and Moore captured, disarmed, and arrested the assassin within only a few feet of Díaz and Taft.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris2009213_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris2009213-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris200426_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris200426-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city was Mexico's largest border town by 1910—and as such, it held strategic importance during the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Revolution" title="Mexican Revolution">Mexican Revolution</a>. In May 1911 about 3,000 revolutionary fighters under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Madero" class="mw-redirect" title="Francisco Madero">Francisco Madero</a> laid siege to Ciudad Juárez, which was garrisoned by 500 regular Federal troops under the command of General Juan J Navarro. Navarro's force was supported by 300 civilian auxiliaries and local police. After two days of heavy fighting most of the city had fallen to the insurrectionists and the surviving federal soldiers had withdrawn to their barracks. Navarro then formally surrendered to Madero. The capture of a key border town at an early stage of the revolution not only enabled the revolutionary forces to bring in weapons and supplies from El Paso, but marked the beginning of the end for the demoralized Diaz regime.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the subsequent years of the conflict <a href="/wiki/Pancho_Villa" title="Pancho Villa">Villa</a> and other revolutionaries struggled for the control of the town (and income from the Federal Customs House), destroying much of the city during battles in <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez_(1911)" title="Battle of Ciudad Juárez (1911)">1911</a> and 1913. Much of the population abandoned the city between 1914 and 1917. Tourism, gambling, and light manufacturing drove the city's recovery from the 1920s until the 1940s. A series of mayors in the 1940s–1960s, like Carlos Villareal and René Mascareñas Miranda, ushered in a period of high growth and development predicated on the PRONAF border industrialization development program. A beautification program spruced up the city center, building a series of arched porticos around the main square, as well as neo-colonial façades for main public buildings such as the city health clinic, the central fire station, and city hall. The Cathedral, built in the 1950s, gave the city center the flavor of central Mexico, with its carved towers and elegant dome, but structural problems required its remodeling in the 1970s. The city's population reached some 400,000 by 1970.</p>

<p>Juárez has grown substantially in recent decades due to a large influx of people moving into the city in search of jobs with the <a href="/wiki/Maquiladoras" class="mw-redirect" title="Maquiladoras">maquiladoras</a>. As of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> more technological firms have moved to the city, such as the <a href="/wiki/Delphi_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Delphi Corporation">Delphi Corporation</a> Technical Center, the largest in the Western Hemisphere, which employs over 2,000 engineers. Large <a href="/wiki/Slum" title="Slum">slum housing</a> communities called <i><a href="/wiki/Colonia_(border_settlement)" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonia (border settlement)">colonias</a></i> have become extensive.</p>
<p>Juárez has gained further notoriety because of violence<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-25">[25]</a></sup> and as a major center of <a href="/wiki/Drug_trafficking" class="mw-redirect" title="Drug trafficking">narcotics trafficking</a> linked to the powerful <a href="/wiki/Ju%C3%A1rez_Cartel" title="Juárez Cartel">Juárez Cartel</a>, and for more than 1000 unsolved <a href="/wiki/Female_homicides_in_Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez" title="Female homicides in Ciudad Juárez">murders of young women</a> from 1993 to 2003.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to its location in the <a href="/wiki/Chihuahuan_Desert" title="Chihuahuan Desert">Chihuahuan Desert</a> and high altitude, Ciudad Juárez has a <a href="/wiki/Cold_desert_climate" class="mw-redirect" title="Cold desert climate">cold desert climate</a> (Köppen climate <a href="/wiki/BWk" class="mw-redirect" title="BWk">BWk</a>). Seasons are distinct, with hot summers, mild springs and autumns, and cold winters. Summer average high is 35&#160;°C (95&#160;°F) with lows of 21&#160;°C (70&#160;°F). Winter highs average 14&#160;°C (57&#160;°F) with lows of 0&#160;°C (32&#160;°F). Rainfall is scarce and greater in summer. Snowfalls occur occasionally (about 4 times a year), between November and March. On 26/27 December 2015, parts of the city received 40&#160;cm (16&#160;in) of snow within a 24-hour period beating the previous record of 28&#160;cm (11&#160;in) set in 1951.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The record high is 49&#160;°C (120&#160;°F) and the record low is −23&#160;°C (−9&#160;°F).</p>

<p>The average annual growth in population over a 10-year period [1990–2000] was 5.3%.<sup id="cite_ref-Business_Frontier_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Business_Frontier-28">[28]</a></sup> According to the 2010 population census, the city had 1,321,004 inhabitants, while the municipality had 1,332,131 inhabitants. During the last decades the city has received migrants from Mexico's interior, some figures state that 32% of the city's population originate outside the state of Chihuahua, mainly from the states of <a href="/wiki/Durango" title="Durango">Durango</a> (9.9%), <a href="/wiki/Coahuila" title="Coahuila">Coahuila</a> (6.3%), <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a> (3.7%) and <a href="/wiki/Zacatecas" title="Zacatecas">Zacatecas</a> (3.5%), as well as from <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a> (1.7%).<sup id="cite_ref-Business_Frontier_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Business_Frontier-28">[28]</a></sup> Though most new residents are Mexican, some also immigrate from <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central American</a> countries, such as <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>However, a March 2009 article noted there has been a mass exodus of people who could afford to leave the city due to the ongoing violence from the Mexican Drug War. The article quoted a city planning department estimate of over 116,000 abandoned homes, which could roughly be the equivalent of 400,000 people who have left the city due to the violence.<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_Street_Journal_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_Street_Journal-29">[29]</a></sup> An article in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> in September 2010 says of Ciudad Juárez – "About 10,670 businesses – 40% of the total – have shut down. A study by the city's university found that 116,000 houses have been abandoned and 230,000 people have left."<sup id="cite_ref-The_Guardian_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Guardian-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<p>Ciudad Juárez has many affluent neighborhoods, such as <a href="/wiki/Campestre_(Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez)" title="Campestre (Ciudad Juárez)">Campestre</a>, Campos Elíseos, and Misión de Los Lagos. Other neighborhoods, including <a href="/wiki/Anapra" title="Anapra">Anapra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chave%C3%B1a" title="Chaveña">Chaveña</a>, and Anáhuac, would be considered more marginal, while the remaining neighborhoods in Juárez represent the middle- to working-class, for example, Infonavit, Las Misiones, Valle de Juárez, Lindavista, Altavista, Guadalajara, Galeana, Flores Magón, Mariano Escobedo, Los Nogales, and Independencia.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The El Paso Regional Economic Development Corporation indicated that Ciudad Juárez is the metropolis absorbing "more new industrial real estate space than any other North American city."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The Financial Times Group through its publication The Foreign Direct Investment Magazine ranked Ciudad Juárez as the "City of the Future" for 2007–2008.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The Ciudad Juárez-El Paso area is a major manufacturing center. <a href="/wiki/ADC_Telecommunications" title="ADC Telecommunications">ADC Telecommunications</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrolux" title="Electrolux">Electrolux</a>, Bosch, Foxconn, <a href="/wiki/Flextronics" title="Flextronics">Flextronics</a>, Lexmark, Delphi, Visteon, Johnson Controls, Lear, Boeing, Cardinal Health, Yazaki, Sumitomo, and Siemens are some of the foreign companies that have chosen Ciudad Juárez for their business operation.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Mexican state of Chihuahua is frequently among the top five states in Mexico with the most foreign investment.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Many foreign retail, banking, and fast-food businesses have locations within Juárez.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_Bus_System">Public Bus System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Public Bus System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main public transportation system in the city is the Public Bus System. The public buses run the main streets of Cd. Juárez throughout the day, costing seven pesos (less than 40 cents) to ride one. Due the aging current bus fleet being considered potentially outdated, the Municipal Government is working on replacing the buses with new ones, along with improving the bus stops, such as by equipping them with shade.</p>
<p>The ViveBus <a href="/wiki/Bus_Rapid_Transit" class="mw-redirect" title="Bus Rapid Transit">Bus Rapid Transit</a> (BRT) system opened to the public in November 2013 with the first route of five planned. The project was made a reality with the collaboration of the local municipal government, the private enterprise of Integradora de Transporte de Juarez (INTRA) as well as other city government agencies. Studies have shown that the current bus system averages 8&#160;mph (13&#160;km/h) while the new system is projected to average 16&#160;mph (26&#160;km/h). The BRT system studies conducted by the Instituto Municipal de Investigacion Y Planeacion project a daily ridership of 40,000.</p>
<p>The first of the five routes opened to users in late 2013 and is officially named Presidencia-Tierra Nueva and has 34 stations distributed along the north to south corridor. The route starts at Avenida Francisco Villa, follows north to Eje Vial Norte-Sur then veers left at Zaragoza Blvd. and ends at Avenida Independencia and the elevated Carretera Federal 2.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airport">Airport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Airport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city is served by <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Gonz%C3%A1lez_International_Airport" title="Abraham González International Airport">Abraham González International Airport</a>, with flights to several Mexican cities. It accommodates national and international air traffic for the city. Nearby <a href="/wiki/El_Paso_International_Airport" title="El Paso International Airport">El Paso International Airport</a> handles flights to cities within the United States.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="International_border_crossings">International border crossings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: International border crossings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first bridge to cross the Rio Grande at El Paso del Norte was built in the time of <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">New Spain</a>, over 250 years ago, from wood hauled in from Santa Fe.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Today, this bridge is honored by the modern <a href="/wiki/Paso_del_Norte_International_Bridge" title="Paso del Norte International Bridge">Santa Fe Street Bridge</a>, and Santa Fe Street in downtown El Paso.</p>
<p>Several bridges serve the El Paso–Ciudad Juárez area in addition to the Paso Del Norte Bridge also known as the Santa Fe Street Bridge, including the <a href="/wiki/Bridge_of_the_Americas_(El_Paso%E2%80%93Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez)" title="Bridge of the Americas (El Paso–Ciudad Juárez)">Bridge of the Americas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stanton_Street_Bridge" title="Stanton Street Bridge">Stanton Street Bridge</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Ysleta%E2%80%93Zaragoza_International_Bridge" title="Ysleta–Zaragoza International Bridge">Ysleta Bridge also known as the Zaragoza Bridge</a>.</p>
<p>There is also a land crossing at nearby <a href="/wiki/Santa_Teresa,_New_Mexico" title="Santa Teresa, New Mexico">Santa Teresa, New Mexico</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Fabens%E2%80%93Caseta_International_Bridge" title="Fabens–Caseta International Bridge">Fabens–Caseta International Bridge</a> in nearby <a href="/wiki/Fabens,_Texas" title="Fabens, Texas">Fabens, Texas</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the latest estimates, the literacy rate in the city is in line with the national average: 97.3% of people above 15 years old are able to read and write.<sup id="cite_ref-Business_Frontier_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Business_Frontier-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Juárez has three public and two private universities. The <i><a href="/wiki/Instituto_Tecnol%C3%B3gico_de_Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez" title="Instituto Tecnológico de Ciudad Juárez">Instituto Tecnológico de Ciudad Juárez</a></i> (ITCJ), founded in 1964, became the first public institution of higher education in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_University_of_Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez" class="mw-redirect" title="Autonomous University of Ciudad Juárez">Autonomous University of Ciudad Juárez</a> (<i>Universidad Autónoma de Ciudad Juárez</i>, UACJ), founded in 1968, is the largest university in the city. It has several locations inside of the city including the Institute of Biomedical Sciences (<i>Instituto de Ciencias Biomédicas</i>, ICB), the Institute of Social and Administrative Sciences (<i>Instituto de Ciencias Sociales y Administrativas</i>, ICSA), the Institute of Architecture, Design and Art (<i>Instituto de Arquitectura, Diseño y Arte</i>, IADA), the Institute of Engineering and Technology (<i>Instituto de Ingeniería y Tecnología</i>, IIT) and the University City (<i>Ciudad Universitaria</i>, CU) located in the southern part of Ciudad Juárez. The IADA and IIT share the same location appearing to be a single institute where the students from both institutes share facilities as buildings or classrooms with the exception of the laboratories of Engineering and the laboratories of Architecture, Design and Arts. The UACJ also has spaces for Fine Arts and Sports.</p>

<p>These latter services are considered among the best because they recluse nearly 30,000 participants in sports such as swimming, racquetball, basketball and gymnastics, and arts such as <a href="/wiki/Ballet" title="Ballet">Classical Ballet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">Drama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Modern_Dance" class="mw-redirect" title="Modern Dance">Modern Dance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hula" title="Hula">Hawaiian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Polynesia" title="Music of Polynesia">Polynesian</a> Dances, <a href="/wiki/Folk_dance" title="Folk dance">Folk dance</a>, Music and <a href="/wiki/Flamenco" title="Flamenco">Flamenco</a>. The Faculty of Political and Social Sciences of the <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_University_of_Chihuahua" title="Autonomous University of Chihuahua">Autonomous University of Chihuahua</a> (<i>Universidad Autónoma de Chihuahua</i>, UACH) which has delivered 70% of the city's media and news crew, is located in the city. The local campuses of the <a href="/wiki/Monterrey_Institute_of_Technology_and_Higher_Education" title="Monterrey Institute of Technology and Higher Education">Monterrey Institute of Technology and Higher Education</a> (ITESM) and the Autonomous University of Durango (UAD) are private universities. The Monterrey Institute of Technology opened its campus in 1983. It is ranked as "third best" among other campuses of the institution, after the Garza Sada campus in Monterrey and the Santa Fe campus in Mexico City.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city is governed by a municipal president and an eighteen-seat council. The current president is Enrique Serrano Escobar, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Institutional_Revolutionary_Party" title="Institutional Revolutionary Party">Institutional Revolutionary Party</a> (PRI). Six national parties are represented on the council: the PRI, the <a href="/wiki/National_Action_Party_(Mexico)" title="National Action Party (Mexico)">National Action Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecologist_Green_Party_of_Mexico" title="Ecologist Green Party of Mexico">Ecologist Green Party of Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Party_of_the_Democratic_Revolution" title="Party of the Democratic Revolution">Party of the Democratic Revolution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Labor_Party_(Mexico)" title="Labor Party (Mexico)">Labor Party</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_Alliance_Party_(Mexico)" title="New Alliance Party (Mexico)">New Alliance Party</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crime_and_safety">Crime and safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Crime and safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Drug_War" title="Mexican Drug War">Mexican Drug War</a> and <a href="/wiki/Female_homicides_in_Ciudad_Juarez" class="mw-redirect" title="Female homicides in Ciudad Juarez">Female homicides in Ciudad Juarez</a></div>

<p>Violence towards women in the municipality has increased dramatically in the past twenty years.<sup id="cite_ref-femicide_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-femicide-37">[37]</a></sup> Since the early 1990s, approximately 370 girls and women have been murdered<sup id="cite_ref-Amnesty_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amnesty-38">[38]</a></sup> and at least 400 women have been reported missing.<sup id="cite_ref-femicide_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-femicide-37">[37]</a></sup> Escalating turf wars between the rival <a href="/wiki/Ju%C3%A1rez_Cartel" title="Juárez Cartel">Juárez</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sinaloa_Cartel" title="Sinaloa Cartel">Sinaloa Cartels</a> led to increasingly brutal violence in the city beginning in 2007.</p>
<p>The Juárez <a href="/wiki/Police_department" class="mw-redirect" title="Police department">police department</a> had approximately 800 officers dismissed in an effort to clean up corruption within its ranks.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Recruitment goals set by the department called for the force to more than double.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In 2009, a vigilante group calling itself Juárez Citizens Command threatened to take action to attempt to put a stop to all the perpetrators of violence if the government continued to fail to curb the violence in the city.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Government officials expressed concern that such <a href="/wiki/Vigilantism" class="mw-redirect" title="Vigilantism">vigilantism</a> would contribute to further instability and violence.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, General Moreno and the Third Infantry Company took over the fight against the cartels in town. They were removed in 2009, with the general and 29 of his associates now in custody and awaiting trial for charges of murder and civil rights violations.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>In response to the increasing violence in the city <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Armed_Forces" title="Mexican Armed Forces">the military</a> and <a href="/wiki/Federal_Police_(Mexico)" title="Federal Police (Mexico)">Federal Police</a>'s presence almost doubled. As of March 2009 at least 4500 soldiers and federal police were in the city to curtail mostly <a href="/wiki/Drug_cartel" title="Drug cartel">drug cartel</a> related violence.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> By August 2009 there were more than 7500 soldiers augmented by an expanded and highly restaffed municipal police force.<sup id="cite_ref-cnn4_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn4-45">[45]</a></sup></p>

<p>As of January 2013, Juárez's murder rate placed #37 of the highest reported in the world at 38 murders per 100,000 inhabitants.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> This marked a decrease of 70% compared to 2008 when the rate was 130 murders per 100,000 inhabitants and represented #1 in the statistic and exceeded second-place Caracas' statistic of 96 murders per 100,000 inhabitants by 35% for the same period.<sup id="cite_ref-presstv_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-presstv-47">[47]</a></sup> Journalist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bowden" title="Charles Bowden">Charles Bowden</a>, in an August 2008 <i><a href="/wiki/GQ" title="GQ">GQ</a></i> article, wrote that multiple factors, including drug violence, government corruption and poverty led to a dispirited and disorderly atmosphere that permeated the city.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GQ_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GQ-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crime_reduction">Crime reduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Crime reduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the homicide rates escalated to the point of making Ciudad Juárez the most violent city in the world, the city has seen a significant and steady decline in violent crime.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> In 2012, homicides were at their lowest rate since 2007 when drug violence flared between the <a href="/wiki/Sinaloa_Cartel" title="Sinaloa Cartel">Sinaloa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ju%C3%A1rez_Cartel" title="Juárez Cartel">Juárez Cartel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-warover_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-warover-50">[50]</a></sup> That trend has continued in 2015 with 300 homicides reported, the lowest number since 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Explanations for the rapid decline in violence include the success of the Sinaloa Cartel in defeating its rivals,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> as well as federal, state and local government efforts to combat crime and improve the city's quality of life.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Juárez has five local newspapers: <i>El Diario</i>, <i>El Norte</i>, <i>El Mexicano</i>, <i>El PM</i> and <i>Hoy</i>.</p>
<p>There are 16 <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_television" title="Terrestrial television">over the air</a> television stations broadcasting in the Ciudad Juarez - El Paso area. Including subchannels there are 33 different channels.</p>
<p>There are three paid television signals available and 24 radio station signals in <a href="/wiki/AM_broadcasting" title="AM broadcasting">AM</a> and 21 in <a href="/wiki/FM_broadcasting" title="FM broadcasting">FM</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city of Juárez plays a central part in <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/Just_Like_Tom_Thumb%27s_Blues" title="Just Like Tom Thumb's Blues">Just Like Tom Thumb's Blues</a>", which describe a nightmare vision of the narrator's experience in Juarez, in which he encounters sickness, despair, prostitutes, saints, shady women, corrupt authorities, alcohol and drugs, before finally deciding to return to New York City.</p>
<p>The city of Juárez is the setting for the 2015 film <i><a href="/wiki/Sicario_(2015_film)" title="Sicario (2015 film)">Sicario</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ciudad Juárez is the inspiration for the fictional city of Santa Teresa which features in the novel "<a href="/wiki/2666" title="2666">2666</a>" by Roberto Bolaño.</p>
