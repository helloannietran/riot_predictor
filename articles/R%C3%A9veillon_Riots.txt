			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Réveillon riots</h1>

<p>The <b>Réveillon riots</b> occurred between 26–29 April 1789<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> centered in the <a href="/wiki/Faubourg_Saint-Antoine" title="Faubourg Saint-Antoine">St. Antoine</a> district of Paris where a factory which produced luxury wallpaper was owned by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_R%C3%A9veillon" title="Jean-Baptiste Réveillon">Jean-Baptiste Réveillon</a>. The factory employed around 300 people. The riots were one of the first instances of <a href="/wiki/Violence" title="Violence">violence</a> during the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>. The factory where the riot took place was unusual in pre-revolutionary France as the factory was <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">guild</a>-free in an era where guilds controlled quality standards.</p>
<p>Protests began after rumors spread that the owner had made a speech stating that workers, many of whom were highly skilled, were to be paid lower wages and, as a result, there would be lower prices. Workers were concerned with food shortages, high unemployment, and low wages after a difficult winter in 1789. However, Réveillon was known for his benevolence towards the poor and actually stated that bread prices should be brought down to those that people could afford (below 15 <a href="/wiki/Solidus_(coin)" title="Solidus (coin)">sous</a> a day) but his comments were misinterpreted as wage restrictions. He made the comments on 21 April when the assembly of the Saint-Marguerite was discussing its <a href="/wiki/Cahiers_de_dol%C3%A9ances" title="Cahiers de doléances"><i>Cahier</i></a> which all Estates drew up before the Estates-General was to be called.</p>
<p>After informal protests on Sunday 26 April, groups of protesters congregated on the Ile de la Cité and in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Faubourg_Saint-Marcel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Faubourg Saint-Marcel (page does not exist)">Faubourg Saint-Marcel</a>, Marais, and Faubourg Saint-Antoine the next day for a series of protest-marches. Though the first three marches - one of which targeted the Third Estate's Assembly of Electors - were resolved peacefully, confrontations between troops and participants in the fourth demonstration led to the outbreak of violence in the Faubourg Saint-Antoine that evening.</p>
<p>While the protesters did not manage to destroy the factory as it was being guarded by a group of around fifty troops, a factory owned by the <a href="/wiki/Potassium_nitrate" title="Potassium nitrate">saltpetre</a> manufacturer Henriot was destroyed after he made similar comments.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However Réveillon’s factory was destroyed a day later as was his home<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-3">[3]</a></sup> The riot killed 25 people<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-3">[3]</a></sup> and wounded around the same number although rumour caused the casualty figures to be exaggerated. The <a href="/wiki/Gardes_Fran%C3%A7aises" title="Gardes Françaises">French Guard</a> were used to restore order.</p>
