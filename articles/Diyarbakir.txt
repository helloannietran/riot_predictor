<p><b>Diyarbakır</b> (<a href="/wiki/Kurdish_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Kurdish language">Kurdish</a>: <span lang="ku" dir="rtl" xml:lang="ku">Amed</span>‎)<sup id="cite_ref-Gunter_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gunter-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> is one of the largest <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">cities</a> in southeastern <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. Situated on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Tigris" title="Tigris">Tigris</a> River, it is the administrative capital of the <a href="/wiki/Diyarbak%C4%B1r_Province" title="Diyarbakır Province">Diyarbakır Province</a>. With a population of about 930,000 it is the second largest city in Turkey's <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_Anatolia_Region,_Turkey" class="mw-redirect" title="Southeastern Anatolia Region, Turkey">south-eastern Anatolia</a> region, after <a href="/wiki/Gaziantep" title="Gaziantep">Gaziantep</a>.</p>
<p>Diyarbakir is considered the unofficial capital of <a href="/wiki/Turkish_Kurdistan" title="Turkish Kurdistan">Turkish Kurdistan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gunter_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gunter-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jr.2015_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jr.2015-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hamelink2016_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hamelink2016-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AyersQuinn2009_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AyersQuinn2009-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MassicardWatts2012_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MassicardWatts2012-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LaberWhitman1988_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LaberWhitman1988-10">[10]</a></sup> As such, it has been a focal point for <a href="/wiki/Kurdish%E2%80%93Turkish_conflict_(1978%E2%80%93present)" title="Kurdish–Turkish conflict (1978–present)">conflict</a> between Turkey's government and <a href="/wiki/Kurdish_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Kurdish people">Kurdish</a> insurgent groups.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_and_etymology"><span id="Names_and_etymology"></span>Names and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diyarbak%C4%B1r&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Names and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Names_of_Diyarbak%C4%B1r_in_different_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Names of Diyarbakır in different languages">Names of Diyarbakır in different languages</a></div>
<p>The name Diyarbakir (<a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> دیار بکر "Diyaru Bakr which means the land of Bakir; <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">Armenian</a>: <span lang="hy" xml:lang="hy">Տիգրանակերտ</span> <i><a href="/wiki/Tigranocerta" title="Tigranocerta">Tigranakert</a></i>;<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient&#160;Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Άμιδα, <i><a href="/wiki/Amida_(Mesopotamia)" title="Amida (Mesopotamia)">Amida</a></i></span>; <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turkish_language" title="Ottoman Turkish language">Ottoman Turkish</a>: <span lang="ota-Arab" dir="rtl" xml:lang="ota-Arab">دیاربکر</span>‎ <i>Diyâr-ı Bekr</i>; <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a>: <span lang="syr" dir="rtl" xml:lang="syr">ܐܡܝܕ</span>‎) is inscribed as <i>Amid</i> on the sheath of a sword from the <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyrian</a> period, and the same name was used in other contemporary Syriac and Arabic works.<sup id="cite_ref-airlines_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-airlines-12">[12]</a></sup> The Romans and Byzantines called the city <i><a href="/wiki/Amida_(Roman_city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Amida (Roman city)">Amida</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-airlines_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-airlines-12">[12]</a></sup> Among the <a href="/wiki/Artukid" class="mw-redirect" title="Artukid">Artukid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Akkoyunlu" class="mw-redirect" title="Akkoyunlu">Akkoyunlu</a> it was known as "Black Amid" (<i>Kara Amid</i>) for the dark color of its walls, while in the <i>Zafername</i>, or eulogies in praise of military victories, it is called "Black Fortress" (<i>Kara Kale</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-airlines_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-airlines-12">[12]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Dede_Korkut" title="Book of Dede Korkut">Book of Dede Korkut</a> and some other Turkish works it appears as <i>Kara Hamid</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-airlines_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-airlines-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Arab_conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab conquest">Arab conquests</a> in the seventh century, the Arab <a href="/wiki/Banu_Bakr" title="Banu Bakr">Bakr tribe</a> settled in this region,<sup id="cite_ref-airlines_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-airlines-12">[12]</a></sup> which became known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Diyar_Bakr" title="Diyar Bakr">Diyar Bakr</a></i> ("<a href="/wiki/Real_Estate" class="mw-redirect" title="Real Estate">landholdings</a> of the Bakr tribe", in Arabic: ديار بكر <i>Diyar Bakr</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1937, <a href="/wiki/Atat%C3%BCrk" class="mw-redirect" title="Atatürk">Atatürk</a> visited Diyarbekir and, after expressing uncertainty on the exact etymology of the city, ordered that it be renamed "Diyarbakır", which means "land of copper" in Turkish after the abundant resources of <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> around the city.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diyarbak%C4%B1r&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest reference to the city comes from Assyrian records which identify it as being the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Arameans" title="Arameans">Aramean</a> kingdom of Bit-Zamani (ca. 1300 BC). In the ninth century BC, the city joined a rebellion against the Assyrian king <a href="/wiki/Shalmaneser_III" title="Shalmaneser III">Shalmaneser III</a>. The city was later reduced to being a province of the <a href="/wiki/Neo-Assyrian_Empire" title="Neo-Assyrian Empire">Neo-Assyrian Empire</a>.</p>
<p>From 189 BCE to 384 CE, the region to the east and south of present Diyarbakır came under the rule of the Hellenistic kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Corduene" title="Corduene">Corduene</a>.</p>
<p>Later, the Romans colonized the city and named it <a href="/wiki/Amida_(Roman_city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Amida (Roman city)">Amida</a>, after the earlier Assyrian name <a href="/wiki/Amid" class="mw-redirect" title="Amid">Amid</a>. During the Roman rule, the first city walls were constructed (297 AD) and later, the greater walls were built as per the command of the Roman emperor <a href="/wiki/Constantius_II" title="Constantius II">Constantius II</a>. After the Romans, the Persians came to power and were succeeded by the Muslim Arabs. It was the leader of the Arab Bekr tribe, Bekr Bin Vail, who named the city Diyar Bakr, meaning "the country of Bakr", i.e. Arabs. Much later, in the Republican era, the city got its current name Diyarbakır, which was derived from the abundance of copper ore that exists here.</p>
<p>After a few centuries, Diyarbakır came under the Ottoman Empire and earned the status of the capital of <a href="/wiki/Diyarbekir_Eyalet" title="Diyarbekir Eyalet">a large province</a>. The city became the base of army troops who guarded the region against Persian invasion. Diyarbakır faced turbulence in the 20th century, particularly with the onset of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. The majority of the city's Syriac and Armenian population were massacred and deported during the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Genocide" title="Armenian Genocide">Armenian Genocide</a> in 1915. In 1925, the Kurdish population rose in the <a href="/wiki/Sheikh_Said_rebellion" title="Sheikh Said rebellion">Sheikh Said rebellion</a> against the newly established government of the Republic of Turkey, which was crushed by Turkish forces.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquity">Antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diyarbak%C4%B1r&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The area around Diyarbakır has been inhabited by humans from the stone age with tools from that period having been discovered in the nearby Hilar cave complex. The pre-pottery neolothic B settlement of Çayönü dates to over 10,000 years ago and its excavated remains are on display at the <a href="/wiki/Diyarbak%C4%B1r_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Diyarbakır Museum">Diyarbakır Museum</a>. Another important site is Girikihaciyan Tumulus in <a href="/wiki/E%C4%9Fil" title="Eğil">Eğil</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first major civilization to establish themselves in the region of what is now Diyarbakır were the <a href="/wiki/Hurrians" title="Hurrians">Hurrian</a> kingdom of the Mitanni. The city was first mentioned by Assyrian texts as the capital of a Semitic kingdom. It was then ruled by a succession of nearly every polity that controlled Upper Mesopotamia such as the <a href="/wiki/Aramean" class="mw-redirect" title="Aramean">Aramean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Assyrian Empire">Assyrians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urartu" title="Urartu">Urartu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenians" title="Armenians">Armenians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Achaemenid Persians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medes" title="Medes">Medes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Parthians" class="mw-redirect" title="Parthians">Parthians</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> gained control of the city in 66 BC by when it was named "Amida".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 359, <a href="/wiki/Shapur_II_of_Persia" class="mw-redirect" title="Shapur II of Persia">Shapur II of Persia</a> <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Amida" title="Siege of Amida">captured Amida</a> after a siege of 73 days which is vividly described by the Roman historian <a href="/wiki/Ammianus_Marcellinus" title="Ammianus Marcellinus">Ammianus Marcellinus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Command.2C_Kimberly_Kagan_p23_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Command.2C_Kimberly_Kagan_p23-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_bishopric">Christian bishopric</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diyarbak%C4%B1r&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Christian bishopric">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Syriac_Christianity" title="Syriac Christianity">Syriac Christianity</a> took hold in the region between the 1st and 4th centuries AD, particularly amongst the Semitic <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_people" title="Assyrian people">Assyrians</a> of the city. The earliest documented bishop of Amida was Simeon of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_East" title="Church of the East">Church of the East</a>, who took part in the <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Nicaea" title="First Council of Nicaea">First Council of Nicaea</a> in 325, on behalf of the city's <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_people" title="Assyrian people">Assyrian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aramean" class="mw-redirect" title="Aramean">Aramean</a> Christians. Maras was at the <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Constantinople" title="First Council of Constantinople">First Council of Constantinople</a> in 381. In the next century, Saint <a href="/wiki/Acacius_of_Amida" title="Acacius of Amida">Acacius of Amida</a> (who died in 425, and is included in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Martyrology" title="Roman Martyrology">Roman Martyrology</a><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>) was noted for having sold the church's gold and silver vessels to ransom and assist Persian prisoners of war.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine Emperor">Byzantine Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_II" title="Theodosius II">Theodosius II</a> (408–450) divided the <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">Roman province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia_(Roman_province)" title="Mesopotamia (Roman province)">Mesopotamia</a> into two, and made Amida the capital of Mesopotamia Prima, and thereby also the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_see" class="mw-redirect" title="Metropolitan see">metropolitan see</a> for all the province's <a href="/wiki/Bishopric" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishopric">bishoprics</a>. A 6th-century <i><a href="/wiki/Notitia_Episcopatuum" class="mw-redirect" title="Notitia Episcopatuum">Notitia Episcopatuum</a></i> indicates as <a href="/wiki/Suffragan" class="mw-redirect" title="Suffragan">suffragans</a> of Amida the sees of <a href="/wiki/Silvan,_Diyarbak%C4%B1r" title="Silvan, Diyarbakır">Martyropolis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia_(Roman_province)#Episcopal_sees" title="Mesopotamia (Roman province)">Ingila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia_(Roman_province)#Episcopal_sees" title="Mesopotamia (Roman province)">Belabitene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia_(Roman_province)#Episcopal_sees" title="Mesopotamia (Roman province)">Arsamosata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sophene" title="Sophene">Sophene</a>, Kitharis, <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia_(Roman_province)#Episcopal_sees" title="Mesopotamia (Roman province)">Cefa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia_(Roman_province)#Episcopal_sees" title="Mesopotamia (Roman province)">Zeugma</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Annuario_Pontificio" title="Annuario Pontificio">Annuario Pontificio</a></i> adds <a href="/wiki/Cizre" title="Cizre">Bethzabda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia_(Roman_province)#Episcopal_sees" title="Mesopotamia (Roman province)">Dadima</a>.</p>
<p>The names of several of the successors of Acacius are known, but their orthodoxy is unclear. The last whose orthodoxy is certain is Cyriacus, a participant in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Council_of_Constantinople" title="Second Council of Constantinople">Second Council of Constantinople</a> (553). Many bishops of the Byzantine Empire fled in the face of the Persian invasion of the early 7th century, with a resultant spread of the <a href="/wiki/Jacobite_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacobite Church">Jacobite Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_the_Syrian" title="Michael the Syrian">Michael the Syrian</a> gives a list of Jacobite bishops of Amida down to the 13th century.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>At some stage, Amida became a see of the Armenian Christians. The bishops who held the see in 1650 and 1681 were in communion with the Holy See, and in 1727 Peter Derboghossian sent his profession of faith to Rome. He was succeeded by two more Catholic Armenians, Eugenius and Ioannes of Smyrna, the latter of whom died in <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> in 1785. After a long vacancy, three more bishops followed. The diocese had some 5,000 Armenian Catholics in 1903,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> but it lost most of its population in the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Genocide" title="Armenian Genocide">Armenian Genocide</a>. The last <a href="/wiki/Diocesan_bishop" title="Diocesan bishop">diocesan bishop</a> of the see, Andreas Elias Celebian, was killed with some 600 of his faithful in the summer of 1915.<sup id="cite_ref-Gams1_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gams1-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gams2_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gams2-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>An eparchy for the local members of the <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Catholic_Church" title="Syriac Catholic Church">Syriac Catholic Church</a> was established in 1862. <a href="/wiki/Ignatius_Philip_I_Arkus" title="Ignatius Philip I Arkus">Ignatius Philip I Arkus</a>, who was its first bishop, was elected patriarch in 1866, he kept the governance of the see of Amida, which he exercised through a patriarchal vicar. The eparchy was united to that of <a href="/wiki/Mardin" title="Mardin">Mardin</a> in 1888. Persecution in Turkey during the First World War brought an end to the existence of both these Syrian residential sees.<sup id="cite_ref-Gams1_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gams1-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gams2_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gams2-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> However, in 1966 a <a href="/wiki/Chaldean_Catholic_Archeparchy_of_Amida" title="Chaldean Catholic Archeparchy of Amida">Chaldean Catholic Archeparchy</a> with jurisdiction over all Chaldean Catholic Turks was revived in Diyarbakir, with the city being both the episcopal see and location of the dioceses <a href="/wiki/St._Mary_Church,_Diyarbak%C4%B1r" title="St. Mary Church, Diyarbakır">main Cathedral</a>.</p>
<p>As of 2015, there are two Assyrian Churches, and 3 Armenian churches in at least periodic operation. three other churches are in ruins, all Armenian. one outside Sur district, one in it, and one in the citadel that is now part of a museum complex.</p>
<p>No longer a residential bishopric, Amida is today listed by the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Titular_see" title="Titular see">titular see</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diyarbak%C4%B1r&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Diyar_Bakr" title="Diyar Bakr">Diyar Bakr</a></div>

<p>In 639, the city was captured by the <a href="/wiki/Muslim_conquests" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim conquests">Muslim conquests</a>, and introduced to the religion of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>. The city passed under <a href="/wiki/Umayyad" class="mw-redirect" title="Umayyad">Umayyad</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Abbasid" class="mw-redirect" title="Abbasid">Abbasid</a> control, but with the progressive fragmentation of the Abbasid Caliphate from the late 9th century, it periodically came under the rule of autonomous dynasties. <a href="/wiki/Isa_ibn_al-Shaykh_al-Shaybani" title="Isa ibn al-Shaykh al-Shaybani">Isa ibn al-Shaykh al-Shaybani</a> and his descendants ruled the city and the wider <a href="/wiki/Diyar_Bakr" title="Diyar Bakr">Diyar Bakr</a> from 871 until 899, when Caliph <a href="/wiki/Al-Mu%27tadid" title="Al-Mu'tadid">al-Mu'tadid</a> restored Abbasid control, but the area soon passed to another local dynasty, the <a href="/wiki/Hamdanids" class="mw-redirect" title="Hamdanids">Hamdanids</a>. The latter were displaced by the <a href="/wiki/Buyids" class="mw-redirect" title="Buyids">Buyids</a> in 978, who were in turn followed by the <a href="/wiki/Marwanids" title="Marwanids">Marwanids</a> a few years later. The Marwanids ruled until after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Manzikert" title="Battle of Manzikert">Battle of Manzikert</a> in 1071, when the city came under the rule of the <a href="/wiki/Mardin" title="Mardin">Mardin</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/Oghuz_Turks" title="Oghuz Turks">Oghuz Turks</a> and then the <a href="/wiki/Anatolian_beylik" class="mw-redirect" title="Anatolian beylik">Anatolian beylik</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Artuqids" title="Artuqids">Artuqids</a>. The whole area was then disputed between the <a href="/wiki/Ilkhanate" title="Ilkhanate">Ilkhanate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ayyubid" class="mw-redirect" title="Ayyubid">Ayyubid</a> dynasties for a century, after which it was taken over by the competing Turkic federations of the <a href="/wiki/Kara_Koyunlu" title="Kara Koyunlu">Kara Koyunlu</a> (the Black Sheep) first and then the <a href="/wiki/Aq_Qoyunlu" class="mw-redirect" title="Aq Qoyunlu">Aq Qoyunlu (White Sheep Turkomans)</a> until the rise of the Persian <a href="/wiki/Safavids" class="mw-redirect" title="Safavids">Safavids</a>, who naturally took over the city and the wider region.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safavids_and_Ottomans">Safavids and Ottomans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diyarbak%C4%B1r&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Safavids and Ottomans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Diyarbekir_Eyalet" title="Diyarbekir Eyalet">Diyarbekir Eyalet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diyarbekir_Vilayet" title="Diyarbekir Vilayet">Diyarbekir Vilayet</a></div>

<p>Between the early 16th century and mid-to late 17th century the city and the much wider Eastern Anatolia region (comprising <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Anatolia" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Anatolia">Eastern Anatolia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_Anatolia" class="mw-redirect" title="Southeastern Anatolia">Southeastern Anatolia</a>) was being heavily competed between the rivalling Persian <a href="/wiki/Safavids" class="mw-redirect" title="Safavids">Safavids</a> and the Ottoman Turks, being passed on numerous times between the two arch rivals. When it was firstly conquered by the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turks" class="mw-redirect" title="Ottoman Turks">Ottoman Turks</a> in the 16th century by the campaigns of Bıyıklı Mehmet Paşa under the rule of Sultan <a href="/wiki/Selim_I" title="Selim I">Selim I</a> following the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Chaldiran" title="Battle of Chaldiran">Battle of Chaldiran</a>, they established an <a href="/wiki/Eyelet" class="mw-redirect" title="Eyelet">eyelet</a> with its centre in Diyarbakır. The Ottoman eyelet of Diyarbakır corresponded to Turkey's southeastern provinces today, a rectangular area between the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Urmia" title="Lake Urmia">Lake Urmia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Palu_(District),_Elaz%C4%B1%C4%9F" class="mw-redirect" title="Palu (District), Elazığ">Palu</a> and from the southern shores of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Van" title="Lake Van">Lake Van</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cizre" title="Cizre">Cizre</a> and the beginnings of the <a href="/wiki/Syrian_desert" class="mw-redirect" title="Syrian desert">Syrian desert</a>, although its borders saw some changes over time. The city was an important military base for controlling this region and at the same time a thriving city noted for its craftsmen, producing glass and metalwork. For example, the doors of <a href="/wiki/Mevlana" class="mw-redirect" title="Mevlana">Mevlana</a>'s tomb in <a href="/wiki/Konya" title="Konya">Konya</a> were made in Diyarbakır, as were the gold and silver decorated doors of the tomb of <a href="/wiki/Ebu_Hanife" class="mw-redirect" title="Ebu Hanife">Imam-i Azam Ebu Hanife</a> in <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a>. Ottoman rule was confirmed by the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Amasya" title="Peace of Amasya">Peace of Amasya</a> of 1555 which followed after the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman%E2%80%93Safavid_War_(1532%E2%80%9355)" title="Ottoman–Safavid War (1532–55)">Ottoman–Safavid War (1532–55)</a>. However, a recapture of the city followed by Safavid Persia, ruled by shah <a href="/wiki/Abbas_I_of_Persia" title="Abbas I of Persia">Abbas I</a>, during the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman-Safavid_War_(1603-1618)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ottoman-Safavid War (1603-1618)">Ottoman-Safavid War (1603-1618)</a>.</p>
<p>In 1895 an estimated 25,000 <a href="/wiki/Armenian_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Armenian people">Armenians</a> and Syriac Christians ("<a href="/wiki/Assyrian_people" title="Assyrian people">Assyrians</a>") were <a href="/wiki/Massacres_of_Diyarbakir_(1895)" class="mw-redirect" title="Massacres of Diyarbakir (1895)">massacred</a> in Diyarbakır vilayet, including the city.<sup id="cite_ref-gunter_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gunter-31">[31]</a></sup> At the turn of the 19th century, the Christian population of the city was mainly made up of Armenians and <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Orthodox_Christians_(Middle_East)" title="Syriac Orthodox Christians (Middle East)">Syriac Orthodox Christians</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The city was also a site of <a href="/wiki/Armenian_genocide" class="mw-redirect" title="Armenian genocide">ethnic cleansing</a> of Armenians and Assyrians in 1915; nearly 150,000 were deported from the city.<sup id="cite_ref-mdumper_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mdumper-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Republic_of_Turkey">Republic of Turkey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diyarbak%C4%B1r&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Republic of Turkey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In the reorganization of the provinces, Diyarbakır was made administrative capital of the <a href="/wiki/Diyarbak%C4%B1r_Province" title="Diyarbakır Province">Diyarbakır Province</a>. During the 1980s and 1990s, at the peak of the <a href="/wiki/Kurdistan_Workers%27_Party" title="Kurdistan Workers' Party">Kurdistan Workers' Party</a> (PKK) insurgency, the population of the city grew dramatically as villagers from remote areas where fighting was serious left or were forced to leave for the relative security of the city. After the cessation of hostilities between the PKK and the Turkish army, a large degree of normality returned to the city, with the Turkish government declaring an end to the 15-year period of emergency rule on 30 November 2002. Diyarbakır grew from a population of 30,000 in the 1930s to 65,000 by 1956, to 140,000 by 1970, to 400,000 by 1990,<sup id="cite_ref-McDowall2004_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McDowall2004-34">[34]</a></sup> and eventually swelled to about 1.5 million by 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 41-year-old American-Turkish <a href="/wiki/Pirin%C3%A7lik" class="mw-redirect" title="Pirinçlik">Pirinçlik</a> Air Force Base near Diyarbakır, known as NATO's frontier post for monitoring the former Soviet Union and the Middle East, closed on 30 September 1997. This closure was the result of the general drawdown of US bases in Europe and the improvement in space surveillance technology. The base housed sensitive electronic intelligence-gathering systems that monitored the Middle East, the Caucasus, and Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to a November 2006 survey by the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sur,_Turkey&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sur, Turkey (page does not exist)">Sur</a> Municipality, one of Diyarbakır's metropolitan municipalities, 72% of the inhabitants of the municipality use <a href="/wiki/Kurdish_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Kurdish language">Kurdish</a> most often in their daily speech, followed by <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> with small minorities of <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_people" title="Assyrian people">Assyrians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenian_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Armenian people">Armenians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yezidi" class="mw-redirect" title="Yezidi">Yezidis</a> still resident. After World War II, as the Kurdish population moved to urban centres, Diyarbakir gradually became predominantly Kurdish.<sup id="cite_ref-HeperSayari2013_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HeperSayari2013-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Diyarbakır has been the victim of terror attacks in recent years. In 2008, a <a href="/wiki/2008_Diyarbak%C4%B1r_bombing" title="2008 Diyarbakır bombing">car bomb exploded</a> in the city, killing five people, a blast for which nobody claimed responsibility. In 2015, <a href="/wiki/2015_Diyarbak%C4%B1r_rally_bombings" title="2015 Diyarbakır rally bombings">a political rally</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Peoples%27_Democratic_Party_(Turkey)" title="Peoples' Democratic Party (Turkey)">People's Democratic Party</a> was targeted by <a href="/wiki/ISIL" class="mw-redirect" title="ISIL">ISIL</a>, killing four people and injuring over 100. And in 2016, <a href="/wiki/2016_Diyarbak%C4%B1r_bombing" class="mw-redirect" title="2016 Diyarbakır bombing">two separate attacks</a> in February and March, each killing six people.</p>
<p>Between November 8, 2015 and May 15, 2016 large parts of Sur were destroyed in fighting between the Turkish military and the PKK.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diyarbak%C4%B1r&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, Diyarbakır produced <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sesame" title="Sesame">sesame</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Prothero60_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero60-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Prothero62_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero62-41">[41]</a></sup> They would preserve the wheat in <a href="/wiki/Warehouse" title="Warehouse">warehouses</a>, with coverings of <a href="/wiki/Straw" title="Straw">straw</a> and twigs from <a href="/wiki/Licorice" class="mw-redirect" title="Licorice">licorice</a> trees. This system would allow the wheat to be preserved for up to ten years.<sup id="cite_ref-Prothero60_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero60-40">[40]</a></sup> In the late 19th and early 20th century, Diyarbakır exported <a href="/wiki/Raisin" title="Raisin">raisins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almonds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Apricot" title="Apricot">apricots</a> to Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Prothero62_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero62-41">[41]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Angora_goat" title="Angora goat">Angora goats</a> were raised, and wool and <a href="/wiki/Mohair" title="Mohair">mohair</a> was exported from Diyarbakır. Merchants would also come from <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, to purchase goats and <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Prothero63_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero63-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">Honey</a> was also produced, but not so much exported, but used by locals. <a href="/wiki/Sericulture" title="Sericulture">Sericulture</a> was observed in the area, too.<sup id="cite_ref-Prothero64_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero64-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prior to World War I, Diyarbakır had an active <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> industry, with six mines. Three were active, with two being owned by locals and the third being owned by the Turkish government. <a href="/wiki/Tenorite" title="Tenorite">Tenorite</a> was the primary type of copper mined. It was mined by hand by Kurds. A large portion of the ore was exported to England. The region also produced <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lime_(material)" title="Lime (material)">lime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jet_(lignite)" title="Jet (lignite)">jet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>, but primarily for local use.<sup id="cite_ref-Prothero70_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero70-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diyarbak%C4%B1r&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographic_history">Demographic history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diyarbak%C4%B1r&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Demographic history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the turn of the 19th century, the Christian population of the city was mainly made up of Armenians and <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Orthodox_Christians_(Middle_East)" title="Syriac Orthodox Christians (Middle East)">Syriac Orthodox Christians</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-JV20_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JV20-45">[45]</a></sup> Armenians had inhabited the town since the 8th century, while the Syriacs were probably descendants of surviving <a href="/wiki/Arameans" title="Arameans">Arameans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-JV20_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JV20-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diyarbak%C4%B1r&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some jewelry making and other craftwork continues today although the fame of the Diyarbakır's craftsmen has long passed. Folk dancing to the drum and <a href="/wiki/Zurna" title="Zurna">zurna</a> (pipe) are a part of weddings and celebrations in the area.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuisine">Cuisine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diyarbak%C4%B1r&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cuisine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Diyarbakır is known for rich dishes of lamb which use spices such as <a href="/wiki/Black_pepper" title="Black pepper">black pepper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumac" title="Sumac">sumac</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coriander" title="Coriander">coriander</a>; rice, <a href="/wiki/Bulgur" title="Bulgur">bulgur</a> and butter. The most famous specialty dish from Diyarbakır is Meftune which is made up of lamb meat and vegetable laced with garlic and sumac. Another known dish is Kaburga Dolması which is a baked lamb's ribs stuffed with rice and many spices. Diyarbakır is also famous for its watermelons which are exported internationally; one of the largest events in the city is the annually held Watermelon Festival.</p>

<p>Diyarbakır is surrounded by an almost intact, dramatic set of high walls of black basalt forming a 5.5&#160;km (3.4&#160;mi) circle around the old city. There are four gates into the old city and 82 watch-towers on the walls, which were built in antiquity, restored and extended by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_emperor" title="Roman emperor">Roman emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Constantius_II" title="Constantius II">Constantius II</a> in 349.</p>
<p>Medieval <a href="/wiki/Mosques" class="mw-redirect" title="Mosques">mosques</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medrese" class="mw-redirect" title="Medrese">medreses</a> include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Great_Mosque_of_Diyarbak%C4%B1r" title="Great Mosque of Diyarbakır">Great Mosque of Diyarbakır</a> built by the <a href="/wiki/Seljuk_Turk" class="mw-redirect" title="Seljuk Turk">Seljuk Turkish</a> Sultan <a href="/wiki/Malik_Shah_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Malik Shah I">Malik Shah</a> in the 11th century. The mosque, one of the oldest in Turkey, is constructed in alternating bands of black basalt and white limestone (The same patterning is used in the 16th century Deliler Han Madrassah, which is now a hotel). The adjoining <i>Mesudiye Medresesi</i>/<i>Medreseya Mesûdiyeyê</i> was built at the same time, as was another prayer-school in the city, <i>Zinciriye Medresesi</i>/<i>Medreseya Zincîriyeyê</i>.</li>
<li><i>Beharampaşa Camii</i>/<i>Mizgefta Behram Paşa</i> – an <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> mosque built in 1572 by the governor of Diyarbakır, Behram <a href="/wiki/Pasha" title="Pasha">Pasha</a>, noted for the well-constructed arches at the entrance.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sheikh_Matar_Mosque" title="Sheikh Matar Mosque">Sheikh Matar Mosque</a> with <i>Dört Ayaklı Minare</i>/<i>Mizgefta Çarling</i> (<i>the Four-legged Minaret</i>) – built by Kasim Khan of the <a href="/wiki/Aq_Qoyunlu" class="mw-redirect" title="Aq Qoyunlu">Aq Qoyunlu</a>.</li>
<li><i>Fatihpaşa Camii</i>/<i>Mizgefta Fetih Paşa</i> – built in 1520 by Diyarbakır's first <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> governor, Bıyıklı Mehmet Paşa ("the moustachioed Mehmet pasha"). The city's earliest Ottoman building, it is decorated with fine tilework.</li>
<li><i>Hazreti Süleyman Camii</i>/<i>Mizgefta Hezretî Silêman</i> – 1155–1169 – Süleyman son of <a href="/wiki/Khalid_ibn_al-Walid" title="Khalid ibn al-Walid">Halid Bin Velid</a>, who died capturing the city from the Arabs, is buried here along with his companions.</li>
<li><i>Hüsrevpaşa Camii</i>/<i>Mizgefta Husrev Paşa</i> – the mosque of the second Ottoman governor, 1512–1528. Originally the building was intended to be a school (<i>medrese</i>)</li>
<li><i>İskender Paşa Camii</i>/<i>Mizgefta Îskender Paşa</i> – a mosque of an Ottoman governor, an attractive building in black and white stone, built in 1551.</li>
<li><i>Melek Ahmet Camii</i>/<i>Melek Ahmed Paşa</i> a 16th-century mosque noted for its tiled prayer-niche and for the double stairway up the minaret.</li>
<li><i>Nebii Camii</i>/<i>Mizgefta Pêxember</i> – an <a href="/wiki/Aq_Qoyunlu" class="mw-redirect" title="Aq Qoyunlu">Aq Qoyunlu</a> mosque, a single-domed stone construction from the 16th century. <i>Nebi Camii</i> means "the mosque of the prophet" and is so-named because of the number of inscriptions in honour of the prophet on its <a href="/wiki/Minaret" title="Minaret">minaret</a>.</li>
<li><i>Safa Camii</i>/<i>Mizgefta Palo</i> – built in 1532 by the <a href="/wiki/Aq_Qoyunlu" class="mw-redirect" title="Aq Qoyunlu">Aq Qoyunlu (White Sheep Turkomans)</a> tribe.</li>
</ul>

<ul>

<li>The <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Orthodox" class="mw-redirect" title="Syriac Orthodox">Syriac Orthodox</a> <a href="/wiki/St._Mary_Church,_Diyarbak%C4%B1r" title="St. Mary Church, Diyarbakır">Church of Our Lady</a> (<a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a>: <span lang="syr" dir="rtl" xml:lang="syr">ܐ ܕܝܠܕܬ ܐܠܗܐ</span>‎ `<i>Idto d-Yolda<u>t</u> Aloho</i>, <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><i>Meryemana kilisesi</i></span>), was first constructed as a pagan temple in the 1st century BCE. The current construction dates back to the 3rd century, has been restored many times, and is still in use as a place of worship today.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Mar Petyun (St. Anthony) Catholic Chaldean Church, built in 1681.<sup id="cite_ref-romeartlover.tripod.com_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-romeartlover.tripod.com-46">[46]</a></sup></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diyarbak%C4%B1r&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Diyarbakır has a borderline <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">Mediterranean climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a> <i>Csa</i>) and hot dry-summer <a href="/wiki/Continental_climate" title="Continental climate">continental climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a> <i>Dsa</i>). Summer are very hot and very dry, due to its location on the Mesopotamian plain which is subject to hot winds from the deserts of <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> to the south. The highest recorded temperature was 44.8&#160;°C (112.64&#160;°F) on 28 August 1998. Winters are cold and wet and with frosty nights. Snowfall is quite common between the months of December and March, snowing for a week or two. The lowest recorded temperature was −23.4&#160;°C (−10.12&#160;°F) on 30 December 2006.</p>
