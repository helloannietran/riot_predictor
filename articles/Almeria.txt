<p><b>Almería</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <small>Spanish:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[almeˈɾi.a]</a></span>, <small>locally:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[aɾmeˈɾi.a, al-]</a></span>) is a city in <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a>, Spain, situated in the southeast of <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. It is the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Almer%C3%ADa" title="Province of Almería">province</a> of the same name.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponym">Toponym</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almer%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Toponym">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Almería was founded due to the need for a better defense system for the regional Arab towns. It was Abd-al-Rahman III who founded the <a href="/wiki/Kasbah" title="Kasbah">Alcazaba</a> (the Citadel), which gave this city its name: Al-Mari'yah (<b>المريّة</b>, the Watchtower).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almer%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the first century, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> documents report that there was a town named <a href="/wiki/Urci" title="Urci">Urci</a>, possibly near the current site of Almería, in the <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. However, this is disputed, as there are several possible sites of the town. However, one Saint <a href="/wiki/Indaletius" title="Indaletius">Indaletius</a>, a missionary is said to have evangelized Urci and become its first bishop, is officially the <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a> of Almería.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later, the city was refounded by Calipha <a href="/wiki/Abd-ar-Rahman_III" title="Abd-ar-Rahman III">Abd-ar-Rahman III</a> of <a href="/wiki/Caliphate_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Caliphate of Córdoba">Córdoba</a> in 955 AD. It was to be a principal harbour in his extensive domain to strengthen his Mediterranean defences.</p>

<p>Its <a href="/wiki/Moorish" class="mw-redirect" title="Moorish">Moorish</a> castle, the <i><a href="/wiki/Alcazaba_of_Almer%C3%ADa" title="Alcazaba of Almería">Alcazaba of Almería</a></i>, is the second largest among the Muslim fortresses of <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a>, after the <a href="/wiki/Alhambra" title="Alhambra">Alhambra</a>.</p>

<p>In this period, the port city of Almería reached its historical peak. After the fragmentation of the <a href="/wiki/Caliphate_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Caliphate of Córdoba">Caliphate of Córdoba</a> in 1031, Almería continued to be ruled by powerful local Muslim <a href="/wiki/Taifa" title="Taifa">Taifa</a> emirs like <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jairan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jairan (page does not exist)">Jairan</a>, the first independent Emir of <a href="/wiki/Taifa_of_Almer%C3%ADa" title="Taifa of Almería">Almería</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cartagena,_Spain" title="Cartagena, Spain">Cartagena</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Almotacin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Almotacin (page does not exist)">Almotacin</a>, the poet emir. Both Jairan and Almotacin were fearless warriors, but also sophisticated patrons of the arts. A <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a> industry, based upon plantings of <a href="/wiki/Mulberry" class="mw-redirect" title="Mulberry">mulberry</a> trees in the hot, dry landscape of the province, supported Almería in the 11th century and made its strategic harbour an even more valuable asset.</p>

<p>Contested by the emirs of <a href="/wiki/Granada" title="Granada">Granada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Valencia" title="Kingdom of Valencia">Valencia</a>, Almería experienced many sieges, including one especially fierce siege when Christians, called to the <a href="/wiki/Second_Crusade" title="Second Crusade">Second Crusade</a> by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Eugene_III" title="Pope Eugene III">Pope Eugene III</a>, were also encouraged to attack the Muslim 'infidels' on a more familiar coast. On that occasion <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_VII_of_Le%C3%B3n_and_Castile" class="mw-redirect" title="Alfonso VII of León and Castile">Alfonso VII</a>, starting on 11 July 1147, at the head of mixed forces of Catalans, Genoese, Pisans and Franks, led a crusade against the rich city, and Almería was occupied on 17 October 1147.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERogers201036_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERogers201036-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within a decade, however, Almería had passed to the control of the puritanical Muslim <a href="/wiki/Almoravid_dynasty" title="Almoravid dynasty">Almoravid</a> emirs,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERogers201036_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERogers201036-3">[3]</a></sup> and not until the late 15th century did it fall permanently into Christian hands. The city surrendered to the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fernando_II_of_Aragon" class="mw-redirect" title="Fernando II of Aragon">Fernando</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isabel_I_of_Castilla" class="mw-redirect" title="Isabel I of Castilla">Isabel</a>, on December 26, 1489.</p>

<p>The 16th century was for Almería a century of natural and human catastrophes; for there were at least four <a href="/wiki/Earthquakes" class="mw-redirect" title="Earthquakes">earthquakes</a>, of which the one in 1522 was especially violent, devastating the city. The people who had remained Muslim were expelled from Almería after the <a href="/wiki/Alpujarras" title="Alpujarras">War of Las Alpujarras</a> in 1568 and scattered across Spain. Landings and attacks by <a href="/wiki/Berber_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Berber people">Berber</a> <a href="/wiki/Pirate" class="mw-redirect" title="Pirate">pirates</a> were also frequent in the 16th century, and continued until the early 18th century. At that time, huge iron mines were discovered and French and British companies set up business in the area, bringing renewed prosperity and returning Almería to a position of relative importance within Spain.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a> the city was <a href="/wiki/Bombardment_of_Almer%C3%ADa" title="Bombardment of Almería">shelled by the German Navy</a>, with news reaching the London and Parisian press about the "criminal bombardment of Almería by German planes".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Almería surrendered in 1939, being the last Andalusian capital city to fall to <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco_Bahamonde" class="mw-redirect" title="Francisco Franco Bahamonde">Francoist</a> forces.</p>
<p>In the second half of the 20th century, Almería witnessed spectacular economic growth due to tourism and intensive agriculture, with crops grown year-round in massive <i>invernaderos</i> – plastic-covered "greenhouses" – for intensive vegetable production.</p>
<p>After Franco's death and popular approval of the new Spanish Constitution, the people of southern Spain were called on to approve an autonomous status for the region in a referendum. While the referendum were approved with 118,186 votes for and 11,092 votes against in Almería province,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> an absolute majority of all 279,300 registered electors was needed, and the result in Almería was just 42%. The Government impugned the result,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and Almería remained part of the present autonomous region of <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_sights">Main sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almer%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Main sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cable_ingl%C3%A9s_noches.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Cable_ingl%C3%A9s_noches.JPG/220px-Cable_ingl%C3%A9s_noches.JPG" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Cable_ingl%C3%A9s_noches.JPG/330px-Cable_ingl%C3%A9s_noches.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Cable_ingl%C3%A9s_noches.JPG/440px-Cable_ingl%C3%A9s_noches.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2848" data-file-height="2144" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Cable Inglés, at night</div>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alcazaba_of_Almer%C3%ADa" title="Alcazaba of Almería">The Alcazaba</a>, a medieval fortress that was begun in the 10th century but destroyed by an earthquake in 1522. It includes a triple line of walls, a majestic <a href="/wiki/Keep" title="Keep">keep</a> and large gardens. It commands a city quarter with buildings dressed in pastel colors, of Muslim-age aspect.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Almer%C3%ADa_air_raid_shelters" title="Almería air raid shelters">Almería air raid shelters</a>, underground galleries for civilian protection during the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a>, nowadays, the longest in Europe open for tourists.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Almeria" class="mw-redirect" title="Cathedral of Almeria">The Cathedral</a> has a fortress-like appearance due to its towers, merlons and protected paths, created to defend it from Mediterranean pirates. Originally designated as a mosque, it was later converted into a Christian church, before being destroyed in the 1522 earthquake. In the 16th century it was rebuilt in the Renaissance style, whilst keeping some of its defensive features.</li>
<li>Renaissance church of Santiago, built in 1533, with tower and portal decorated with reliefs.</li>
<li><i>Chanca</i>, a group of houses carved into rocks.</li>
<li>Castle of <i>San Cristobal</i>, now in ruins. It is connected to the Alcazaba by a line of walls.</li>
<li>Museum of Almería. Includes findings from Prehistoric, Iberic, Roman, Greek ages and Muslim objects, mostly from the Alcazaba.</li>
<li><i>Paseo de Coches</i>, a modern seaside promenade with gardens and palms.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almer%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="People_and_culture">People and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almer%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: People and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Almeria_Puerta_de_Purchena_fcm.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Almeria_Puerta_de_Purchena_fcm.jpg/220px-Almeria_Puerta_de_Purchena_fcm.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Almeria_Puerta_de_Purchena_fcm.jpg/330px-Almeria_Puerta_de_Purchena_fcm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Almeria_Puerta_de_Purchena_fcm.jpg/440px-Almeria_Puerta_de_Purchena_fcm.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1920" /></a>

<p>Famous natives of Almería include <a href="/wiki/Nicol%C3%A1s_Salmer%C3%B3n_y_Alonso" title="Nicolás Salmerón y Alonso">Nicolás Salmerón y Alonso</a>, who in 1873 was the third president of the <a href="/wiki/First_Spanish_Republic" title="First Spanish Republic">First Spanish Republic</a>, as well as several musicians, including the composer José Padilla Sánchez, whose music was declared of "universal interest" by Unesco in 1989, the popular folk singer <a href="/wiki/Manolo_Escobar" title="Manolo Escobar">Manolo Escobar</a>, renowned Flamenco guitar player <a href="/wiki/Tomatito" title="Tomatito">José Tomás "Tomatito"</a> and Grammy Award winner <a href="/wiki/David_Bisbal" title="David Bisbal">David Bisbal</a>; the champion motorcyclist <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Maeso" title="Antonio Maeso">Antonio Maeso</a> moved to Almería as a child.</p>
<p>The Irish folk-rock group <a href="/wiki/The_Pogues" title="The Pogues">The Pogues</a> paid tribute to Almeria in "Fiesta," a rollicking Spike Jones-flavored song on the band's third album, <i>If I Should Fall From Grace with God</i>.</p>
<p>Although administratively annexed to the Autonomous Community of Andalucia, in Southern Spain, some people of the province<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2013)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> have shown a clear desire for regional autonomy in different referendums. The insular effect produced by its geographical situation has made several customs, accents and history different from the rest of the Autonomous Region of Andalucia.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almer%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Almería hosted the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Games" title="Mediterranean Games">Mediterranean Games</a> in 2005. The city has 2 football teams: <a href="/wiki/UD_Almer%C3%ADa" title="UD Almería">UD Almería</a>, which plays in the Spanish <a href="/wiki/Liga_Adelante" class="mw-redirect" title="Liga Adelante">Segunda División</a> following relegation from <a href="/wiki/La_Liga" title="La Liga">La Liga</a> in 2013 and <a href="/wiki/CP_Almer%C3%ADa" title="CP Almería">CP Almería</a>, in regional division.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almer%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ayunt_Almer%C3%ADa.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Ayunt_Almer%C3%ADa.JPG/220px-Ayunt_Almer%C3%ADa.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Ayunt_Almer%C3%ADa.JPG/330px-Ayunt_Almer%C3%ADa.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Ayunt_Almer%C3%ADa.JPG/440px-Ayunt_Almer%C3%ADa.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>

<p>The economy of Almería is mostly based on agriculture, which is located mainly in the western part of the region. Numerous greenhouses mostly constructed with <a href="/wiki/Plasticulture" title="Plasticulture">plasticulture</a> produce tonnes of fruit and vegetables, more than 70% of their product being exported to the rest of Europe.</p>

<p>By land, Almería can be reached by the <a href="/wiki/Autov%C3%ADa_A-7" title="Autovía A-7">A-7 Mediterranean Highway</a>, which connects the Mediterranean area with the <a href="/wiki/Autov%C3%ADa_A-92" title="Autovía A-92">Spanish A-92</a> that unites it with the rest of Andalusia.</p>
<p>By sea, the port of Almería has connections to Melilla, Algeria and Morocco, and also tourist cruises in the Mediterranean. It also has a marina with moorings for pleasure boats. Currently the port of Almería is being expanded with new docks and transformed into a container port to take large-scale international shipping and thereby increase its freight traffic. It normally connects with the following destinations:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Acciona_Trasmediterr%C3%A1nea" class="mw-redirect" title="Acciona Trasmediterránea">Acciona</a>: Ghazaouet (Algeria), <a href="/wiki/Oran" title="Oran">Oran</a> (Algeria), <a href="/wiki/Nador_Port" title="Nador Port">Nador</a> (<a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Melilla" title="Melilla">Melilla</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Comarit" title="Comarit">Comarit</a>: Nador.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Compagnie_Marocaine_de_Navigation" title="Compagnie Marocaine de Navigation">Comanav</a>: Nador.</li>
</ul>
<p>By air, Almería is served by <a href="/wiki/Almer%C3%ADa_Airport" title="Almería Airport">Almería Airport</a>, the fourth largest in Andalusia<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> with domestic and international connections to <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melilla" title="Melilla">Melilla</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> and Swiss, German and other EU airports.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almer%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to its arid landscape, numerous <a href="/wiki/Spaghetti_western" class="mw-redirect" title="Spaghetti western">spaghetti westerns</a> were filmed in Almería. According to Christopher Frayling, the author of <i>Once Upon a Time in Italy: The Films of <a href="/wiki/Sergio_Leone" title="Sergio Leone">Sergio Leone</a></i>, some of the sets are still there.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> These sets are located in the desert of <a href="/wiki/Tabernas" title="Tabernas">Tabernas</a>. The town and region were also used by <a href="/wiki/David_Lean" title="David Lean">David Lean</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Lawrence_of_Arabia_(film)" title="Lawrence of Arabia (film)">Lawrence of Arabia</a></i> (1962), <a href="/wiki/John_Milius" title="John Milius">John Milius</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wind_and_the_Lion" title="The Wind and the Lion">The Wind and the Lion</a></i> (1975) and others.</p>
<p>One of Almería's most famous natural spots is the <a href="/wiki/Cabo_de_Gata-N%C3%ADjar_Natural_Park" title="Cabo de Gata-Níjar Natural Park">Cabo de Gata-Níjar Natural Park</a>. This park is of volcanic origin, and is the largest and most ecologically significant marine-terrestrial space in the European Western <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. With one of the most beautiful and ecologically rich coasts of the western Mediterranean and an area of 380 square kilometres it is one of Spain’s natural jewels. The Cabo de Gata Natural Park runs through the municipal areas of <a href="/wiki/N%C3%ADjar" title="Níjar">Níjar</a>, Almería and <a href="/wiki/Carboneras" title="Carboneras">Carboneras</a>. Its villages, previously dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a>, have become <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a> spots for those interested in nature. One of the greatest attractions of the Cabo de Gata Natural Park is its beaches.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almer%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Almería city is one of the driest cities in Europe and the Mediterranean coast. According to the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a>, the city receives just enough rainfall to be classified as a <a href="/wiki/Hot_semi-arid_climate" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot semi-arid climate">hot semi-arid climate</a> (<i>BSh</i>), although nearby coastal areas within Almeria province have the only true <a href="/wiki/Hot_desert_climate" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot desert climate">hot desert climate</a> (<i>BWh</i>) in Europe. With an average annual temperature of above 19&#160;°C it also qualifies as one of the warmest cities in continental Europe. The dry season (although there is no real wet season) occurs during the hottest months, as in the Mediterranean climate.</p>
<p>This climatic region spreads along the coastline around Almeria, from approximately Almerimar to the west, to Carboneras to the east. Inland the climate is temperate (mean annual temperature less than 18&#160;°C) arid or semi-arid (for instance in the <a href="/wiki/Tabernas_desert" class="mw-redirect" title="Tabernas desert">Tabernas desert</a> where the temperature is 17.9&#160;°C).</p>
<p>Almeria also experiences the warmest winters of any city on the European continent with a population over 100,000, and hot and dry summers with no precipitation between June and September. Almería enjoys about 3000 hours of sunshine annually with over 320 sunny days a year on average (6 hours of sunshine in January and 12 in July). The city has an average of only 26 days with precipitation annually.</p>
<p>Almería is unique, for a city in <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">Continental Europe</a>, for not having any registered temperature under the freezing mark in its recorded weather history. The coldest temperature recorded was 0.2&#160;°C (32.4&#160;°F) on 9 February 1935.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the winter, daily maximum temperatures tend to stay around 18&#160;°C (64&#160;°F). At night, the temperature very rarely drops below 8&#160;°C (46&#160;°F). Precipitation even during the wettest months is rare, this falls in short showers or thunderstorms. Inland areas of the Almería province have reached 50&#160;°C (122&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the warmest months - July and August, the sky is clear and sunny and no rainfall occurs. The typical daily temperatures are around 33&#160;°C (91&#160;°F) during the heat of the day. This is often influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Levant_(wind)" title="Levant (wind)">Levant wind</a>, a hot dry easterly wind that blows from the interior desert that makes temperatures soar to 38&#160;°C (100&#160;°F) or higher. These can also carry dust or sand. The minimum temperatures stay around 24&#160;°C (75&#160;°F) during July and August. Heatwaves in Almería are quite common; Almería reached up to 43&#160;°C (109&#160;°F) in August 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crystal_Cave">Crystal Cave</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almer%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Crystal Cave">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2000, a team of geologists found a cave filled with giant <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> crystals in an abandoned silver mine near Almería. The cavity, which measures 1.8x1.7 metres, would be the largest <a href="/wiki/Geode" title="Geode">geode</a> ever found. The entrance of the cave has been blocked by five tons of rocks, and is under police protection (to prevent looters from entering). According to geological models, the cave was formed during the <a href="/wiki/Messinian_salinity_crisis" title="Messinian salinity crisis">Messinian salinity crisis</a> 6 million years ago, when the Mediterranean sea evaporated and left thick layers of salt sediments (<a href="/wiki/Evaporite" title="Evaporite">evaporites</a>). The cave is currently not accessible to tourists.</p>
