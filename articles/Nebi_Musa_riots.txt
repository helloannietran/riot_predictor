<p>The <b>1920 Nebi Musa riots</b> or <b>1920 Jerusalem riots</b> took place in British-controlled part of <a href="/wiki/Occupied_Enemy_Territory_Administration" title="Occupied Enemy Territory Administration">OETA</a> (which would shortly become Mandatory Palestine) between Sunday, 4 and Wednesday, 7 April 1920 in and around the <a href="/wiki/Old_City_(Jerusalem)" title="Old City (Jerusalem)">Old City of Jerusalem</a>. Five Jews and four Arabs were killed, and several hundred were injured.<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The riots coincided with and are named after the <a href="/wiki/Nabi_Musa" title="Nabi Musa">Nabi Musa</a> festival, which took place every year on <a href="/wiki/Easter_Sunday" class="mw-redirect" title="Easter Sunday">Easter Sunday</a>, and followed rising tensions in Arab-Jewish relations. The events came shortly after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tel_Hai" title="Battle of Tel Hai">Battle of Tel Hai</a> and the increasing pressure on Arab nationalists in Syria in the course of the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Syrian_War" title="Franco-Syrian War">Franco-Syrian War</a>. Speeches were given by Arab religious leaders during the festival (in which large numbers of Muslims traditionally gathered for a religious procession), which included slogans referencing <a href="/wiki/Zionism" title="Zionism">Zionist</a> immigration and previous confrontations around outlying Jewish villages in the <a href="/wiki/Galilee" title="Galilee">Galilee</a>. The trigger which turned the procession into a riot is not known with certainty - some evidence exists suggesting Jewish provocation, but it is also possible, though unreported, that Arab activities triggered the riots.<sup id="cite_ref-FreitagFuccaro2015_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FreitagFuccaro2015-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The British <a href="/wiki/Occupied_Enemy_Territory_Administration" title="Occupied Enemy Territory Administration">military administration</a> of Palestine <a href="/wiki/Palin_Report_1920" class="mw-redirect" title="Palin Report 1920">was criticized</a> for withdrawing troops from inside Jerusalem and because it was slow to regain control.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> As a result of the riots, trust between the British, Jews, and Arabs eroded. One consequence was that the <a href="/wiki/Yishuv" title="Yishuv">Jewish community</a> increased moves towards an autonomous infrastructure and security apparatus parallel to that of the British administration.</p>
<p>In its wake, <a href="/wiki/Sheikh" title="Sheikh">sheikhs</a> of 82 villages round the city and Jaffa, claiming to represent 70% of the population, issued a document protesting the demonstrations against the Jews. This condemnation may have been procured with bribes.<sup id="cite_ref-TSegev_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TSegev-4">[4]</a></sup> Notwithstanding the riots, the <a href="/wiki/Yishuv" title="Yishuv">Palestinian Jewish community</a> <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_Representatives_election,_1920" title="Assembly of Representatives election, 1920">held elections</a> for the Assembly of Representatives on 19 April 1920 among Jews everywhere in Palestine except Jerusalem, where they were delayed to 3 May.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The riots also preceded the <a href="/wiki/San_Remo_conference" title="San Remo conference">San Remo conference</a> which was held from 19 to 26 April 1920 at which the fate of the Middle East was to be decided.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1920_Nebi_Musa_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The contents and proposals of both the <a href="/wiki/Balfour_Declaration" title="Balfour Declaration">Balfour Declaration</a> of 1917 and <a href="/wiki/Paris_Peace_Conference,_1919" title="Paris Peace Conference, 1919">Paris Peace Conference, 1919</a>, which later concluded with the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles" title="Treaty of Versailles">Treaty of Versailles</a>, were the subject of intensive discussion by both Zionist and Arab delegations, and the process of the negotiations were widely reported in both communities. In particular, the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, led to an undertaking by the victorious powers, predominantly Great Britain and France, to assume a 'holy mission of civilization' in the power vacuum of the Middle East. Under the Balfour Declaration, a homeland for the Jewish people was to be created in Palestine. The principle of <a href="/wiki/Self-determination" title="Self-determination">self-determination</a> affirmed by the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a> was not to be applied to Palestine, given the foreseeable rejection by the people of Zionism, which the British sponsored. These post-WW1 arrangements both for Palestine and other Arab societies led to a 'radicalization' of the Arab world.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 1 March 1920, the death of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Trumpeldor" title="Joseph Trumpeldor">Joseph Trumpeldor</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tel_Hai" title="Battle of Tel Hai">Battle of Tel Hai</a> at the hands of a <a href="/wiki/Shiite" class="mw-redirect" title="Shiite">Shiite</a> group from Southern Lebanon, caused deep concern among Jewish leaders, who made numerous requests to the OETA administration to address the <a href="/wiki/Yishuv" title="Yishuv">Yishuv</a>'s security and forbid a pro-Syrian public rally. However, their fears were largely discounted by the Chief Administrative Officer General <a href="/wiki/Louis_Bols" title="Louis Bols">Louis Bols</a>, Military Governor <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Storrs" title="Ronald Storrs">Ronald Storrs</a> and General <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Allenby" class="mw-redirect" title="Edmund Allenby">Edmund Allenby</a>, despite a warning from the head of the <a href="/wiki/Zionist_Commission" title="Zionist Commission">Zionist Commission</a> <a href="/wiki/Chaim_Weizmann" title="Chaim Weizmann">Chaim Weizmann</a> that a "<a href="/wiki/Pogrom" title="Pogrom">pogrom</a> is in the air", supported by assessments available to Storrs.<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup> Communiqués had been issued about foreseeable troubles between Arabs, and Arabs and Jews. To Weizmann and the Jewish leadership, these developments were reminiscent of instructions that <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> generals had issued on the eve of pogroms.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In the meantime, local Arab expectations had been raised to a pitch by the declaration of the Syrian Congress on 7 March of the independence of <a href="/wiki/Greater_Syria" title="Greater Syria">Greater Syria</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Syria" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Syria">Kingdom of Syria</a>, with Faisal as its king,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> that included the British-controlled territory within its claimed domain. On 7 and 8 March, demonstrations took place in all cities of Palestine, shops were closed and many Jews were attacked. Attackers carried slogans such as "Death to Jews" or "Palestine is our land and the Jews are our dogs!"<sup id="cite_ref-Morris_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morris-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jewish leaders requested that OETA authorise the arming of the Jewish defenders to make up for the lack of adequate British troops. Although this request was declined, <a href="/wiki/Ze%27ev_Jabotinsky" title="Ze'ev Jabotinsky">Ze'ev Jabotinsky</a>, together with <a href="/wiki/Pinhas_Rutenberg" title="Pinhas Rutenberg">Pinhas Rutenberg</a>, led an effort to openly train Jewish volunteers in self-defense, an effort of which the Zionist Commission kept the British informed. Many of the volunteers were members of the <a href="/wiki/Maccabi_World_Union" title="Maccabi World Union">Maccabi sports club</a> and some of them were veterans of the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_Legion" title="Jewish Legion">Jewish Legion</a>. Their month of training largely consisted of <a href="/wiki/Calisthenics" title="Calisthenics">calisthenics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hand_to_hand_combat" class="mw-redirect" title="Hand to hand combat">hand to hand combat</a> with sticks.<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup> By the end of March, about 600 were said to be performing military drills daily in Jerusalem.<sup id="cite_ref-wasserstein63_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wasserstein63-10">[10]</a></sup> Jabotinsky and Rutenberg also began organizing the collection of arms.<sup id="cite_ref-wasserstein63_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wasserstein63-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nebi_Musa" class="mw-redirect" title="Nebi Musa">Nebi Musa</a> festival was an annual spring Muslim festival that began on the Friday before <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday" title="Good Friday">Good Friday</a> and included a procession to the Nebi Musa shrine (tomb of Moses) near <a href="/wiki/Jericho" title="Jericho">Jericho</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Canaan_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canaan-11">[11]</a></sup> It had apparently existed since the time of <a href="/wiki/Saladin" title="Saladin">Saladin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Arab educator and essayist <a href="/wiki/Khalil_al-Sakakini" title="Khalil al-Sakakini">Khalil al-Sakakini</a> described how tribes and caravans would come with banners and weapons.<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> Turks usually deployed thousands of soldiers and even artillery to keep order in the narrow streets of Jerusalem during the Nebi Musa procession. However, Storrs issued a warning to Arab leaders, but deployed only 188 policemen.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="4.E2.80.937_April_1920.2C_Old_City">4–7 April 1920, Old City</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1920_Nebi_Musa_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 4–7 April 1920, Old City">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nebi_Musa_riots,_The_Times,_Thursday,_Apr_08,_1920.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Nebi_Musa_riots%2C_The_Times%2C_Thursday%2C_Apr_08%2C_1920.png/220px-Nebi_Musa_riots%2C_The_Times%2C_Thursday%2C_Apr_08%2C_1920.png" width="220" height="410" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a1/Nebi_Musa_riots%2C_The_Times%2C_Thursday%2C_Apr_08%2C_1920.png 1.5x" data-file-width="268" data-file-height="500" /></a>

<p>By 10:30&#160;a.m. on Sunday, 4 April 1920, 60,000–70,000 Arabs had congregated in the city square for the Nebi Musa festival, and groups had been attacking Jews in the Old City's alleys for over an hour. Inflammatory <a href="/wiki/Anti-Zionism" title="Anti-Zionism">anti-Zionist</a> rhetoric was delivered by <a href="/wiki/Amin_al-Husayni" class="mw-redirect" title="Amin al-Husayni">Amin al-Husayni</a> from the balcony of the Arab Club. Another inciter was <a href="/wiki/Musa_al-Husayni" title="Musa al-Husayni">Musa al-Husayni</a>, his uncle, the mayor, who spoke from the municipal building's balcony.</p>
<p>The editor of the newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Southern_Syria_(newspaper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Southern Syria (newspaper)">Suriya al-Janubia</a></i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Southern_Syria" title="Southern Syria">Southern Syria</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Aref_al-Aref" title="Aref al-Aref">Aref al-Aref</a>, another Arab Club member, delivered his speech on horseback at the <a href="/wiki/Jaffa_Gate" title="Jaffa Gate">Jaffa Gate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wasserstein_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wasserstein-13">[13]</a></sup> The nature of his speech is disputed. According to <a href="/wiki/Benny_Morris" title="Benny Morris">Benny Morris</a>, he said "If we don't use force against the Zionists and against the Jews, we will never be rid of them",<sup id="cite_ref-Morris_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morris-9">[9]</a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Wasserstein" title="Bernard Wasserstein">Bernard Wasserstein</a> wrote "he seems to have co-operated with the police, and there is no evidence that he actively instigated violence".<sup id="cite_ref-Wasserstein_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wasserstein-13">[13]</a></sup> In fact, Wasserstein adds, "Zionist intelligence reports of this period are unanimous in stressing that he spoke repeatedly against violence".<sup id="cite_ref-Wasserstein_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wasserstein-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The crowd reportedly shouted "Independence! Independence!" and "Palestine is our land, the Jews are our dogs!"<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup> Arab police joined in applause, and violence started.<sup id="cite_ref-Sachar123_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sachar123-14">[14]</a></sup> The local Arab population ransacked the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_Quarter_(Jerusalem)" title="Jewish Quarter (Jerusalem)">Jewish Quarter</a> of Jerusalem. The <a href="/wiki/Ateret_Cohanim#Torath_Chaim_Yeshiva" title="Ateret Cohanim">Torath Chaim Yeshiva</a> was raided, and <a href="/wiki/Torah_scroll" class="mw-redirect" title="Torah scroll">Torah scrolls</a> were torn and thrown on the floor, and the building then set alight.<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup> During the next three hours, 160 Jews were injured.<sup id="cite_ref-Sachar123_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sachar123-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Khalil al-Sakakini witnessed the eruption of violence in the Old City:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"[A] riot broke out, the people began to run about and stones were thrown at the Jews. The shops were closed and there were screams. … I saw a Zionist soldier covered in dust and blood. … Afterwards, I saw one Hebronite approach a Jewish shoeshine boy, who hid behind a sack in one of the wall's comers next to Jaffa Gate, and take his box and beat him over the head. He screamed and began to run, his head bleeding and the Hebronite left him and returned to the procession. … The riot reached its zenith. All shouted, "<a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>'s religion was born with the sword". … I immediately walked to the municipal garden. … my soul is nauseated and depressed by the madness of humankind."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>The army imposed night <a href="/wiki/Curfew" title="Curfew">curfew</a> on Sunday night and arrested several dozen rioters, but on Monday morning they were allowed to attend morning prayers and were then released. Arabs continued to attack Jews and break into their homes, especially in Arab-majority mixed buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>On Monday, as disturbances grew worse, the Old City was sealed off by the army and no one was allowed to exit the area. <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">Martial law</a> was declared, but <a href="/wiki/Looting" title="Looting">looting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burglary" title="Burglary">burglary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">rape</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a> continued. Several homes were set on fire, and tombstones were shattered. British soldiers found that the majority of illicit weapons were concealed on the bodies of Arab women.<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup> On Monday evening, the soldiers were evacuated from the Old City, a step described in the Palin Report as "an error of judgment". Even with martial law, it took the British authorities another 4 days to restore order.</p>
<p>The Old City's Jewish community had no training or weapons, and Jabotinsky's men had found themselves outside the walled Old City, and shut out by British soldiers.<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup> Two volunteers were able to enter the Jewish Quarter disguised as medical personnel to organize self-defense – using rocks and boiling water.<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Five Jews and four Arabs died in the riots. Two-hundred and sixteen Jews were injured, 18 critically, and 23 Arabs, one critically. About 300 Jews were evacuated from the Old City.<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accusations_of_British_complicity">Accusations of British complicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1920_Nebi_Musa_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Accusations of British complicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Development_Of_Palestine._Statement_By_Mr._Herbert_Samuel._The_Times,_Monday,_Apr_05,_1920.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Development_Of_Palestine._Statement_By_Mr._Herbert_Samuel._The_Times%2C_Monday%2C_Apr_05%2C_1920.png/220px-Development_Of_Palestine._Statement_By_Mr._Herbert_Samuel._The_Times%2C_Monday%2C_Apr_05%2C_1920.png" width="220" height="332" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2d/Development_Of_Palestine._Statement_By_Mr._Herbert_Samuel._The_Times%2C_Monday%2C_Apr_05%2C_1920.png 1.5x" data-file-width="277" data-file-height="418" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Development_Of_Palestine._Statement_By_Mr._Herbert_Samuel._The_Times,_Monday,_Apr_05,_1920.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Reaction of the soon-to-appointed <a href="/wiki/High_Commissioner_for_Palestine" class="mw-redirect" title="High Commissioner for Palestine">High Commissioner for Palestine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Samuel,_1st_Viscount_Samuel" title="Herbert Samuel, 1st Viscount Samuel">Herbert Samuel</a>, on a visit to Palestine a few days prior to the riots. Published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It was asserted soon after, by <a href="/wiki/Chaim_Weizmann" title="Chaim Weizmann">Chaim Weizmann</a> and British army <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Colonel" class="mw-redirect" title="Lieutenant Colonel">Lieutenant Colonel</a> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Meinertzhagen" title="Richard Meinertzhagen">Richard Meinertzhagen</a>, that <a href="/wiki/Haj_Amin_al-Husseini" class="mw-redirect" title="Haj Amin al-Husseini">Haj Amin al-Husseini</a> had been put up to inciting the riot by British <a href="/wiki/Field-marshal" class="mw-redirect" title="Field-marshal">Field-marshal</a> <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Allenby,_1st_Viscount_Allenby" title="Edmund Allenby, 1st Viscount Allenby">Allenby</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Chief_of_Staff" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief of Staff">Chief of Staff</a>, Colonel Bertie Harry Waters-Taylor, to demonstrate to the world that Arabs would not tolerate a Jewish homeland in Palestine.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The assertion was never proven, and Meinertzhagen was dismissed.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Zionist Commission noted that before the riots Arab milkmen started to demand their customers in <a href="/wiki/Meah_Shearim" class="mw-redirect" title="Meah Shearim">Meah Shearim</a> pay them on the spot, explaining that they would no longer be serving the Jewish neighbourhood. Christian storekeepers had marked their shops in advance with the sign of the cross so that they would not be mistakenly looted. A previous commission report also accused Storrs of inciting the Arabs, blaming him for sabotaging attempts to purchase the <a href="/wiki/Western_Wall" title="Western Wall">Western Wall</a> as well. A petition circulated among American citizens and presented to their consul protested that the British had prevented Jews from defending themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the violence broke out, <a href="/wiki/Ze%27ev_Jabotinsky" title="Ze'ev Jabotinsky">Ze'ev Jabotinsky</a> met Military Governor Storrs and suggested deployment of his volunteers, but his request was rejected. Storrs confiscated his pistol and demanded to know the location of his other weapons, threatening to arrest him for possessing a firearm. Later, Storrs changed his mind and asked for 200 volunteers to report to the police headquarters to be sworn in as deputies. After they arrived and the administering of the oath had begun, orders came to cease and he sent them away. Arab volunteers had also been invited, and were likewise sent away.</p>
<p>On Sunday night, the first day of the riots, several dozen rioters were arrested, but on Monday morning they were allowed to attend morning prayers and were then released.<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup> On Monday evening, after <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">martial law</a> was declared, the soldiers were evacuated from the Old City, a step described in the Palin Report as "an error of judgment".</p>
<p>After the riots, Storrs visited <a href="/wiki/Menachem_Ussishkin" title="Menachem Ussishkin">Menachem Ussishkin</a>, the new head of the Zionist Commission, to express "regrets for the tragedy that has befallen us".</p>
<p>- Ussishkin asked, "What tragedy?"</p>
<p>- "I mean the unfortunate events that have occurred here in the recent days", Storrs said.</p>
<p>- "His excellency means the pogrom", suggested Ussishkin.</p>
<p>When Storrs hesitated to categorize the events as such, Ussishkin replied,</p>
<p>- "You Colonel, are an expert on matters of management and I am an expert on the rules of pogroms."<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Palin_Report_1920" class="mw-redirect" title="Palin Report 1920">The Palin Report</a> noted that Jewish representatives persisted in describing the events as a "pogrom", implying that the British administration had connived in the violence.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Palin_Commission_of_Inquiry">Palin Commission of Inquiry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1920_Nebi_Musa_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Palin Commission of Inquiry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Palin_Report_1920" class="mw-redirect" title="Palin Report 1920">Palin Commission</a> (or Palin Court of Inquiry), a committee of inquiry sent to the region in May 1920 by the British authorities, examined the reasons for this trouble. According to the <a href="/wiki/Survey_of_Palestine" title="Survey of Palestine">Survey of Palestine</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Savage attacks were made by Arab rioters in Jerusalem on Jewish lives and property. Five Jews were killed and 211 injured. Order was restored by the intervention of British troops; four Arabs were killed and 21 injured. It was reported by a military commission of inquiry that the reasons for this trouble were:--</p>
<dl>
<dd>(a) Arab disappointment at the non-fulfilment of the promises of independence which they claimed had been given to them during the war.</dd>
<dd>(b) Arab belief that the Balfour Declaration implied a denial of the right of self-determination and their fear that the establishment of a National Home would mean a great increase in Jewish immigration and would lead to their economic and political subjection to the Jews.</dd>
<dd>(c) The aggravation of these sentiments on the one hand by propaganda from outside Palestine associated with the proclamation of the Emir Feisal as King of a re-united Syria and with the growth of Pan-Arab and Pan-Moslem ideas, and on the other hand by the activities of the Zionist Commission supported by the resources and influence of Jews throughout the world.</dd>
</dl>
</blockquote>
<p>The Palin Report on the April riots was not signed until July 1920, after the <a href="/wiki/San_Remo_conference" title="San Remo conference">San Remo conference</a> and replacement of the British OETA by a <a href="/wiki/High_Commissioner" title="High Commissioner">High Commissioner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Samuel,_1st_Viscount_Samuel" title="Herbert Samuel, 1st Viscount Samuel">Sir Herbert Samuel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup> The Report was submitted in August 1920, though never published, and was critical of both sides.</p>
<p>The report blamed the Zionists, 'whose impatience to achieve their ultimate goal and indiscretion are largely responsible for this unhappy state of feeling’<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and singled out <a href="/wiki/Amin_al-Husayni" class="mw-redirect" title="Amin al-Husayni">Amin al-Husayni</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ze%27ev_Jabotinsky" title="Ze'ev Jabotinsky">Ze'ev Jabotinsky</a> in particular. The latter, however, was not, as the Court believed, an exponent of '<a href="/wiki/Bolshevism" class="mw-redirect" title="Bolshevism">Bolshevism</a>', which it thought 'flowed in Zionism's inner heart', but rather fiercely anti-<a href="/wiki/Socialist" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialist">Socialist</a>. They had confused his politics with that of the Socialist-aligned <a href="/wiki/Poalei_Zion" class="mw-redirect" title="Poalei Zion">Poalei Zion</a> ('Zionist Workers') party, which it called 'a definite Bolshevist institution.'</p>
<p>The report was critical of some of the actions of OETA military command, particularly the withdrawal of troops from inside Jerusalem early on the morning of Monday, 5 April and that, once martial law had been proclaimed, it was slow to regain control.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1920_Nebi_Musa_riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mousa-Qasem.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Mousa-Qasem.jpg/220px-Mousa-Qasem.jpg" width="220" height="278" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Mousa-Qasem.jpg/330px-Mousa-Qasem.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a9/Mousa-Qasem.jpg 2x" data-file-width="395" data-file-height="500" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mousa-Qasem.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Musa_al-Husayni" title="Musa al-Husayni">Musa Kazim al-Husayni</a>, the mayor of Jerusalem, was dismissed by the British after the April riots</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Over 200 people were put on trial as a result of the riots, including 39 Jews.<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Musa_al-Husayni" title="Musa al-Husayni">Musa Kazim al-Husayni</a> was replaced as mayor by the head of the rival Nashashibi clan, Ragheb Bey Nashashibi.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Amin_al-Husayni" class="mw-redirect" title="Amin al-Husayni">Amin al-Husayni</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aref_al-Aref" title="Aref al-Aref">Aref al-Aref</a> were arrested for incitement, but when they were let out on bail they both escaped to Syria.<sup id="cite_ref-Wasserstein_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wasserstein-13">[13]</a></sup> In another version, al-Aref was warned and escaped before being arrested.<sup id="cite_ref-Tauber_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tauber-24">[24]</a></sup> In their absence, a military court sentenced them to 10 years imprisonment.<sup id="cite_ref-Wasserstein_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wasserstein-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Arab riots were publicly protested by sheikhs from 82 villages in the Jerusalem and Jaffa areas who issued a formal statement saying that, in their view, Zionist settlement was not a danger to their communities. Similar declarations would be repeated in cablegrams sent to London in 1922, as hundreds of sheikhs and muhktars lent their authority and support to Jewish immigration. The tenor of these positions was that such immigration would, as the Zionist movement itself affirmed, improve the lives of Arabs as industrial development progressed. The sheikhs protesting the riots, and telegramming later the British colonial secretary to express solidarity with the Zionist programme were sometimes bribed to state this position by the World Zionist Organisation. Their opinions were procured.<sup id="cite_ref-TSegev_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TSegev-4">[4]</a></sup> British soldiers were sent to search Jews for arms at the demand of the Palestinian Arab leadership. They searched the offices and apartments of <a href="/wiki/Chaim_Weizmann" title="Chaim Weizmann">Chaim Weizmann</a>, the head of the <a href="/wiki/Zionist_Commission" title="Zionist Commission">Zionist Commission</a>, and Jabotinsky. At Jabotinsky's house, they found three rifles, two pistols, and 250 rounds of ammunition. Nineteen men were arrested, but not Jabotinsky, who went to the jail of his own volition to insist on his arrest. A military judge released him because he had not been home when the guns were discovered, but he was again arrested a few hours later.<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup> Jabotinsky was convicted of possessing the pistol that Storrs had confiscated on the riot's first day, among other things. The primary witness was none other than Ronald Storrs, who said he "did not remember" being told about the self-defence organization. He was sentenced to 15 years' imprisonment and sent to <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, though the next day he was returned to <a href="/wiki/Acre_Prison" title="Acre Prison">Acre Prison</a>. Jabotinsky's trial and sentencing created an uproar, and were protested by <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> press including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> and questioned in the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">British Parliament</a>. Even before the editorials appeared, the commander of British forces in Palestine and Egypt, General Congreve, wrote Field Marshal Wilson that Jews were sentenced far more severely than Arabs who had committed worse offences. He reduced Jabotinsky's sentence to a year, and that of the other 19 Jews arrested with him to six months.<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The new civilian government under <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Samuel,_1st_Viscount_Samuel" title="Herbert Samuel, 1st Viscount Samuel">Herbert Samuel</a> granted a general <a href="/wiki/Amnesty" title="Amnesty">amnesty</a> in early 1921. However, Amin al-Husayni and Aref al-Aref were excluded from the amnesty because they had fled before their convictions had been passed down. Samuel pardoned Amin in March 1921 and appointed him Mufti of Jerusalem. When the Supreme Muslim Council was created in the following year, Husseini demanded and received the title Grand Mufti,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> a position which came with life tenure.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Also, General Storr became the civil governor of Jerusalem under the new administration.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the riots began, Jewish immigration to Palestine was temporarily halted by the British. Also, feeling that the British were unwilling to defend Jewish settlements from continuous Arab attacks, Palestinian Jews set up self-defense units, which came to be called the <a href="/wiki/Haganah" title="Haganah">Haganah</a> ("defense"). Furthermore, the riots prompted the Arab leadership in Palestine to view themselves less as southern Syrian Arabs and more as a unique Palestinian Arab community.<sup id="cite_ref-segev_1-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-segev-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
