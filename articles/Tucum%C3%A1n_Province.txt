<p><b>Tucumán</b> (<small>Spanish pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[tukuˈman]</a></span>) is the most densely populated, and the smallest by land area, of the <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Argentina" title="Provinces of Argentina">provinces</a> of <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>. Located in the northwest of the country, the province has the capital of <a href="/wiki/San_Miguel_de_Tucum%C3%A1n" title="San Miguel de Tucumán">San Miguel de Tucumán</a>, often shortened to Tucumán. Neighboring provinces are, clockwise from the north: <a href="/wiki/Salta_Province" title="Salta Province">Salta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santiago_del_Estero_Province" title="Santiago del Estero Province">Santiago del Estero</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catamarca_Province" title="Catamarca Province">Catamarca</a>. It is nicknamed <b>El Jardín de la República</b> (<i>The Garden of the Republic</i>), as it is a highly productive agricultural area.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tucum%C3%A1n_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>Tucumán</i> probably originated from the <a href="/wiki/Quechua_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Quechua languages">Quechua languages</a>. It may represent a deformation of the term <i>Yucumán</i>, which denotes the "place of origin of several rivers". It can also be a deformation of the word <i>Tucma</i>, which means "the end of things". Before Spanish colonization, the region lay in the outer limits of the <a href="/wiki/Inca_empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Inca empire">Inca empire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>Before the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">Spanish colonization</a>, this land was inhabited by the <a href="/wiki/Diaguita" title="Diaguita">Diaguitas</a>, who practiced agriculture.</p>

<p>In 1533 <a href="/wiki/Diego_de_Almagro" title="Diego de Almagro">Diego de Almagro</a> explored the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Northwest" title="Argentine Northwest">Argentine Northwest</a>, including Tucumán.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1549 the Peruvian governor <a href="/wiki/Pedro_de_la_Gasca" title="Pedro de la Gasca">Pedro de la Gasca</a> granted <a href="/wiki/Juan_N%C3%BA%C3%B1ez_de_Prado_(conquistador)" title="Juan Núñez de Prado (conquistador)">Juan Núñez de Prado</a> the territory of Tucumán. Prado established the first Spanish settlement at the town of Barco on the <a href="/wiki/Dulce_River_(Argentina)" title="Dulce River (Argentina)">Dulce River</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Moses1966_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moses1966-3">[3]</a></sup> Prado named his province "Tucumán" after Tucumamahao, one of the leaders of the local people who formed an alliance with him.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1552 <a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Aguirre_(conquistador)" title="Francisco de Aguirre (conquistador)">Francisco de Aguirre</a> was dispatched to take possession of the territory for Chile. Aguirre followed a repressive policy, triggering a rebellion by the native people. Outnumbered, the colonists were forced to move in 1553 to a new location, where they founded the town of <a href="/wiki/Santiago_del_Estero" title="Santiago del Estero">Santiago del Estero</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Moses1966_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moses1966-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1565 Diego de Villaroel founded <i>San Miguel de Tucumán</i> and the <i>Provincia de Tucumán, Juríes y Diaguitas</i> was organized. Because of frequent attacks by the indigenous peoples, the <a href="/wiki/Mal%C3%B3n" title="Malón">Malones</a>, in 1685 San Miguel de Tucumán was moved by Miguel de Salas some 65 kilometres from its first location, where it was redeveloped. The regional aborigines of the region presented a strong resistance to the Spanish, who decided to move the defeated tribes toward <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>. The most noted of these relocations was the case of the <a href="/wiki/Quilmes_(tribe)" class="mw-redirect" title="Quilmes (tribe)">Quilmes</a>, who were moved to the city of <a href="/wiki/Quilmes,_Buenos_Aires" class="mw-redirect" title="Quilmes, Buenos Aires">Quilmes</a>.</p>
<p>Tucumán was a midpoint for shipments of gold and silver from the <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_Peru" title="Viceroyalty of Peru">Viceroyalty of Peru</a> to <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>. It produced cattle, textiles, and wood products that provided supplies for the convoys on their way to Buenos Aires. Because of its important geographical position, and as head of the civil and Catholic governments it acquired special importance during the 18th century.</p>
<p>The creation of the <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_the_R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Viceroyalty of the Río de la Plata">Viceroyalty of the Río de la Plata</a> in 1776 meant the end of the convoys from Perú to Buenos Aires. Tucumán, with 20,000 inhabitants by that time, suffered also from the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> imports from the newly opened customs of Buenos Aires, no longer under the monopoly of the Spanish Crown.</p>
<p>In 1783 the Intendency of Tucumán was divided; Tucumán was set under the control of the Intendency of Salta del Tucumán, with its centre in <a href="/wiki/Salta" title="Salta">Salta</a>. <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_de_San_Mart%C3%ADn" title="José de San Martín">José de San Martín</a> arrived in Tucumán in 1813 and installed the Military School. In 1814 the Intendency of Salta was divided into the present provinces.</p>
<p>On July 9, 1816, at the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Tucum%C3%A1n" title="Congress of Tucumán">Congress of Tucumán</a>, the <i>Provincias Unidas del Río de la Plata</i> ("United Provinces of the Río de la Plata") declared their independence from <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>. Internal conflicts delayed the final fusion of the provinces into the <i>República Argentina</i>.</p>
<p>Following the failure of Argentina's first independence-era government, the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Director_of_the_United_Provinces_of_the_R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Supreme Director of the United Provinces of the Río de la Plata">Directorio</a>, Governor <a href="/wiki/Bernab%C3%A9_Ar%C3%A1oz" title="Bernabé Aráoz">Bernabé Aráoz</a> on March 22, 1820, proclaimed the creation of the "<a href="/wiki/Federal_Republic_of_Tucum%C3%A1n" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Republic of Tucumán">Federal Republic of Tucumán</a>." The experiment collapsed, however, when the neighboring provinces of <a href="/wiki/Catamarca_Province" title="Catamarca Province">Catamarca</a> and <a href="/wiki/Santiago_del_Estero_Province" title="Santiago del Estero Province">Santiago del Estero</a> withdrew the following year.</p>
<p>The beginning of the 20th century, with the customs restrictions and the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport_in_Argentina" title="Rail transport in Argentina">railway</a>, brought prosperous economic times for the province and its <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a> production. Numerous landmarks were built, such as <a href="/wiki/Ninth_of_July_Park" title="Ninth of July Park">Ninth of July Park</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tucum%C3%A1n_Government_Palace" title="Tucumán Government Palace">Tucumán Government Palace</a>, and a daily newspaper founded in 1912, <i><a href="/wiki/La_Gaceta_(Tucum%C3%A1n)" title="La Gaceta (Tucumán)">La Gaceta</a></i>, became the most circulated Argentine daily outside <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>; but, the sugar price crisis of the 1960s and President <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_Ongan%C3%ADa" title="Juan Carlos Onganía">Juan Carlos Onganía</a>'s order to have eleven large state-owned sugar mills closed in 1966, hit Tucumán's economy hard, and ushered in an era of instability for the province.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1975, President <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Per%C3%B3n" class="mw-redirect" title="Isabel Perón">Isabel Perón</a> declared a state of emergency in the province. The decree led to <a href="/wiki/Operativo_Independencia" title="Operativo Independencia">Operation Independence</a>, an official military campaign at least as brutal on local magistrates, lawmakers and faculty as it was on its stated target, the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Revolutionary_Army_(Argentina)" title="People's Revolutionary Army (Argentina)">ERP</a>. Violence did not fully abate until the appointment of General <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Domingo_Bussi" title="Antonio Domingo Bussi">Antonio Domingo Bussi</a>, the operation's commander, as governor at the behest of the <a href="/wiki/National_Reorganization_Process" title="National Reorganization Process">dictatorship</a> that deposed Perón in 1976. Efficient as well as ruthless, Bussi oversaw the completion of several stalled public works; but, also presided over some of the worst human rights abuses during that painful 1976-77 period.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Retaining a sizable following, Bussi was elected governor in his own right in 1995, but lost much of his earlier popularity during his four-year tenure.</p>
<p>Life in Tucumán has since returned to a certain normality. Its economy has recovered strongly during the expansive period Argentina has had in the decade since 2002. <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Alperovich" title="José Alperovich">José Alperovich</a>, elected governor in 2003, has presided over record investment in public works while reaping criticism for attempts to eliminate <a href="/wiki/Term_limit" title="Term limit">term limits</a> for his office.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tucum%C3%A1n_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dique_angostura_tucuman.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Dique_angostura_tucuman.JPG/220px-Dique_angostura_tucuman.JPG" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Dique_angostura_tucuman.JPG/330px-Dique_angostura_tucuman.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Dique_angostura_tucuman.JPG/440px-Dique_angostura_tucuman.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1165" data-file-height="763" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dique_angostura_tucuman.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tucumán: La Angostura Lake</div>

<p>Despite Tucumán's small size, it has two main different geographical systems. The east is associated with the <a href="/wiki/Gran_Chaco" title="Gran Chaco">Gran Chaco</a> flat lands, while the west presents a mixture of the Sierras of the <a href="/wiki/Pampa" class="mw-redirect" title="Pampa">Pampas</a> to the south and the canyons of the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Northwest" title="Argentine Northwest">Argentine Northwest</a> to the north. The <i><a href="/wiki/Cerro_del_Bols%C3%B3n" title="Cerro del Bolsón">Cerro del Bolsón</a></i> is the highest peak at an elevation of 5,550 metres (18,209&#160;ft).</p>
<p>The Salí is the province’s main river. Tucumán also has four dams that are used for hydroelectricity and irrigation: El Cadillal on Salí River, the province’s most important dam; Embalse Río Hondo on the Hondo River; La Angostura on de los Sosa River; and Escaba on the Marapa River. The Santa María River crosses the <a href="/wiki/Valles_Calchaqu%C3%ADes" class="mw-redirect" title="Valles Calchaquíes">Valles Calchaquíes</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tucum%C3%A1n_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_Argentina" title="Climate of Argentina">Climate of Argentina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climatic_regions_of_Argentina" title="Climatic regions of Argentina">Climatic regions of Argentina</a></div>

<p>Tucuman lies at the convergence of temperate climates and tropical climates further north, retaining characteristics of both in the lowlands. Furthermore, it is highly monsoonal, and depends also on elevation. As a rule of thumb, the far east is dryer and hotter, whereas the Piedmont sees many storms in the summer which moderate temperatures. The first foothills are very humid, provoking a dense jungle, and as altitude increases, a thick forest and then cool grasslands. The westernmost areas are once again a bit dryer because of a rain shadow.</p>
<p>The eastern parts have an average annual temperature of 18 to 20&#160;°C (64.4 to 68.0&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-Sesma1998_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sesma1998-7">[7]</a></sup> Summers are hot with mean temperatures averaging between 24 to 26&#160;°C (75.2 to 78.8&#160;°F) while in winter, the mean temperatures are between 10 to 12&#160;°C (50.0 to 53.6&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-Sesma1998_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sesma1998-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bravo_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bravo-8">[8]</a></sup> Easternmost parts of the province, which borders the Chaco region are home to the highest and lowest temperatures in the province where absolute maximum temperatures can exceed 40&#160;°C (104.0&#160;°F) while absolute minimum temperatures can reach close to −7&#160;°C (19.4&#160;°F) owing to the accumulation of cold air that descends from the mountains.<sup id="cite_ref-Sesma1998_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sesma1998-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IRNtucuman_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IRNtucuman-9">[9]</a></sup> At higher altitudes, the climate is cooler with summer temperatures averaging 20&#160;°C (68.0&#160;°F) and winter temperatures averaging 10&#160;°C (50.0&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-Sesma1998_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sesma1998-7">[7]</a></sup> The annual temperature in the higher altitudes is between 12 to 14&#160;°C (53.6 to 57.2&#160;°F) at an altitude of 2,500 metres (8,202.1&#160;ft) above sea level.<sup id="cite_ref-IRNtucuman_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IRNtucuman-9">[9]</a></sup> Within the valleys located between the mountains, temperatures are cooler with a mean annual temperature of 13.1&#160;°C (55.6&#160;°F) (summers average 17.1&#160;°C (62.8&#160;°F) while winters average 9.0&#160;°C (48.2&#160;°F)) in the Tafi valley.<sup id="cite_ref-Sesma1998_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sesma1998-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Precipitation in the lowlands ranges from 600&#160;mm (24&#160;in) in the east, to close to 1,200&#160;mm in the foothills (48&#160;in), in a very monsoonal pattern with 4-5 completely dry winter months, and a peak of about 200&#160;mm (7.9&#160;in) in the rainiest summer month.</p>
<p>The eastward facing slopes concentrate not only the heaviest precipitation, with spots around 1,800&#160;mm (71&#160;in) falling mostly in the 5 months of the summer monsoon; they also have a unique characteristic, which is that during part of the year, they are constantly immersed in a thick fog, providing humidity for the development of a thick jungle. The climate quickly becomes decidedly temperate with altitude, supporting different kinds of forest which even receive some snow every winter, finally reaching high-altitude grasslands with cool, windy weather year-round.</p>
<p>The abundant precipitation creates a wide area of abundant vegetation and justifies Tucumán’s title of "Jardín de la República" (Garden of the Republic).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tucum%C3%A1n_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tucuman_1853d.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Tucuman_1853d.JPG/220px-Tucuman_1853d.JPG" width="220" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Tucuman_1853d.JPG/330px-Tucuman_1853d.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Tucuman_1853d.JPG/440px-Tucuman_1853d.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1111" data-file-height="773" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tucuman_1853d.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/San_Miguel_de_Tucum%C3%A1n" title="San Miguel de Tucumán">San Miguel de Tucumán</a>.</div>

<p>Long among the most underdeveloped Argentine provinces, Tucumán Province has been growing strongly, and, in 2006, its economy reached US$7.2 billion (which shall be around US$11 billion in 2011, according to Argentina's economical growing), the nation's 7th largest. Its per capita output of US$5,400 (around US$7,900 in 2011) was nearly 40% below the national average but compares favourably with that of most of its neighbors.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2012, the per capita income of the province is of 8,000 dollars</p>
<p>Known internationally for its prodigious <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a> (with 2,300&#160;km², and the sugar production, 60% of the country's), Tucumán's economy is quite diversified and agriculture accounts for about 7% of output. After the sugar crisis of the 1960s, Tucumán tried to diversify its crops and now cultivates, among others, <a href="/wiki/Lemons" class="mw-redirect" title="Lemons">lemons</a> (world 1st. producer), <a href="/wiki/Strawberries" class="mw-redirect" title="Strawberries">strawberries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kiwifruit" title="Kiwifruit">kiwifruit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beans" class="mw-redirect" title="Beans">beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">alfalfa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybeans</a>.</p>
<p>Cattle, sheep, and goats are raised mainly for local consumption.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">Manufacturing</a> in Tucumán initially centered on sugar production but has diversified significantly since 1960. Sugar mills add about 15% to the total economy. Besides the industrialisation of the sugarcane into sugar, paper, and alcohol, there are food, textile, <a href="/wiki/Automotive_industry" title="Automotive industry">automotive</a>, and metallurgical industries. Among the latter, the freight-<a href="/wiki/Truck" title="Truck">truck</a> assembly operated by the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Group" title="Volkswagen Group">Volkswagen Group</a> controlled <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> <a href="/wiki/Scania_(company)" class="mw-redirect" title="Scania (company)">Scania</a> company is probably the best known. Mining is a minor activity, centered on salt, clay, lime and other non-metallic extractions.</p>
<p>Cultural and sport tourism is common in the province, and attracts a number of Argentine tourists every year. The <a href="/wiki/Panamerican_Highway" class="mw-redirect" title="Panamerican Highway">Panamerican Highway</a> (<a href="/wiki/National_Route_9_(Argentina)" title="National Route 9 (Argentina)">Route 9</a>) crosses <a href="/wiki/San_Miguel_de_Tucum%C3%A1n" title="San Miguel de Tucumán">San Miguel de Tucumán</a>, and connects it with <a href="/wiki/Santiago_del_Estero" title="Santiago del Estero">Santiago del Estero</a> and Buenos Aires. The city also serves as a mid-stop for tourists visiting other provinces of the Argentine Northwest. The <a href="/wiki/Teniente_General_Benjam%C3%ADn_Matienzo_International_Airport" title="Teniente General Benjamín Matienzo International Airport">Teniente General Benjamín Matienzo International Airport</a> have regular flights to <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a> and <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_de_la_Sierra" title="Santa Cruz de la Sierra">Santa Cruz de la Sierra</a>, and receives almost 300,000&#160;passengers every year.</p>
<p>The most visited destinations of the Province are the <a href="/wiki/Campo_de_los_Alisos_National_Park" title="Campo de los Alisos National Park">Campo de los Alisos National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Valles_Calchaqu%C3%ADes" class="mw-redirect" title="Valles Calchaquíes">Valles Calchaquíes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taf%C3%AD_del_Valle" title="Tafí del Valle">Tafí del Valle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruins_of_Quilmes" title="Ruins of Quilmes">Ruins of Quilmes</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Diaguita" title="Diaguita">Diaguita</a> community of <a href="/wiki/Amaicha_del_Valle" title="Amaicha del Valle">Amaicha del Valle</a>, and the city of <a href="/wiki/San_Miguel_de_Tucum%C3%A1n" title="San Miguel de Tucumán">San Miguel de Tucumán</a>.</p>
<p>There are four important universities in the province: the <a href="/wiki/Universidad_Nacional_de_Tucum%C3%A1n" class="mw-redirect" title="Universidad Nacional de Tucumán">Universidad Nacional de Tucumán</a> (with 60,000 students), the <a href="/wiki/Universidad_Tecnol%C3%B3gica_Nacional" class="mw-redirect" title="Universidad Tecnológica Nacional">Universidad Tecnológica Nacional</a>, <a href="/wiki/Universidad_del_Norte_Santo_Tom%C3%A1s_de_Aquino" class="mw-redirect" title="Universidad del Norte Santo Tomás de Aquino">Universidad del Norte Santo Tomás de Aquino</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Universidad_de_San_Pablo-T" class="mw-redirect" title="Universidad de San Pablo-T">Universidad de San Pablo-T</a>.</p>
