			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">2010 United Kingdom student protests</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>2010 United Kingdom student protests</b> were a series of <a href="/wiki/Demonstration_(people)" class="mw-redirect" title="Demonstration (people)">demonstrations</a> in November and December 2010 that took place in several areas of the country, with the focal point of protests being in <a href="/wiki/Central_London" title="Central London">central London</a>. Largely student-led, the protests were held in opposition to planned spending cuts to <a href="/wiki/Further_education" title="Further education">further education</a> and an increase of the cap on <a href="/wiki/Tuition_fees_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tuition fees (UK)">tuition fees</a> by the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_coalition_government_(2010%E2%80%93present)" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom coalition government (2010–present)">Conservative-Liberal Democrat coalition government</a> following their <a href="/wiki/Browne_Review" title="Browne Review">review into higher education funding</a> in England. Student groups said that the intended cuts to education were excessive, would damage higher education, give students higher debts, and broke campaign promises made by politicians.</p>
<p>The first major demonstration occurred on 10 November, jointly organised by the <a href="/wiki/National_Union_of_Students_(United_Kingdom)" title="National Union of Students (United Kingdom)">National Union of Students</a> (NUS) and the <a href="/wiki/University_and_College_Union" title="University and College Union">University and College Union</a> (UCU). It involved between 30,000 and 50,000 demonstrators marching through central London, with several hundred branching off to attack and occupy the Conservative Party headquarters. This measure brought condemnation from the establishment and a divide within the student movement over the appropriateness of such tactics. The <a href="/wiki/National_Campaign_Against_Fees_and_Cuts" title="National Campaign Against Fees and Cuts">National Campaign Against Fees and Cuts</a> (NCAFC) called for a mass walk-out and demonstration on 24 November, with occupations taking place at campuses throughout the UK. A march in central London was <a href="/wiki/Kettling" title="Kettling">kettled</a> in <a href="/wiki/Whitehall" title="Whitehall">Whitehall</a>, resulting in violent confrontation with protesters. Further demonstrations were held in central London on 30 November, when police clashed with protesters and kettled them in <a href="/wiki/Trafalgar_Square" title="Trafalgar Square">Trafalgar Square</a>, while other protests took place throughout the country. Another central London protest took place on 9 December, the day that the proposed reforms were passed into law, with protesters clashing with police and being kettled in <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Square" title="Parliament Square">Parliament Square</a>.</p>
<p>The student protests were unsuccessful in their aim of preventing the government's reforms. The demonstrations had been highly controversial in the UK, being condemned for instances of violence and vandalism by the establishment. The behaviour of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Police" class="mw-redirect" title="Metropolitan Police">Metropolitan Police</a> in dealing with the protests were also widely criticised for instances of untruthfulness and excessive use of force.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Browne_Report_and_proposed_reforms">The Browne Report and proposed reforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Browne Report and proposed reforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clegg_Victory_for_the_Gurkhas.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Clegg_Victory_for_the_Gurkhas.jpg/300px-Clegg_Victory_for_the_Gurkhas.jpg" width="300" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Clegg_Victory_for_the_Gurkhas.jpg/450px-Clegg_Victory_for_the_Gurkhas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Clegg_Victory_for_the_Gurkhas.jpg/600px-Clegg_Victory_for_the_Gurkhas.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1066" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clegg_Victory_for_the_Gurkhas.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Prime Minister David Cameron (left) and Deputy Prime Minister Nick Clegg (right) were at the heart of much student anger</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In November 2009, the centrist <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> government of Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Brown" title="Gordon Brown">Gordon Brown</a> commissioned a study into higher education funding in England. Chaired by <a href="/wiki/John_Browne,_Baron_Browne_of_Madingley" title="John Browne, Baron Browne of Madingley">Lord Browne of Madingley</a>, the former chief executive of <a href="/wiki/BP" title="BP">BP</a>, the report was to be titled the <a href="/wiki/Browne_Review" title="Browne Review">Browne Review</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In the build up to the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">United Kingdom General Election in May 2010</a>, the leader of the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats" title="Liberal Democrats">Liberal Democrats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nick_Clegg" title="Nick Clegg">Nick Clegg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vote_for_Students_pledge" title="Vote for Students pledge">pledged</a> that he would vote against any proposed increase in tuition fees if elected to Parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-Clegg_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clegg-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the election and resulting <a href="/wiki/Hung_parliament" title="Hung parliament">hung parliament</a>, Clegg made an agreement with the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> to form a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_coalition_government_(2010%E2%80%93present)" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom coalition government (2010–present)">coalition government</a> in which Conservative leader <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> became Prime Minister and Clegg became Deputy Prime Minister. The Browne Review was subsequently published in October 2010, and contained the suggestion that the government should remove outright the existing cap of £3,290 on <a href="/wiki/Tuition_fees_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tuition fees (UK)">tuition fees</a>. The government rejected this proposal, instead choosing to keep a cap but increasing it to £9,000.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/David_Willetts" title="David Willetts">David Willetts</a>, the Minister of State for Universities and Science, stated that the measures were "a very progressive package" and "at the end of this we will have a better university system than we have at the moment."<sup id="cite_ref-Student_tuition_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Student_tuition-4">[4]</a></sup> Contravening his pre-election pledge, Clegg expressed support for the rise in the cap on tuition fees, which would result in future students paying higher fees for their university education.<sup id="cite_ref-Nick_Clegg_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nick_Clegg-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_opposition">Student opposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Student opposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several, but not all Student union leaders were critical of the cuts. The <a href="/wiki/National_Union_of_Students_(United_Kingdom)" title="National Union of Students (United Kingdom)">National Union of Students</a> (NUS) feared that the increased cap on tuition fees would prevent potential students from poorer backgrounds from attending university.<sup id="cite_ref-Cameron.27s_response_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cameron.27s_response-6">[6]</a></sup> Many protesters focused in particular on Clegg's campaign promise that he would oppose any rise in tuition fees. David Barclay, the president of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a>'s student union, said: "This is the day a generation of politicians learn that though they might forget their promises, students won't."<sup id="cite_ref-Student_tuition_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Student_tuition-4">[4]</a></sup> Rahul Mansigani, the students' union president for the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a>, said: "Large numbers of students voted for the Liberal Democrats, and there is no question that the pledge is a binding commitment."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two weeks before, on 28 October, a protest was held in the University of Oxford to coincide with a visit from the Liberal Democrat minister and Business Secretary <a href="/wiki/Vince_Cable" title="Vince Cable">Vince Cable</a>. Cable cancelled his visit after taking advice from the police about the protest.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Several days later, on 3 November, there was a <a href="/wiki/2010_student_protest_in_Dublin" title="2010 student protest in Dublin">student protest in Dublin</a>. The subsequent London protest was described by one Irish reporter as "scenes bizarrely similar" to those in the Irish capital.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The initial event was the largest student protest in Britain since the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> government first proposed the <a href="/wiki/Teaching_and_Higher_Education_Act_1998" title="Teaching and Higher Education Act 1998">Teaching and Higher Education Act</a> in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-Thousands_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thousands-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>The first major demonstration in protest at the government's proposed reforms was held on 10 November 2010 in central London, jointly organised by the <a href="/wiki/National_Union_of_Students_(United_Kingdom)" title="National Union of Students (United Kingdom)">National Union of Students</a> (NUS) and the <a href="/wiki/University_and_College_Union" title="University and College Union">University and College Union</a> (UCU). This demonstration was officially known as "Fund Our Future: Stop Education Cuts", although also termed "Demo 2010" or "Demo-lition 10.11.10".<sup id="cite_ref-promote_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-promote-11">[11]</a></sup> Arriving from all regions of Great Britain and Northern Ireland,<sup id="cite_ref-Student_tuition_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Student_tuition-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NI_students_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NI_students-12">[12]</a></sup> approximately 30,000 to 52,000 protesters attended the demonstration.<sup id="cite_ref-Student_tuition_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Student_tuition-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Student_Protest_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Student_Protest-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Demo_2010_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Demo_2010-14">[14]</a></sup> The demonstration's route was pre-approved with the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Police_Service" title="Metropolitan Police Service">Metropolitan Police Service</a>, with marchers moving from <a href="/wiki/Whitehall" title="Whitehall">Whitehall</a> past <a href="/wiki/Downing_Street" title="Downing Street">Downing Street</a>, the home of the Prime Minister, and then past the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Houses of Parliament</a>, chanting such slogans as "no ifs, no buts – no education cuts", "they say cut back – we say fight back", "I say Tories – you say scum".<sup id="cite_ref-NI_students_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NI_students-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Student_Protest_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Student_Protest-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Demo_2010_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Demo_2010-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reuters_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-15">[15]</a></sup> Journalist Harry Mount of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i>, said: "Perhaps because their cause was justified, the students I saw had none of the swaggering, self-righteous manner of the student protester of legend."<sup id="cite_ref-The_students_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_students-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Political groups that sent contingents to take part included the Labour Party, <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">Green Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Workers_Party_(Britain)" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialist Workers Party (Britain)">Socialist Workers Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(England_and_Wales)" title="Socialist Party (England and Wales)">Socialist Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Revolution_(political_group)" title="Revolution (political group)">Revolution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Young_Communist_League_(Britain)" class="mw-redirect" title="Young Communist League (Britain)">Young Communist League</a>, <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_Communist_Group_(UK)" title="Revolutionary Communist Group (UK)">Revolutionary Communist Group</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Communist_Students_(Autonomous)" title="Communist Students (Autonomous)">Communist Students</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Morning_Star_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morning_Star-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RCG_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCG-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Communist_Students_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Communist_Students-19">[19]</a></sup> A few members of parliament (MPs) joined the demonstration, among them Labour MP <a href="/wiki/John_McDonnell_(politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="John McDonnell (politician)">John McDonnell</a>, who told reporters: "This is the biggest workers' and students' demonstration in decades. It just shows what can be done when people get angry. We must build on this".<sup id="cite_ref-Morning_Star_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morning_Star-17">[17]</a></sup> Representatives of the <a href="/wiki/National_Pensioners_Convention" title="National Pensioners Convention">National Pensioners Convention</a> also took part, with the group's banner carrier, Janet Shapiro, stating that: "We're here because we believe education should be free, funded by the taxpayer. It is something that benefits the community, the country."<sup id="cite_ref-Channel_4_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Channel_4-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the end of the march, a <a href="/wiki/Political_rally" class="mw-redirect" title="Political rally">rally</a> took place outside <a href="/wiki/Tate_Britain" title="Tate Britain">Tate Britain</a> where demonstrators were addressed by the UCU general secretary Sally Hunt, who introduced a series of clips displayed on a giant plasma screen featuring Clegg giving a series of promises to the electorate on tuition fees, all of which he had subsequently broken.<sup id="cite_ref-Channel_4_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Channel_4-20">[20]</a></sup> Hunt stated that making the public university system in Britain "the most expensive in the world" wasn't fair, that discouraging young people from going to college was not progressive, and that the increase in tuition fees represented further debts for students.<sup id="cite_ref-promote_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-promote-11">[11]</a></sup> The rally was also addressed by NUS President <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Porter" title="Aaron Porter">Aaron Porter</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Trades_Union_Congress" title="Trades Union Congress">Trades Union Congress</a> Deputy general secretary <a href="/wiki/Frances_O%27Grady" title="Frances O'Grady">Frances O'Grady</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-promote_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-promote-11">[11]</a></sup> O'Grady offered the message to the government that: "Don't you dare tell us we're all in this together. The deficit certainly wasn't caused by the students."<sup id="cite_ref-Channel_4_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Channel_4-20">[20]</a></sup> The protest was scheduled to end at 2&#160;pm, but slightly overran.<sup id="cite_ref-Financial_Times_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Financial_Times-21">[21]</a></sup> The Metropolitan Police were expecting 20,000 demonstrators to turn out, well below the 50,000 figure most widely quoted in the press after the event, and did not expect any violence, so deployed only 225 officers to police the event.<sup id="cite_ref-Demo_2010_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Demo_2010-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Millbank_occupation">Millbank occupation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Millbank occupation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the afternoon, as protesters passed the Houses of Parliament and moved towards Tate Britain for the rally, several thousand surrounded <a href="/wiki/Millbank_Tower" title="Millbank Tower">30 Millbank</a> in <a href="/wiki/City_of_Westminster" title="City of Westminster">Westminster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Campaign_Headquarters" title="Conservative Campaign Headquarters">campaign headquarters of the Conservative Party</a>, despite attempts by NUS organisers to stop them.<sup id="cite_ref-Financial_Times_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Financial_Times-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Forcing their way past the limited police presence,<sup id="cite_ref-Met_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Met-23">[23]</a></sup> approximately 200 people broke in and occupied the building, whilst a thousand more cheered and supported them from outside.<sup id="cite_ref-Demo_2010_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Demo_2010-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_Info_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_Info-24">[24]</a></sup> These left-wing protesters lit placards on fire, and smashed windows before occupying and vandalising the reception area. Petrified staff working in the building were evacuated by police around 1&#160;pm.<sup id="cite_ref-Demo_2010_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Demo_2010-14">[14]</a></sup> Around 100 protesters proceeded to the roof of the building, chanting slogans including "<a href="/wiki/May_2010_Greek_protests" class="mw-redirect" title="May 2010 Greek protests">Greece!</a> <a href="/wiki/2010_French_pension_reform_strikes" title="2010 French pension reform strikes">France!</a> Now here too".<sup id="cite_ref-NI_students_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NI_students-12">[12]</a></sup> Initial press sources blamed this action on a group of <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchists</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Student_Protest_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Student_Protest-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_students_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_students-16">[16]</a></sup> This was later disputed, with a reporter from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> noting that most of the protesters occupying the building were students who had been radicalised to become left-wing Trotskyites in opposition to the cuts, and were not all political anarchists.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_Info_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_Info-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rooftop_protest_at_30_Millbank.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Rooftop_protest_at_30_Millbank.jpg/300px-Rooftop_protest_at_30_Millbank.jpg" width="300" height="199" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Rooftop_protest_at_30_Millbank.jpg/450px-Rooftop_protest_at_30_Millbank.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Rooftop_protest_at_30_Millbank.jpg/600px-Rooftop_protest_at_30_Millbank.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="2000" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rooftop_protest_at_30_Millbank.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Protesters on the roof of 30 Millbank</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Riot police from the <a href="/wiki/Territorial_Support_Group" title="Territorial Support Group">Territorial Support Group</a> arrived an hour after the building was occupied to remove the protesters.<sup id="cite_ref-Met_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Met-23">[23]</a></sup> In retaliation, the police were pelted with eggs, rotten fruit, banners, and shards of glass.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> One of the protesters on the building's roof, Edward Woolard, threw a <a href="/wiki/Fire_extinguisher" title="Fire extinguisher">fire extinguisher</a> onto the police below. He received instant criticism from some of the protesting crowds, who called on those on the roof to "stop throwing shit".<sup id="cite_ref-Demo_2010_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Demo_2010-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[nb 1]</a></sup> To control the situation, police used the controversial technique of <a href="/wiki/Kettling" title="Kettling">kettling</a> to trap the protesters within Millbank Square, the forecourt to 30 Millbank, whilst protesters on the outside of the building were pushed back.<sup id="cite_ref-Financial_Times_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Financial_Times-21">[21]</a></sup> The police began letting demonstrators out of the building from 6:30&#160;pm, arresting those whom they believed were responsible for vandalism.<sup id="cite_ref-Demo_2010_14-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Demo_2010-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The demonstration led to a disruption in London's transport, with journalist Harry Mount stating: "I have never seen London traffic so jammed in 39 years living in the city."<sup id="cite_ref-The_students_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_students-16">[16]</a></sup> Alongside the occupation of Millbank, a smaller number of protesters had travelled to the headquarters of the Liberal Democrats in Cowley Street, where a car window was smashed.<sup id="cite_ref-Student_tuition_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Student_tuition-4">[4]</a></sup> In all, 14 people were injured and required hospitalisation, at least three of whom were police officers, whilst police arrested 35 of the demonstrators, sending them off to various police stations around the city.<sup id="cite_ref-Student_tuition_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Student_tuition-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Demo_2010_14-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Demo_2010-14">[14]</a></sup> Later accounts that the numbers arrested had risen to 54, (33 men and 21 women), ten of whom were aged under 18 and the majority of whom were students.<sup id="cite_ref-Man_arrested_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Man_arrested-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Response_to_the_Millbank_occupation">Response to the Millbank occupation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Response to the Millbank occupation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Students_and_unions">Students and unions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Students and unions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>NUS President Aaron Porter condemned the occupation of 30 Millbank, claiming that it was caused by "those who are here to cause trouble" and that he was "disgusted that the actions of a minority of idiots are trying to undermine 50,000 who came to make a peaceful protest."<sup id="cite_ref-Student_Protest_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Student_Protest-13">[13]</a></sup> General Secretary of UCU Sally Hunt also condemned the occupation, declaring that "the overwhelming majority of staff and students on the march came here to send a clear and peaceful message to the politicians.... The actions of a minority, out of 50,000 people, is regrettable."<sup id="cite_ref-NI_students_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NI_students-12">[12]</a></sup> Oxford University's Student Union (OUSU) President David Barclay issued a statement declaring that "OUSU supports the rights of students to protest non-violently. It is hugely unfortunate that some people yesterday were injured and that arrests were made."<sup id="cite_ref-Youth_Revolt_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Youth_Revolt-30">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Contrastingly, other student leaders, trade unionists, and academics expressed support for elements of the occupation of 30 Millbank. Amongst others, the president of University of London Union <a href="/wiki/Clare_Solomon" class="mw-redirect" title="Clare Solomon">Clare Solomon</a>, the Education Officer of the London School of Economics, Ashok Kumar, the Education and Campaigns Officer at University College London, Michael Chessum, the National Union of Students' black students' officer Kanjay Sesay, the NUS' LGBT students' officers Vicki Baars and Alan Bailey, the President of the <a href="/wiki/National_Union_of_Rail,_Maritime_and_Transport_Workers" title="National Union of Rail, Maritime and Transport Workers">RMT</a> trade union Alex Gordon and the playwright <a href="/wiki/Lee_Hall_(playwright)" title="Lee Hall (playwright)">Lee Hall</a> all signed a statement declaring that:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>We reject any attempt to characterise the Millbank protest as small, "extremist" or unrepresentative of our movement. We celebrate the fact that thousands of students were willing to send a message to the Tories that we will fight to win. Occupations are a long established tradition in the student movement that should be defended. It is this kind of action in France and Greece that has been an inspiration to many workers and students in Britain faced with such a huge assault on jobs, benefits, housing and the public sector. We stand with the protesters, and anyone who is victimised as a result of the protest.<sup id="cite_ref-Sign_the_Unity_Statement_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sign_the_Unity_Statement-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Solomon informed the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> that she had "no problem with direct actions or occupation", and when questioned regarding the damage done to Millbank, responded that "these were a few windows of the Tory Party headquarters – what they're doing to our education is absolutely millions... and they want to complain about a few windows."<sup id="cite_ref-Cameron.27s_response_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cameron.27s_response-6">[6]</a></sup> Some socialist and student commentators criticised Porter and the NUS for their response to the situation, accusing them of careerism.<sup id="cite_ref-Palestine_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palestine-32">[31]</a></sup> Meanwhile, various university Conservative societies around London condemned the protests, and criticised students' unions for providing the "false impression that the majority of students are left-wing" and opposed to the governments' proposed reforms.<sup id="cite_ref-Conservative_Societies_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conservative_Societies-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_and_press">Government and press</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Government and press">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tory_Scum.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Tory_Scum.jpg/300px-Tory_Scum.jpg" width="300" height="191" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Tory_Scum.jpg/450px-Tory_Scum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/37/Tory_Scum.jpg 2x" data-file-width="582" data-file-height="370" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tory_Scum.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Anti-Conservative graffiti scrawled during the protest.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Metropolitan Police Service admitted that they were unprepared to deal with the occupation of 30 Millbank, something which they had not been expecting. <a href="/wiki/Paul_Stephenson_(police_officer)" title="Paul Stephenson (police officer)">Sir Paul Stephenson</a>, the Metropolitan Police's Commissioner, told the press that he was "embarrassed" by how police had lost control of the situation, and condemned what he saw as "thuggish, loutish behaviour by criminals."<sup id="cite_ref-Met_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Met-23">[23]</a></sup> Stephenson remarked that "the one thing I would say is that it must have been an awful time for the people trying to go about their daily business in those buildings. I feel terribly sorry that they have had to go through what must have been quite a traumatic experience… We are determined to make sure that sort of thing does not happen again on our streets. I'm clear on that, the Met is clear on that."<sup id="cite_ref-Met_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Met-23">[23]</a></sup> His views were echoed by <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_London" title="Mayor of London">Mayor of London</a> <a href="/wiki/Boris_Johnson" title="Boris Johnson">Boris Johnson</a>, who stated: "This is intolerable and all those involved will be pursued and they will face the full force of the law… The Metropolitan Police commissioner has assured me that there will be a vigorous post-incident investigation. He will also be reviewing police planning and response."<sup id="cite_ref-Met_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Met-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> condemned the occupation of Millbank, stating that he would not abandon his position on the issue of education cuts. Speaking from the <a href="/wiki/2010_G-20_Seoul_summit" class="mw-redirect" title="2010 G-20 Seoul summit">2010 G-20 Seoul summit</a> in South Korea, Cameron said the occasion had been "extremely serious" and praised the "bravery" of the police officers. He also stated that the actions of the protesters were "unacceptable" and that "I was worried for the safety of the people in the building because I know people who work there".<sup id="cite_ref-Cameron.27s_response_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cameron.27s_response-6">[6]</a></sup> The following morning, Clegg went on <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">ITV</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Daybreak_(ITV)" class="mw-redirect" title="Daybreak (ITV)">Daybreak</a></i> to state: "I should have been more careful perhaps in signing that pledge [to not increase tuition fees]... At the time [prior to his election] I really thought we could do it. I just didn't know, of course, before we came into government, quite what the state of the finances were."<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>A reporter from <i>The Daily Telegraph</i> commented that the "anarchic behaviour" of those occupying Millbank was "counter-productive" to the students' cause, and that it was the photographs of "a few hundred vicious hotheads" that would "linger" in the public imagination rather than that of the main march.<sup id="cite_ref-Infantile_behaviour_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Infantile_behaviour-35">[34]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Financial_Times" title="Financial Times">Financial Times</a></i> reported that an anonymous vice-chancellor from a London university had told them that the violence would undermine the campaign, and that it "could not have gone better for the government. <a href="/wiki/George_Osborne" title="George Osborne">George Osborne</a> will be delighted."<sup id="cite_ref-Financial_Times_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Financial_Times-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_protests">Related protests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Related protests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the following days, smaller protests were held in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, with no violent confrontations.<sup id="cite_ref-Manchester_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Manchester-36">[35]</a></sup> On 11 November, student protesters occupied a building at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Manchester" title="University of Manchester">University of Manchester</a> for three hours, demanding to see the accounts that discussed how government funding cuts would affect students. Between 60 and 100 students held a peaceful <a href="/wiki/Sit-in" title="Sit-in">sit-in</a> at Manchester's John Owens Building on Oxford Road after an NUS meeting earlier that day.<sup id="cite_ref-Manchester_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Manchester-36">[35]</a></sup> Representing this group, protester Jeremy Buck said: "This is just what a few students who had the energy left after the London demo managed to achieve… Imagine what will happen when they have enough time to organise properly for the 24th. It is a matter of watch this space."<sup id="cite_ref-Sign_the_Unity_Statement_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sign_the_Unity_Statement-31">[30]</a></sup> That same day, protesters at the University of Cambridge held a demonstration against the cuts at the university's annual science, engineering and technology careers fair.<sup id="cite_ref-Sign_the_Unity_Statement_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sign_the_Unity_Statement-31">[30]</a></sup> On 23 November, anti-education cuts protesters had assembled outside of the offices of <i>The Guardian</i> newspaper, where Clegg was giving his <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Young" title="Hugo Young">Hugo Young</a> lecture. They brought out an effigy of Clegg, sentencing it to death and executing it by <a href="/wiki/Hanging" title="Hanging">hanging</a>, shouting the slogan "Nick Clegg, shame on you, shame on you for turning <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">blue</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Nick_Clegg_must_die_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nick_Clegg_must_die-37">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="24_November">24 November</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 24 November">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A second significant demonstration took place in London on 24 November, which again led to clashes with police, this time outside Whitehall, after police <a href="/wiki/Kettling" title="Kettling">kettled</a> a large crowd.<sup id="cite_ref-new_era_of_riots_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-new_era_of_riots-38">[37]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A more detailed citation is required. (March 2016)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-students_stage_day_of_protests_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-students_stage_day_of_protests-39">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>An organisation known as the <a href="/wiki/National_Campaign_Against_Fees_and_Cuts" title="National Campaign Against Fees and Cuts">National Campaign Against Fees and Cuts</a> (NCAFC) organised a mass national walk-out of education and protest for 24 November.<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup> As a part of this, demonstrations were held in London and other locations across the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-new_era_of_riots_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-new_era_of_riots-38">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-students_stage_day_of_protests_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-students_stage_day_of_protests-39">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> According to a group on the social networking website Facebook, 25,000 people had signed up to take the day off from studies and protest prior to the actual event,<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup> and these protesters included not only university students, but also school children who had walked out of lessons to join the demonstration.<sup id="cite_ref-students_stage_day_of_protests_39-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-students_stage_day_of_protests-39">[38]</a></sup> The NCAFC encouraged protesters to use social media to invite others to join them.<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup> One of those protesting was <a href="/wiki/Jessica_Linley" title="Jessica Linley">Jessica Linley</a>, a law student at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Nottingham" title="University of Nottingham">University of Nottingham</a> who had been crowned <a href="/wiki/Miss_England" title="Miss England">Miss England</a> in September 2010 and who used her status to gain media coverage for the cause, telling press that she would not be able to afford to go to university if the tuition fees were increased, and that "these sweeping austerity measures are unacceptable."<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Whitehall_march">Whitehall march</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Whitehall march">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several thousand protesters, most of whom were students or school children, gathered in <a href="/wiki/Trafalgar_Square" title="Trafalgar Square">Trafalgar Square</a>, central London, late in the morning. They proceeded toward Whitehall shouting the slogan, "fuck David Cameron".<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup> Believing that there had been a lack of officers at 10 November demonstration, the Metropolitan Police drafted in 1000 police officers from across <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a> to oversee the event, almost five times the number at 10 November.<sup id="cite_ref-era_of_violence_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-era_of_violence-42">[41]</a></sup> Police informed press that they planned to monitor fringe elements within the demonstration whom they believed planned to encourage "vandalism and violence".<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:24_Nov_protests_Trafalgar_police_van.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/24_Nov_protests_Trafalgar_police_van.jpg/220px-24_Nov_protests_Trafalgar_police_van.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/24_Nov_protests_Trafalgar_police_van.jpg/330px-24_Nov_protests_Trafalgar_police_van.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/24_Nov_protests_Trafalgar_police_van.jpg/440px-24_Nov_protests_Trafalgar_police_van.jpg 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="540" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:24_Nov_protests_Trafalgar_police_van.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A vandalised police vehicle on Whitehall</div>

<p>At Whitehall, just before 1&#160;pm, police prevented the protesters from reaching <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Square" title="Parliament Square">Parliament Square</a> and the Houses of Parliament, setting up a line of riot police to kettle the protesters. A reporter <i>The Guardian</i> described the crowd at this point as being "predominantly good natured, although very noisy". Nonetheless, the demonstrators tried to push through the police line, leading to clashes. An unoccupied police van which had been left in the midst of the crowd was vandalised; protesters rocked it back and forth, climbed on its roof, smashed its windows, wrote <a href="/wiki/Graffiti" title="Graffiti">graffiti</a> on it and threw a <a href="/wiki/Smoke_bomb" title="Smoke bomb">smoke bomb</a> inside.<sup id="cite_ref-students_stage_day_of_protests_39-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-students_stage_day_of_protests-39">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup> A group of school girls encircled the van, urging people not to vandalise it. One of them later told reporters that "the cause that we're here for today isn't about 'I hate the police, I want to burn the police and I want to destroy everything they represent.'"<sup id="cite_ref-Other_face_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Other_face-43">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>At around 6&#160;pm, <a href="/wiki/Mounted_police" title="Mounted police">mounted police</a> charged at the north end of the crowd to push them back; despite video evidence police denied that this was an actual charge, describing it as crowd control using horses.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Battle_lines_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Battle_lines-45">[44]</a></sup> Roughly 1000 protesters broke free of the police kettle, running throughout central London while pursued by police; <i>The Guardian'</i>s Paul Lewis stated that "police were caught in a game of cat and mouse, along <a href="/wiki/Charing_Cross" title="Charing Cross">Charing Cross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Covent_Garden" title="Covent Garden">Covent Garden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Picadilly_Circus" class="mw-redirect" title="Picadilly Circus">Picadilly Circus</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup> Some of these protesters committed acts of vandalism along the side streets, including knocking over rubbish bins and throwing <a href="/wiki/Traffic_cones" class="mw-redirect" title="Traffic cones">traffic cones</a> into the road.<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup> Various bus shelters and ticket machines in the area were vandalised, with <a href="/wiki/Transport_for_London" title="Transport for London">Transport for London</a> diverting buses away from the unrest.<sup id="cite_ref-students_stage_day_of_protests_39-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-students_stage_day_of_protests-39">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-era_of_violence_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-era_of_violence-42">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Approximately 200 protesters were unable to escape and remained kettled in Whitehall. Police falsely informed press that the crowd were provided with toilet facilities and water; protesters disputed these claims on social media, with ULU President Clare Solomon commenting on Facebook: "we're still illegally kettled in the freezing cold on Whitehall. No food, water or toilets despite what the police are telling the media. Thousands of young people needing to go home."<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup> To keep warm, protesters set fire to a ticket machine within the Square, prompting riot police to secure the machine and put out the flames.<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup> From 9 to 10&#160;pm, police permitted the rest of the Whitehall protesters to leave, approximately nine to ten hours after they had first been contained, some being searched as they left.<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Battle_lines_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Battle_lines-45">[44]</a></sup> During the clashes, police arrested 41 protesters.<sup id="cite_ref-era_of_violence_42-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-era_of_violence-42">[41]</a></sup> A number of individuals were also injured, including seven police officers and 11 others.<sup id="cite_ref-era_of_violence_42-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-era_of_violence-42">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupations_and_local_protests">Occupations and local protests <span id="Occupations"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Occupations and local protests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Across the UK, protesters occupied university buildings in at least 12 universities.<sup id="cite_ref-students_stage_day_of_protests_39-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-students_stage_day_of_protests-39">[38]</a></sup> On 23 November, protesters occupied the picture gallery corridor of <a href="/wiki/Royal_Holloway,_University_of_London" title="Royal Holloway, University of London">Royal Holloway, University of London</a>. They were later joined by supportive members of university staff who took part in what was labelled a "teach-in".<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup> On 24 November, the Language Centre at <a href="/wiki/London_South_Bank_University" title="London South Bank University">London South Bank University</a> was occupied for 51 hours by over 100 students as part of the "Defend LSBU! Defend Our Education!" campaign.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> That same day, students occupied the <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Bentham" title="Jeremy Bentham">Jeremy Bentham</a> Room in <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College London</a>, stating that they were protesting against "savage cuts to higher education and government attempts to force society to pay for a crisis it didn't cause."<sup id="cite_ref-students_stage_day_of_protests_39-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-students_stage_day_of_protests-39">[38]</a></sup> A BBC reporter visited the occupation, remarking that the protesters "seem as distant from the old <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">left</a> as they do from the new <a href="/wiki/Right-wing_politics" title="Right-wing politics">right</a>" and that "you get a reminder that these are students born in the 1990s. They're quoting <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">Harry Potter</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Che_Guevara" title="Che Guevara">Che Guevara</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup> Protesters also occupied the <a href="/wiki/University_of_East_London" title="University of East London">University of East London</a>, demanding that university managers "put pressure on the government on the issue of H[higher] E[ducation] cuts and tuition fee rises".<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 24 November, students and supporters went into occupation of <a href="/wiki/Appleton_Tower" title="Appleton Tower">Appleton Tower</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a>, stating "We stand firm alongside all other students, university staff and others nationwide affected by education cuts and the risk this poses to the future of higher education."<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> At Manchester's University Place, 3000 students assembled to demonstrate, but several hundred broke away to march towards the <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Town_Hall" title="Manchester Town Hall">town hall</a>. A group of about 100 occupied a lecture theatre in the Roscoe Building. At the University of Oxford university sixth form students initially occupied the <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_Camera" title="Radcliffe Camera">Radcliffe Camera</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup> At the University of Cambridge, 200 students scaled the fence of <a href="/wiki/Senate_House_(University_of_Cambridge)" title="Senate House (University of Cambridge)">Senate House</a> and marched onto the grounds of <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="King's College, Cambridge">King's College</a>, then on 26 November students started an 11-day occupation of part of <a href="/wiki/Old_Schools" title="Old Schools">Old Schools</a>, the main administrative block of the University.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bristol" title="University of Bristol">University of Bristol</a>, 2000 protesters clashed with police when they tried to move into the city centre. Four were arrested. In <a href="/wiki/Brighton" title="Brighton">Brighton</a>, 3000 demonstrators marched through the city, with nearly 50 occupying a university building.<sup id="cite_ref-Students_join_forces_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Students_join_forces-51">[50]</a></sup> Hundreds of students from <a href="/wiki/Kingston_University" title="Kingston University">Kingston University</a> and various local schools staged an impromptu march through the town and a sit down protest at College Roundabout, leading to one arrest. In the morning of 24 November, demonstrators at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Birmingham" title="University of Birmingham">University of Birmingham</a> occupied the Aston Webb building, the site of the Prime Ministerial debates earlier in the year; they issued a statement in which they declared that "we believe the government's cuts to be economically unnecessary, unfair and ideologically motivated" and that "if [the government] continue to destroy the livelihoods of the majority to benefit the rich and powerful minority, they will face increasingly widespread and radical action."<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, protesters amassed at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leeds" title="University of Leeds">University of Leeds</a>. Hundreds of them had walked out of the local Allerton Grange High School to join the demonstration, and later occupied the Michael Sadler lecture theatre and a room at <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Metropolitan_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Leeds Metropolitan University">Leeds Metropolitan University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup> A room at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Plymouth" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Plymouth">University of Plymouth</a> was also occupied.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_University" title="Cardiff University">Cardiff University</a>, around 200 protesters occupied a lecture theatre after failing to gain entry to the vice-chancellor's building.<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Response">Response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>During the Whitehall incident, the Metropolitan Police publicly defended their use of kettling, with Chief Inspector Jane Connors claiming that they had only used it as "a last resort". She stated: "it's a valid tactic. Police officers came under attack and we needed to make sure the violence didn't spread out across the London streets."<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup> The police came under increasing criticism however, particularly as there were large numbers of children and young teenagers in the crowd, who were held for nearly ten hours in near-zero temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Battle_lines_45-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Battle_lines-45">[44]</a></sup> Green Party MP <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Lucas" title="Caroline Lucas">Caroline Lucas</a> brought up the topic in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> that afternoon, stating: "there are many hundreds of students and school children who have been kettled for over four hours and are going to be out there for another several hours, according to the police, in the freezing cold… whatever one thinks of the student protest, [holding people against their will in the contained crowd was] neither proportionate, nor, indeed, effective."<sup id="cite_ref-Second_day_40-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_day-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>At a meeting of the Metropolitan Police Authority the following day, the Metropolitan Police's Commissioner <a href="/wiki/Sir_Paul_Stephenson" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Paul Stephenson">Sir Paul Stephenson</a> defended the tactics. He condemned the protest, stating: "we have not seen this sort of behaviour for some considerable time… it was thuggery, it was disgraceful, [and] we are determined to find [those responsible for vandalism]." He further warned that "the likelihood is for more disorder on our streets. We must be prepared for it."<sup id="cite_ref-era_of_violence_42-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-era_of_violence-42">[41]</a></sup> He was criticised by <a href="/wiki/Jenny_Jones_(Green_politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jenny Jones (Green politician)">Jenny Jones</a>, a Green Party member of the <a href="/wiki/London_Assembly" title="London Assembly">London Assembly</a>, who told him: "when you imprison thousands of people, which is essentially what you did yesterday, you do have a duty of care to them... You kept people for nine-and-a-half hours. You punished innocent people for going on a protest."<sup id="cite_ref-Battle_lines_45-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Battle_lines-45">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>A spokesperson for Prime Minister Cameron stated that "people have a right to engage in lawful and peaceful protest, but there is no place for violence or intimidation",<sup id="cite_ref-students_stage_day_of_protests_39-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-students_stage_day_of_protests-39">[38]</a></sup> while education minister David Willetts responded by claiming that protesting students did not understand the government's plans.<sup id="cite_ref-students_stage_day_of_protests_39-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-students_stage_day_of_protests-39">[38]</a></sup> Clegg stated on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2" title="BBC Radio 2">BBC Radio 2</a> that "I hate in politics, as in life, to make promises that you then find you can't keep. We made a promise we can't deliver – we didn't win the election outright and there are compromises in coalition."<sup id="cite_ref-students_stage_day_of_protests_39-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-students_stage_day_of_protests-39">[38]</a></sup> Labour Party leader <a href="/wiki/Ed_Miliband" title="Ed Miliband">Ed Miliband</a> stated that he would not rule out joining further demonstrations, remarking that "I was quite tempted to go out and talk to them [the protesters]. Peaceful demonstrations are part of our society. As Labour leader I am willing to talk to people who are part of them."<sup id="cite_ref-Battle_lines_45-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Battle_lines-45">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="30_November">30 November</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 30 November">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_London_protests">Central London protests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Central London protests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:London_student_protest_30Nov.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/London_student_protest_30Nov.jpg/220px-London_student_protest_30Nov.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/London_student_protest_30Nov.jpg/330px-London_student_protest_30Nov.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/London_student_protest_30Nov.jpg/440px-London_student_protest_30Nov.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:London_student_protest_30Nov.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A number of protesters in central London on 30 November 2010</div>

<p>A third central London protest was organised for Tuesday 30 November, a day that saw cold temperatures and snow in the city. Protesters assembled at Trafalgar Square, but a police line prevented their march down Whitehall towards the Houses of Parliament; the NCAFC accused the Police of "pre-emptively block[ing]" the protest route.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-53">[52]</a></sup> Fearing that they would be kettled in the Square, protesters dispersed throughout the city center, pursued by police.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-54">[53]</a></sup> Other officers stood photographing and filming the students for later identification, while police vans blocked off certain streets.<sup id="cite_ref-SharpBBC_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SharpBBC-55">[54]</a></sup> Some protesters chanted "Peaceful protest! Peaceful protest!" and "no ifs, no buts, no education cuts", and others blew <a href="/wiki/Vuvuzela" title="Vuvuzela">vuvuzelas</a> and played <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a> from portable stereo systems.<sup id="cite_ref-SharpBBC_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SharpBBC-55">[54]</a></sup> A BBC reporter talked to members of the demonstration, noting that while many desired a peaceful outcome, others believed that violent confrontation with the police was inevitable. Many were angry with how they had been portrayed in the media, with one commenting that confrontation was not caused by "hardcore anarchists" but by "students who are very, very angry."<sup id="cite_ref-SharpBBC_55-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SharpBBC-55">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many protesters ran onto <a href="/wiki/Pall_Mall,_London" title="Pall Mall, London">Pall Mall</a> and then past <a href="/wiki/St._James%27s_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="St. James's Park">St. James's Park</a>. Denied access to Parliament Square by police, they turned around and headed toward <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SharpBBC_55-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SharpBBC-55">[54]</a></sup> A reporter from <i>The Guardian</i> noted that "the march is fracturing – people are going up different streets and getting lost. <a href="/wiki/Text_messaging" title="Text messaging">Texts</a> come through from the front, giving information." The reporter noted that police pursued protesters, and that "it feels like '<a href="/wiki/Kiss_chase" class="mw-redirect" title="Kiss chase">kiss chase</a>' – or, when I see a policeman punch a boy out of the way, entirely without provocation, 'punch chase'."<sup id="cite_ref-Tanya_Gold_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tanya_Gold-56">[55]</a></sup> Many protesters returned to Trafalgar Square, where they were still unable to march down to Whitehall due to the heavy police line.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-54">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>The police kettled those in the Square, whilst some of the protesters waved banners with slogans such as "Don't put the kettle on, Mr. Cameron" and "<a href="/wiki/I_Can%27t_Believe_It%27s_Not_Butter!" title="I Can't Believe It's Not Butter!">I Can't Believe It's Not</a> <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Thatcher</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Tanya_Gold_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tanya_Gold-56">[55]</a></sup> The police "put lines [of officers] across all the exits" to the kettled area of the Square, but reportedly allowed small groups of protesters to leave, even though the majority, around 150 to 200, decided to stay and continue protesting in the snow.<sup id="cite_ref-Standoff_BBC_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Standoff_BBC-57">[56]</a></sup> Some protesters burned placards and one spray-painted the word "Revolution" on <a href="/wiki/Nelson%27s_Column" title="Nelson's Column">Nelson's Column</a>. Others threw plastic bottles and <a href="/wiki/Fireworks" title="Fireworks">fireworks</a> at police lines, and at one point there "was a scuffle as a knot of policemen rushed one of the protesters, grabbing him to arrest him, and the crowd flocked angrily to the area."<sup id="cite_ref-Standoff_BBC_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Standoff_BBC-57">[56]</a></sup> At another time, a group of riot police moved into the crowd of protesters to attempt to secure Nelson's Column, only to be surrounded by demonstrators shouting "Who's kettling who? We're kettling you!"<sup id="cite_ref-SharpBBC_55-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SharpBBC-55">[54]</a></sup> Other slogans shouted at police during the protest included "Shame on you!" and "Your job's next".<sup id="cite_ref-SharpBBC_55-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SharpBBC-55">[54]</a></sup> Police then arrested 146 demonstrators who refused to leave the Square; 139 of them were arrested for <a href="/wiki/Breach_of_the_peace" title="Breach of the peace">breach of the peace</a>, whilst seven were arrested on suspicion of violent disorder.<sup id="cite_ref-google1_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google1-58">[57]</a></sup> Seven more had been arrested in central London earlier in the day.<sup id="cite_ref-google1_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google1-58">[57]</a></sup> As one reporter noted, "above us, on the steps of the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery" title="National Gallery">National Gallery</a>, tourists look confused at this vision of Britain 2010, angry and fighting in the snow."<sup id="cite_ref-Tanya_Gold_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tanya_Gold-56">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catford_protest">Catford protest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Catford protest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

Cambridge <a href="/wiki/Old_Schools" title="Old Schools">Old Schools</a> Combination Room during an eleven-day occupation.</div>

<p>The night before, on 28 November, a crowd of two to three hundred protesters gathered outside of <a href="/wiki/Lewisham" title="Lewisham">Lewisham</a> Town Hall in <a href="/wiki/Catford" title="Catford">Catford</a>, south London, where a council meeting was then in progress, to protest against wider public sector cuts. Several Youtube videos of the incident were shown on national news, including the BBC. Many of the protesters had come from nearby sixth forms (due to potential <a href="/wiki/Education_Maintenance_Allowance" title="Education Maintenance Allowance">Education Maintenance Allowance</a> cuts) and also from <a href="/wiki/Goldsmiths_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Goldsmiths College">Goldsmiths College</a>. Demonstrators, playing music and political slogans from <a href="/wiki/Boombox" title="Boombox">boomboxes</a> forced their way into the building, where a smoke bomb was let off, while another protester climbed onto the roof and unfurled a banner. Several more flares were set off outside and windows smashed, with riot police from the Territorial Support Group close by, were called and several arrests were made.<sup id="cite_ref-Lewisham_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lewisham-59">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rachel_H_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rachel_H-60">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LewishamBBC_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LewishamBBC-61">[60]</a></sup> One of the protesters, Sue Luxton, a former Green Party councillor who had subsequently become a teacher, told the press that "I wanted to peacefully express my anger at the cuts... People were angry that they couldn't get in."<sup id="cite_ref-LewishamBBC_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LewishamBBC-61">[60]</a></sup> Jeremy Burton, the Lewisham Borough Commander, later told press that "unfortunately due to the actions of a minority of people present a number of my officers were injured whilst carrying out their police duties", with 16 officers being treated for minor injuries.<sup id="cite_ref-Lewisham_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lewisham-59">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protests_elsewhere">Protests elsewhere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Protests elsewhere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the day of the main demonstration, there were also further protests across the United Kingdom, including in Cardiff, Cambridge, <a href="/wiki/Colchester" title="Colchester">Colchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a>, Leeds, <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>, Edinburgh, <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>, Brighton, York, Manchester, Plymouth, <a href="/wiki/Scunthorpe" title="Scunthorpe">Scunthorpe</a> and Bristol.<sup id="cite_ref-Standoff_BBC_57-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Standoff_BBC-57">[56]</a></sup> About 1,500 students, including school children, took part in the protest in Brighton,<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup> whilst protests in Bristol involved police being pelted with <a href="/wiki/Mustard_(condiment)" title="Mustard (condiment)">mustard</a> and ten demonstrators were arrested.<sup id="cite_ref-google1_58-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google1-58">[57]</a></sup> In Sheffield, police were pelted with snowballs as they guarded the constituency office of Nick Clegg from a crowd of two hundred protesters.<sup id="cite_ref-Police_Arrest_153_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Police_Arrest_153-63">[62]</a></sup> The British protests coincided with those in Italy, where demonstrations occurred in Milan, Turin, Naples, <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, Palermo, <a href="/wiki/Bari" title="Bari">Bari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a>, and Rome where riot police were called in to prevent students from gaining access to the parliament building.<sup id="cite_ref-TanyCBC_News_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TanyCBC_News-64">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, whilst occupations that had begun the previous week continued at University College London, <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_University" title="Newcastle University">Newcastle University</a> and the University of Cambridge,<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[64]</a></sup> a new one began at the University of Nottingham, where 150 protesters occupied a building.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-53">[52]</a></sup> University buildings and local government buildings were occupied in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> while police blocked an attempt at occupation of the council building in <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[65]</a></sup> The protesters occupying the council chamber in Birmingham left after four hours, with a police spokesperson commending the protesters for their "wholly peaceful" behaviour, and noting that it "couldn't have been more different from the violent clashes seen recently in London".<sup id="cite_ref-Police_Arrest_153_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Police_Arrest_153-63">[62]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BirminghamBBC_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BirminghamBBC-67">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="9_December">9 December</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: 9 December">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parliament_Square_protest">Parliament Square protest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Parliament Square protest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Protestors_in_london_climb_traffic_lights.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Protestors_in_london_climb_traffic_lights.jpg/220px-Protestors_in_london_climb_traffic_lights.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Protestors_in_london_climb_traffic_lights.jpg/330px-Protestors_in_london_climb_traffic_lights.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Protestors_in_london_climb_traffic_lights.jpg/440px-Protestors_in_london_climb_traffic_lights.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="683" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Protestors_in_london_climb_traffic_lights.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Protesters mount traffic lights in central London on 9 December 2010</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On Thursday 9 December, the day of the scheduled vote on education reform in the Houses of Parliament, two separate protests were organised in central London; one led by the <a href="/wiki/National_Union_of_Students_(United_Kingdom)" title="National Union of Students (United Kingdom)">National Union of Students</a> (NUS), the other jointly by the University of London Union (ULU) and the NCAFC, with an expected 40,000 people attending.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened-68">[67]</a></sup> ULU members handed out green <a href="/wiki/Hard_hats" class="mw-redirect" title="Hard hats">hard hats</a> with the words "Tax the banks, not the students" on them, whilst a rally was held in <a href="/wiki/Bloomsbury" title="Bloomsbury">Bloomsbury</a> at midday, where ULU President Solomon addressed the crowd.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened-68">[67]</a></sup> The Metropolitan Police had positioned lines of riot police and police vans along the Houses of Parliament, to prevent any protesters gaining access to it. Superintendent Julia Pendry issued a statement saying: "Protesters will be allowed sight and sound of parliament. However, there is evidence to suggest a number of people will come to London intent on causing violence and disorder. They are jumping on the bandwagon of these demonstrations with no intention to protest or interest in student tuition fees… those who are intent on committing crime will also be dealt with and they will suffer the consequences of their actions."<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened_68-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened-68">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>The protesters marched from Bloomsbury to Parliament Square in the afternoon, where they pushed down metal barriers and occupied the Square.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened_68-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened-68">[67]</a></sup> Around 3:30&#160;pm, police kettled those several thousand protesters in the square, preventing them from leaving, stating that it was necessary "due to the level of violence that our officers are facing."<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened_68-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened-68">[67]</a></sup> Mounted police charged into the crowd on one side of the Square in an attempt to disperse them. A field hospital was set up on the green providing emergency first aid to protesters as well as tea and food within the containment area. Around 30 protesters were treated, most for head injuries.<sup id="cite_ref-Observer_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Observer-69">[68]</a></sup> Police used <a href="/wiki/Baton_(law_enforcement)" title="Baton (law enforcement)">batons</a> to hit protesters, and a <a href="/wiki/St_John_Ambulance" class="mw-redirect" title="St John Ambulance">St John Ambulance</a> member told press that he had treated ten protesters for head injuries from being struck by police batons by 4.30&#160;pm.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened_68-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened-68">[67]</a></sup> One protester, philosophy student Alfie Meadows, suffered a blow to the head from a police truncheon that knocked him unconscious. Taken to hospital, it was discovered that he was suffering from bleeding of the brain, and required brain surgery.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC:_Alfie_Meadows_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC:_Alfie_Meadows-70">[69]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[nb 2]</a></sup> Reporter Jonathan Haynes of <i>The Guardian</i>, who was present, characterised the police tactics as "very heavy handed".<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened_68-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened-68">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>Police informed press that they were allowing young and vulnerable protesters to leave the kettle, but those inside the kettle, including journalists, asserted that this was untrue.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened_68-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened-68">[67]</a></sup> At 5:41&#160;pm, news reached the protesters that the government had voted to support the proposals. Clashes ensued between the crowd and the police, and the protesters pulled along metal fencing to separate themselves from riot police, who were trying to push them all into the centre of the Square.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened_68-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened-68">[67]</a></sup> Later on in the evening, with the protesters still kettled in the Square, masked protesters smashed all of the windows on the ground floor of <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Treasury" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty's Treasury">Her Majesty's Treasury</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC:_Heather_Sharp.2C_protesters_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC:_Heather_Sharp.2C_protesters-74">[72]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (July 2014)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> At 9:15&#160;pm the protest was forced onto Westminster Bridge where it was kettled until approximately 23:30.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protests_elsewhere_in_central_London">Protests elsewhere in central London</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Protests elsewhere in central London">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Transport_Police_riot_gear.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/British_Transport_Police_riot_gear.jpg/200px-British_Transport_Police_riot_gear.jpg" width="200" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/British_Transport_Police_riot_gear.jpg/300px-British_Transport_Police_riot_gear.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/British_Transport_Police_riot_gear.jpg/400px-British_Transport_Police_riot_gear.jpg 2x" data-file-width="768" data-file-height="1024" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Transport_Police_riot_gear.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/British_Transport_Police" title="British Transport Police">British Transport Police</a> officer deployed in riot gear on 9 December 2010</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Due to the Parliament Square protest being kettled, many other demonstrators could not enter the Square and so disseminated across much of the rest of central London. Some were separately kettled around <a href="/wiki/Cenotaph#The_Cenotaph.2C_London" title="Cenotaph">The Cenotaph</a>, where Charlie Gilmour, the adopted son of <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a> guitarist <a href="/wiki/David_Gilmour" title="David Gilmour">David Gilmour</a> and a student at <a href="/wiki/Girton_College,_Cambridge" title="Girton College, Cambridge">Girton College, Cambridge</a>, was pictured swinging from a <a href="/wiki/Union_Flag" class="mw-redirect" title="Union Flag">Union Flag</a> on the memorial; he later apologised, claiming that he "did not realise" it was the Cenotaph.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC:_David_Gilmour.27s_son_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC:_David_Gilmour.27s_son-75">[73]</a></sup> Gilmour was also photographed attempting to start a fire at the Supreme Court; and tossing a lump of concrete while wearing latex gloves.<sup id="cite_ref-DailyMail_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DailyMail-76">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meanwhile, many of those students who remained around the area of Trafalgar Square continued to protest, with about 150 holding a sit-in in the adjacent National Gallery, while others attempted to set fire to the <a href="/wiki/Trafalgar_Square_Christmas_tree" title="Trafalgar Square Christmas tree">Trafalgar Square Christmas tree</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC:_Royal_car_attacked_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC:_Royal_car_attacked-77">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the protests, a car taking <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">The Prince of Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Camilla,_Duchess_of_Cornwall" title="Camilla, Duchess of Cornwall">The Duchess of Cornwall</a> to the evening's <a href="/wiki/Royal_Variety_Performance" title="Royal Variety Performance">Royal Variety Performance</a> at the <a href="/wiki/London_Palladium" title="London Palladium">London Palladium</a> was attacked on <a href="/wiki/Regent_Street" title="Regent Street">Regent Street</a><sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[76]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[77]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Home_Secretary" title="Home Secretary">Home Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/Theresa_May" title="Theresa May">Theresa May</a> confirmed a protester 'made contact' with Camilla.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[78]</a></sup> In Oxford Street, <a href="/wiki/Topshop" title="Topshop">Topshop</a> was damaged, as rioters sprayed "pay your tax" on the building and broke windows.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[79]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Response_2">Response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_United_Kingdom_student_protests&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Home Secretary Theresa May issued a statement in which she "utterly condemned" the actions of the protesters, and declared that "What we are seeing in London tonight, the wanton vandalism, smashing of windows, has nothing to do with peaceful protest... Attacks on police officers and property show that some of the protesters have no respect for London or its citizens."<sup id="cite_ref-BBC:_Royal_car_attacked_77-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC:_Royal_car_attacked-77">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nearly 50 people complained to the IPCC about police behaviour during the various protests held around the country, with the majority directed against the Metropolitan Police, including complaints of violence used against protesters.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p><span id="Jody_McIntyre"></span> Following the protests, video footage of Jody McIntyre, who has <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_palsy" title="Cerebral palsy">cerebral palsy</a>, being pulled out of his wheelchair by police, who claimed they were acting in the interest of his own safety (he had positioned himself to the front-line facing the police) was posted to YouTube. The footage showed him being pulled out of his wheelchair and dragged across the ground by officers from the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Yard#The_Metropolitan_Police" title="Scotland Yard">Metropolitan Police</a> during the protests.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[81]</a></sup> McIntyre said he had been pulled out of his wheelchair twice; only one incident was shown in the video footage. An interview with McIntyre about the incident by BBC journalist <a href="/wiki/Ben_Brown_(journalist)" title="Ben Brown (journalist)">Ben Brown</a> on 13 December 2010 was described by <i>The Guardian</i> newspaper as "having a distinct lack of sympathy from the BBC" and that the incident had "attracted thousands of complaints."<sup id="cite_ref-kira_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kira-84">[82]</a></sup> Mcintyre's complaint was later rejected by Scotland Yard.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[83]</a></sup> His subsequent appeal was partially upheld by the IPCC.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>Street medics treated student protesters during the parliament square protest on Thursday 9 December, the day of the scheduled vote to raise university tuition fees. A field tent was set up on the green providing emergency first aid to protesters as well as tea and food within the containment area. Around 30 protesters were treated, most for head injuries.<sup id="cite_ref-Observer_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Observer-69">[68]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 30 November, following the third main day of protesting, the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Assembly">Welsh Assembly</a> announced that it would not permit an increase in fees for Welsh students. A reporter from the BBC noted that this meant that if the plans went through in England, "it would mean that an English student at a university in England could pay more than £17,000 more for a three-year degree than a Welsh student on the same course." .<sup id="cite_ref-Standoff_BBC_57-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Standoff_BBC-57">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>A writer in British newspaper <i>The Guardian</i>, writing several hours before the government vote on the topic, noted that "It seems likely the tuition fees bill will pass but I'd still argue that – whatever your view on the merits of the new fees system – the protests have been a success at least in calling politicians to account for broken pledges, something you see rarely theses [sic] days."<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened_68-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian:_Student_protests.2C_as_they_happened-68">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2011, three school children will challenge the kettling of children at the 24 November 2010 protest. They will seek a Judicial Review in the High Court, arguing in particular that children had a right to protest and that their safety was jeopardised, breaking the laws of the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Rights_of_the_Child" title="Convention on the Rights of the Child">United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Children_Act_2004" title="Children Act 2004">Children Act 2004</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2012, two years after the 2010 demonstrations, protests ignited again from the student movement. Organised by the NUS, around 10,000 demonstrators marched in central London.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p>After a five-day trial in the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court</a> in June 2012, 27-year-old assistant tutor Luke Cooper, reported to be completing a PhD in <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international relations</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Sussex" title="University of Sussex">University of Sussex</a>, was awarded £35,000 over a front page <i><a href="/wiki/Evening_Standard" class="mw-redirect" title="Evening Standard">Evening Standard</a></i> article and £25,000 over a follow-up piece in the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mail" title="Daily Mail">Daily Mail</a></i> that implied he was the "ringleader" of the protesters who invaded the Conservative Party's headquarters. Cooper complained that the allegations were untrue, threatened his future academic prospects and left his reputation "as badly trashed" as the <a href="/wiki/Millbank_Tower" title="Millbank Tower">Millbank Tower</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[87]</a></sup></p>
