<p>The <b>Léopoldville Riots</b> were an outbreak of civil disorder in <a href="/wiki/Kinshasa" title="Kinshasa">Léopoldville</a> (modern-day Kinshasa) in the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Congo" title="Belgian Congo">Belgian Congo</a> which took place in early 1959 and which were an important moment for the Congolese independence movement. The rioting occurred when members of the <i><a href="/wiki/ABAKO" title="ABAKO">Alliance des Bakongo</a></i> (ABAKO) political party were not allowed to assemble for a protest and colonial authorities reacted harshly. The exact death toll is not known, but at least 49 people were killed and total casualties may have been as high as 500. The Congo received its independence on 30 June 1960, becoming the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_Congo_(L%C3%A9opoldville)" title="Republic of the Congo (Léopoldville)">Republic of the Congo</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Riots">Riots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%A9opoldville_Riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Riots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Joseph_Kasa-Vubu_in_Israel.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Joseph_Kasa-Vubu_in_Israel.png/170px-Joseph_Kasa-Vubu_in_Israel.png" width="170" height="199" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Joseph_Kasa-Vubu_in_Israel.png/255px-Joseph_Kasa-Vubu_in_Israel.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/82/Joseph_Kasa-Vubu_in_Israel.png 2x" data-file-width="293" data-file-height="343" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Joseph_Kasa-Vubu_in_Israel.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
ABAKO leader <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Kasa-Vubu" title="Joseph Kasa-Vubu">Joseph Kasa-Vubu</a> who played an important role in the disorder and became the Congo's first post-independent President</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Calls for Congolese independence had been building for several years and a slew of new political parties competed for popular support, including the <i><a href="/wiki/ABAKO" title="ABAKO">Alliance des Bakongo</a></i> (ABAKO), led by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Kasa-Vubu" title="Joseph Kasa-Vubu">Joseph Kasa-Vubu</a> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Mouvement_National_Congolais" title="Mouvement National Congolais">Mouvement National Congolais</a></i> (MNC), led by <a href="/wiki/Patrice_Lumumba" title="Patrice Lumumba">Patrice Lumumba</a>. On 28 December 1958, Lumumba organized a major MNC rally in Kinshasa where he reported on his attendance of the <a href="/wiki/All-African_Peoples%27_Conference" title="All-African Peoples' Conference">All-African Peoples' Conference</a> in <a href="/wiki/Accra" title="Accra">Accra</a>, Ghana earlier that month. Noting the success of the rally, Kasa-Vubu decided to organize his own event one week later, on Sunday January 4, 1959.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENzongola-Ntalaja200284-6_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENzongola-Ntalaja200284-6-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kasa-Vubu was set to address the crowd on <a href="/wiki/African_nationalism" title="African nationalism">African nationalism</a>, but when the group requested permission to hold the meeting at the <a href="/wiki/YMCA" title="YMCA">Young Men's Christian Association</a> (YMCA) Belgian officials warned that if the event became political, ABAKO leaders would be held responsible.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReuters1969_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReuters1969-2">[2]</a></sup> Interpreting this as a prohibition of the meeting, ABAKO leadership attempted on 3 January to postpone the event, but on Sunday, 4 January, a large crowd gathered at the YMCA anyway.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENzongola-Ntalaja200284-6_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENzongola-Ntalaja200284-6-1">[1]</a></sup> Kasa-Vubu and other ABAKO officials arrived to send the protesters home. They were unable to calm the crowd,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReuters1969_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReuters1969-2">[2]</a></sup> and the violence began following the protesters refusal to disperse.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGilroy1959a_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGilroy1959a-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The crowd began throwing rocks at police and attacking white motorists, before the conflagration spread. The initial group of protesters were soon joined by 20,000 Congolese leaving a nearby soccer stadium.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENzongola-Ntalaja200284-6_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENzongola-Ntalaja200284-6-1">[1]</a></sup> At the time press accounts estimated that 35,000 Africans were involved in the violence, which quickly spread as the rioters attempted to enter the European section of the capital.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAP1959_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAP1959-4">[4]</a></sup> Rioters allegedly smashed and looted storefronts, burned Catholic missions and beat Catholic priests. Order was restored with the use of African police officers in the employ of the colonial government and with the use of armored cars.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAP1959_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAP1959-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Colonial authorities arrested as many as 300 Congolese, including Kasa-Vubu, who would later become the newly-independent Congo's first president, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_Mzeza&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Simon Mzeza (page does not exist)">Simon Mzeza</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGilroy1959b_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGilroy1959b-5">[5]</a></sup> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Kanza&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Daniel Kanza (page does not exist)">Daniel Kanza</a> the vice-president of ABAKO, and charged them with inciting the riot.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAP1959_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAP1959-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Result">Result</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%A9opoldville_Riots&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Result">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Estimates of the riot's final death toll vary, but estimates of total casualties range from 49 to as high as 500.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZeilig200870_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZeilig200870-6">[6]</a></sup> More than just a significant loss of life, the January riots marked a turning point in the Congolese liberation movement, forcing colonial and Belgian authorities to acknowledge that serious issues existed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESwarthmore2012_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESwarthmore2012-7">[7]</a></sup> The event, which is believed to have been spontaneous and featured crowds chanting "<i>indépendance immédiate</i>", a shocking public sentiment.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENzongola-Ntalaja200284-6_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENzongola-Ntalaja200284-6-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>In the immediate aftermath, Belgian authorities laid blame on unemployed Africans, but claimed the majority of the city's 250,000 African residents were not involved.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGilroy1959a_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGilroy1959a-3">[3]</a></sup> However, within days, Belgian authorities began to move quickly to put into place reforms that would offer Congolese more say in their own government, including the announcement of elections in December 1959.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReuters1959a_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReuters1959a-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>4 January is now celebrated as a public holiday in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, known as <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_the_Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Public holidays in the Democratic Republic of the Congo">Day of the Martyrs</a>. The events marked the radicalization of the independence movement and are often considered to be the "death knell" for Belgian control of the Congo.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENzongola-Ntalaja200284-6_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENzongola-Ntalaja200284-6-1">[1]</a></sup> This radicalization occurred on both sides, with a Congolese group signalling a willingness to use violence to achieve independence for the first time<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENzongola-Ntalaja200284-6_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENzongola-Ntalaja200284-6-1">[1]</a></sup> as well as many in the white community also becoming increasingly prepared for violence. Some whites planned to attempt a <i><a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">coup d'état</a></i> if a black majority government took power.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZeilig200870_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZeilig200870-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The riots also marked a period of rising tension and a break for the <i><a href="/wiki/Mouvement_National_Congolais" title="Mouvement National Congolais">Mouvement National Congolais</a></i> (MNC), the main political rivals for the ABAKO. Starting with the unrest in January, both of the nationalist parties' influence expanded outside the major cities for the first time, and nationalist demonstrations and riots became a regular occurrence over the next year,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReuters1959b_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReuters1959b-9">[9]</a></sup> bringing large numbers of black people from outside the <i>évolué</i> class into the independence movement. Because the bulk of the ABAKO leadership was arrested, leaving the MNC in an advantageous position.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZeilig200870.E2.80.9373_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZeilig200870.E2.80.9373-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
