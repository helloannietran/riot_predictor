<p><b>Hagåtña</b> (pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[həˈɡɑtɲə]</a></span> ), formerly English <b>Agana</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> and in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> <b>Agaña</b>, is the capital of the United States territory of <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a>. From the 18th through mid 20th century, it was Guam's population center, but today it is the second smallest of the island's 19 <a href="/wiki/Villages_of_Guam" class="mw-redirect" title="Villages of Guam">villages</a> in both area and population. However, it remains one of the island's major <a href="/wiki/Commercial_district" title="Commercial district">commercial districts</a> in addition to being the seat of government.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hag%C3%A5t%C3%B1a,_Guam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Hagåt" (also <a href="/wiki/Romanized" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanized">romanized</a> as <i>haga'</i>, with a glottal stop instead of a syllable-final "t") means "blood" in the <a href="/wiki/Chamorro_language" title="Chamorro language">Chamorro</a> language. The suffix "-ña" can be translated as either the possessive pronouns <i>his</i>, <i>hers</i> or <i>its</i> in English, or a signification of greater comparative degree, similar to some uses of the English suffix "-er". There is much speculation that the natives originally migrated from the village of Agat/<a href="/wiki/Hag%C3%A5t" class="mw-redirect" title="Hagåt">Hagåt</a>. Therefore, "Hagåtña" can be translated "his or her blood" possibly meaning "related to him, her or it", or it could be translated to what might roughly mean "more Hagåt", as in, an extension of the village of Hagåt. It could also mean "better Hagåt", or "more than, surpassing or superior to Hagåt" in a sense of being "more Hagåt than Hagåt itself". In 1998, the <a href="/wiki/Legislature_of_Guam" title="Legislature of Guam">Guam Legislature</a> changed the name from "Agana" back to the original Chamorro/Chamoru form. However, the name of the neighboring village <a href="/wiki/Agana_Heights" class="mw-redirect" title="Agana Heights">Agana Heights</a>, remains unchanged.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hag%C3%A5t%C3%B1a,_Guam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hagåtña is located at the mouth of the Hagåtña River on Guam's west coast. According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the city has a total area of 1 square mile (2.6&#160;km²). The village is bounded by the sandy beaches of Agana Bay to the north, the Agana River and associated wetlands to the east, and a cliff (above which is the village of Agana Heights) to the South. Several high-rise office buildings are in the center of the village, while the western portion of the city known as <b>Anigua</b> is more residential. Unlike many villages, central Hagåtña is divided into city blocks with shops and small restaurants throughout the center of the village. Highly populated residential areas in the villages of <a href="/wiki/Mongmong-Toto-Maite" class="mw-redirect" title="Mongmong-Toto-Maite">Mongmong-Toto-Maite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sinajana,_Guam" title="Sinajana, Guam">Sinajana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Agana_Heights,_Guam" title="Agana Heights, Guam">Agana Heights</a> surround Hagåtña.</p>

<p>Hagåtña was a prominent village before Guam's <a href="/wiki/Colonization" title="Colonization">colonization</a> by the Spanish. In 1668, the first Spanish <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionary</a>, Padre <a href="/wiki/San_Vitores" class="mw-redirect" title="San Vitores">San Vitores</a> arrived on the island. The family of <a href="/wiki/Chief_Kepuha" title="Chief Kepuha">Chief Kepuha</a> donated land in Hagåtña enabling San Vitores to build the first church (Dulce Nombre de Maria Cathedral-Basilica) on Guam.</p>
<p>Under Spanish rule, much of the indigenous population of Guam and other <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Islands" title="Mariana Islands">Mariana Islands</a> was forced to relocate to the city. The remains of buildings from the Spanish administration can be seen in the Plaza de España located beside the cathedral of the <a href="/wiki/Archdiocese" class="mw-redirect" title="Archdiocese">Archdiocese</a> of Agana. The remains of the Spanish Governor's Palace is here and is closer to the Department of Education than the Cathedral.</p>

<p>After Guam was ceded by <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a> of 1898, 'Agana' remained the seat of government under U.S. Naval Administration. By 1940, the city's population had grown to about 10,000 containing nearly half of the island's residents. Villages had been established nearby for immigrants from the <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Islands" title="Caroline Islands">Caroline Islands</a>.</p>
<p>Guam was captured by <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> forces on December 8, 1941. During Guam's 1944 liberation from the Japanese during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the city was heavily damaged by U.S. naval bombardment. Many former residents settled in other parts of Guam after the war. As part of Guam's reconstruction plan, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> constructed new straight city streets that passed through existing lots and created many plots of land with multiple owners. This has hindered the development of the city to the present day. In December 1944 Guam was the scene of the <a href="/wiki/Agana_race_riot" title="Agana race riot">Agana race riot</a>, between black and white servicemen stationed on the island.</p>
<p>Today, despite a resident population of only 1,100 (less than 1% of Guam's total), the city remains the seat of the territorial government. Its historic sites are major attractions for visitors. Hagåtña is served by <a href="/wiki/Antonio_B._Won_Pat_International_Airport" title="Antonio B. Won Pat International Airport">Antonio B. Won Pat International Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tamuning" class="mw-redirect" title="Tamuning">Tamuning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barrigada" class="mw-redirect" title="Barrigada">Barrigada</a>.</p>

<p>As Guam's historic population and administrative center, many traditional celebrations take place in Hagåtña. On December 8, <a href="/wiki/Blessed_Virgin_Mary" class="mw-redirect" title="Blessed Virgin Mary">Santa Marian</a> Kamalen, Patroness of the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Islands" title="Mariana Islands">Mariana Islands</a>, is honored by a procession where a statue of the patroness is pulled on a cart amid the prayers of thousands of the island's <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholics</a> <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://ns.gov.gu/kamalen.html">[1]</a>. Guam's most celebrated patriotic holiday, Liberation Day is on July 21. The annual Liberation Day Parade takes place on Marine Corps Drive in Hagåtña. In addition to the historic sites at the Plaza de España and the Basilica, <a href="/wiki/Latte_stone" title="Latte stone">Latte Stone</a> Park and the Chamorro Village shopping area offer further information about the island's history and culture.</p>

<p>The island's capital, the <a href="/wiki/Legislature_of_Guam" title="Legislature of Guam">legislature</a>, the governor's office and other government offices are located in Hagåtña. The Government House (<a href="/wiki/Chamorro_language" title="Chamorro language">Chamorro</a>: <span lang="ch" xml:lang="ch"><i>Guma I Taotao Guam</i></span>), traditionally the governor's official residence, is situated above the cliff but technically within the city limit of Hagåtña. <a href="/wiki/Adelup" title="Adelup">Adelup</a>, now home of the <b>Ricardo J. Bordallo Governor’s Complex</b> since 1990, was once part of <a href="/wiki/Asan,_Guam" title="Asan, Guam">Asan-Maina</a> before being annexed into Hagåtña so that Hagåtña remains the seat of the Government of Guam.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Guam_Department_of_Corrections" title="Guam Department of Corrections">Guam Department of Corrections</a> operates the Hagåtña Detention Facility in Hagåtña.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Notable federal government agencies in Hagåtña include the <a href="/wiki/District_Court_of_Guam" title="District Court of Guam">District Court of Guam</a> at 520 West Soledad Avenue,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Attorney_for_the_Districts_of_Guam_and_the_Northern_Mariana_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Attorney for the Districts of Guam and the Northern Mariana Islands">United States Attorney</a> at Sirena Plaza, 108 Hernan Cortez, Suite 500,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a> Post Office at 223 West Chalan Santo Papa.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
