<p>The <b>Newport Rising</b> was the last large-scale armed rebellion against authority in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> , and one of the largest civil massacres committed by the British government in the 19th century, when, on 4 November 1839, almost 10,000<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chartism" title="Chartism">Chartist</a> sympathisers, led by <a href="/wiki/John_Frost_(Chartist)" title="John Frost (Chartist)">John Frost</a>, marched on the town of <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Monmouthshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Newport, Monmouthshire">Newport, Monmouthshire</a>. The men, including many coal-miners, most with home-made arms, were intent on liberating fellow Chartists who were reported to have been taken prisoner in the town's <a href="/wiki/Westgate_Hotel" title="Westgate Hotel">Westgate Hotel</a>. About 22 demonstrators were killed when troops opened fire on them. The leaders of the <a href="/wiki/Rebellion" title="Rebellion">rebellion</a> were convicted of <a href="/wiki/High_treason" title="High treason">high treason</a> and were sentenced to a <a href="/wiki/Hanged,_drawn_and_quartered" title="Hanged, drawn and quartered">traitor's death</a>. The sentence was later commuted to <a href="/wiki/Penal_transportation" title="Penal transportation">transportation for life</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newport_Rising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origins of <a href="/wiki/Chartism_in_Wales" title="Chartism in Wales">Chartism in Wales</a> can be traced to the foundation in the autumn of 1836 of Carmarthen Working Men's Association.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among the factors that precipitated the rising were the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a>' rejection of the first Chartist petition, the <a href="/wiki/Chartism#People.27s_Charter_of_1838" title="Chartism">People's Charter of 1838</a>, on 12 July 1839, and the conviction of the Chartist <a href="/wiki/Henry_Vincent" title="Henry Vincent">Henry Vincent</a> for <a href="/wiki/Unlawful_assembly" title="Unlawful assembly">unlawful assembly</a> and conspiracy on 2 August.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some kind of rising had been in preparation for a few months and the march had been gathering momentum over the course of the whole weekend, as John Frost and his associates led the protesters down from the industrialised valley towns to the north of Newport. Some of the miners who joined the march had armed themselves with home-made <a href="/wiki/Pike_(weapon)" title="Pike (weapon)">pikes</a>, bludgeons and <a href="/wiki/Firearms" class="mw-redirect" title="Firearms">firearms</a>.</p>
<p>The march was headed by Frost leading a column into Newport from the west, <a href="/wiki/Zephaniah_Williams" title="Zephaniah Williams">Zephaniah Williams</a> leading a column from <a href="/wiki/Blackwood,_Monmouthshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackwood, Monmouthshire">Blackwood</a> to the northwest and <a href="/wiki/William_Jones_(Chartist)" title="William Jones (Chartist)">William Jones</a> leading a column from <a href="/wiki/Pontypool" title="Pontypool">Pontypool</a> to the north.</p>
<p>The exact rationale for the confrontation remains opaque, although it may have its origins in Frost's ambivalence towards the more violent attitudes of some Chartists, and the personal animus he bore towards some of the Newport establishment. The Chartist movement in southeast Wales was chaotic in this period and the feelings of the workers were running extremely high.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events_leading_up_to_the_Rising">Events leading up to the Rising</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newport_Rising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Events leading up to the Rising">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Heavy rainfall delayed the marchers and there were delays in the planned meeting of each contingent at the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Oak_(pub)" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Oak (pub)">Welsh Oak</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rogerstone" title="Rogerstone">Rogerstone</a>. Jones and his men from Pontypool in fact never arrived, delaying the final march into Newport into the daylight hours and thus contributing to its defeat. As the march progressed down the valleys on the Sunday morning, even one entire chapel congregation was pressed into swelling the ranks of the marchers.</p>
<p>After spending Sunday night mostly out of doors in the rain, the commitment of many of the marchers was lukewarm. Many had been ambivalent to the Chartist cause in the first place, more concerned with the immediate problems of their own employment conditions. Thus many marchers did not participate in the final assault on Newport and simply waited in the outskirts of the town.</p>

<p>Rumours of a possible Chartist rising and previous violence elsewhere, following the earlier arrest of Chartist leader <a href="/wiki/Henry_Vincent" title="Henry Vincent">Henry Vincent</a> and his imprisonment at the gaol in <a href="/wiki/Monmouth" title="Monmouth">Monmouth</a>, meant that the authorities had suspected there might be a riot. The sheer scale of the rising, however was not fully appreciated until November 3, the day before the riot. The authorities then quickly started to prepare. The <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_Newport" title="Mayor of Newport">Mayor of Newport</a> <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Phillips_(mayor)" title="Thomas Phillips (mayor)">Thomas Phillips</a> had sworn in 500 Special Constables and asked for more troops to be sent. There were about 60 soldiers stationed in Newport already, and he gathered 32 soldiers of the <a href="/wiki/45th_(Nottinghamshire)_Regiment_of_Foot" class="mw-redirect" title="45th (Nottinghamshire) Regiment of Foot">45th (Nottinghamshire) Regiment of Foot</a> in the Westgate Hotel where the Chartist prisoners were held.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climax_of_the_Rising">Climax of the Rising</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newport_Rising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climax of the Rising">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Chartists were convinced that some of their fellows had been imprisoned at the Westgate Hotel. Filing quickly down the steep Stow Hill, the Chartists arrived at the small square in front of the hotel at about 9.30 a.m. The flash point came when the crowd demanded the release of the imprisoned Chartists. A brief, violent and bloody battle ensued. Shots were fired by both sides, contemporary accounts indicating that the Chartists attacked first. But the soldiers defending the hotel despite being greatly outnumbered by the large and very angry crowd, had vastly superior firepower, training and discipline, all of which soon broke the crowd. The Chartists did manage to enter the building temporarily, but were forced to retreat in disarray. After a fiercely fought battle, lasting approximately half an hour, between 10 and 24 of their number (a fair estimate is 22) had been killed by the troops and upwards of 50 had been wounded.</p>
<p>Amongst the defenders of the hotel, Mayor Thomas Phillips was badly wounded and one soldier was seriously hurt, along with two of the special constables.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As the chartists fled they abandoned many of their weapons, a selection of which can still be seen in <a href="/wiki/Newport_Museum" title="Newport Museum">Newport Museum</a>.</p>
<p>An eye-witness report spoke of one man, wounded with gunshot, lying on the ground, pleading for help until he died an hour later.</p>
<p>Some of the Chartist dead were buried in St Woolos parish church (now <a href="/wiki/Newport_Cathedral" title="Newport Cathedral">Newport Cathedral</a>) in the town where there is still a plaque to their memory. Some of the bullet holes from the skirmish remained in the masonry of the hotel entrance porch until well into modern times.</p>

<p>In the aftermath 200 or more Chartists were arrested for being involved and twenty-one were charged with <a href="/wiki/High_treason" title="High treason">high treason</a>. All three main leaders of the march, John Frost, Zephaniah Williams, and <a href="/wiki/William_Jones_(Chartist)" title="William Jones (Chartist)">William Jones</a>, were found guilty on the charge of high treason and were sentenced at the <a href="/wiki/Shire_Hall,_Monmouth" title="Shire Hall, Monmouth">Shire Hall</a> in <a href="/wiki/Monmouth" title="Monmouth">Monmouth</a> to be <a href="/wiki/Hanged,_drawn_and_quartered" title="Hanged, drawn and quartered">hanged, drawn and quartered</a>. They were to be the last people to be sentenced to this punishment in England and Wales.</p>
<p>After a nationwide petitioning campaign and, extraordinarily, direct lobbying of the Home Secretary by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chief_Justice" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Chief Justice">Lord Chief Justice</a> the government eventually commuted the sentences of each to <a href="/wiki/Penal_transportation" title="Penal transportation">transportation for life</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Other Chartists involved in some way included <a href="/wiki/James_Stephens_(trade_unionist)" title="James Stephens (trade unionist)">James Stephens</a>, John Lovell, <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Rees_(Chartist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John Rees (Chartist) (page does not exist)">John Rees</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Price_(doctor)" class="mw-redirect" title="William Price (doctor)">William Price</a>, and according to some accounts <a href="/wiki/Allan_Pinkerton" title="Allan Pinkerton">Allan Pinkerton</a>.</p>
<p>Testimonies exist from contemporaries, such as the Yorkshire Chartist Ben Wilson, that a successful rising at Newport was to have been the signal for a national uprising. Older histories suggested that Chartism slipped into a period of internal division after Newport. In fact the movement was remarkably buoyant (and remained so until late 1842). Initially, while the majority of Chartists, under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Feargus_O%27Connor" title="Feargus O'Connor">Feargus O'Connor</a>, concentrated on petitioning for Frost, Williams and Jones to be pardoned, significant minorities in <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_End_of_London" title="East End of London">East End of London</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a> planned their own risings in response. <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Holberry" title="Samuel Holberry">Samuel Holberry</a> led an aborted rising in Sheffield on 12 January; police action thwarted a major disturbance in the East End of London on 14 January, and on 26 January a few hundred Bradford Chartists staged a rising in the hope of precipitating a domino effect across the country.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> After this Chartism turned to a process of internal renewal and more systematic organisation, but the transported and imprisoned Newport Chartists were regarded as heroes and martyrs amongst workers.</p>
<p>Meanwhile <a href="/wiki/The_Establishment" title="The Establishment">the Establishment</a> and middle classes became convinced that the rising meant all Chartists were dangerously violent. Newport Mayor Thomas Phillips was proclaimed a national hero for his part in crushing the rising and was knighted by <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> barely six weeks later.</p>
<p>Frost himself was eventually given an unconditional pardon in 1856 and allowed to return to Britain, receiving a triumphant welcome in Newport.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> But he never lived in Newport again, settling instead in <a href="/wiki/Stapleton,_Bristol" title="Stapleton, Bristol">Stapleton</a> near <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, where he continued to publish articles advocating reform until his death, aged 93, in 1877.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commemoration">Commemoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newport_Rising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Commemoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Interest in the Newport Rising was kept alive through occasional articles in the <i>Monmouthshire Merlin</i> and <i>South Wales Argus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1939, to commemorate the centenary of the Rising, Newport Borough Council erected a plaque on the Post Office building near the birthplace of John Frost. In the 1960s, redevelopment of Newport led to the creation of a central square which was named John Frost Square. The Chartist mural (see below) was situated in an underpass leading to the square. Newport Museum has a display relating to the uprising which includes homemade weapons. In 1991 three statues, 'Union, Prudence, Energy' by Christopher Kelly, commemorating the uprising were installed on Commercial Street at the front of the Westgate Hotel. In 2015 it was announced that Duffryn High School was to be renamed John Frost School. An annual 'Chartist Convention' is held in the city.</p>
<p>Commemorative sites in other communities includes a 26&#160;ft tall statue of a Chartist marcher designed by Sebastian Boyson, erected by Caerphilly County Borough Council near the Chartist Bridge at Blackwood. The Shire Hall in Monmouth, scene of the Chartist trial in 1840, has a preserved courtroom and displays relating to the trial. A plaque commemorating the departure of Frost, Williams and Jones from Chepstow by ship to Portsmouth on the first stage of their transportation voyage is situated on the Chepstow river front.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="John_Frost_Square_mural">John Frost Square mural</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newport_Rising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: John Frost Square mural">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chartist_Mural" title="Chartist Mural">Chartist Mural</a></div>
<p>In the 1960s, as part of a redevelopment scheme, a new square was named <a href="/wiki/John_Frost_Square" title="John Frost Square">John Frost Square</a> to commemorate the leader of the Rising,<sup id="cite_ref-documenting_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-documenting-10">[10]</a></sup> and in 1978 a 35 metres (115&#160;ft) long <a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">mosaic</a> mural, by <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Budd" title="Kenneth Budd">Kenneth Budd</a>, was created in a <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian_underpass" class="mw-redirect" title="Pedestrian underpass">pedestrian underpass</a> in the square. In 2007, an introductory panel was removed, and it was proposed that, as part of a further redevelopment scheme, the mural would be removed.<sup id="cite_ref-documenting_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-documenting-10">[10]</a></sup> Proposals to demolish the mural were restated in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_March_2012_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_March_2012-11">[11]</a></sup> Despite a campaign to protect the mural, the council's contractors demolished it on 3 October 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A trust is to be set up to commission a new memorial with £50,000 of funding provided by <a href="/wiki/Newport_City_Council" title="Newport City Council">Newport City Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newport_Rising&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In literature, the events of the Rising are portrayed in <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Cordell" title="Alexander Cordell">Alexander Cordell</a>'s 1959 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Rape_of_the_Fair_Country" title="Rape of the Fair Country">Rape of the Fair Country</a></i>. the novel <i>Children of Rebecca</i> by Vivien Annis Bailey, a novel published by Honno Press in 1995, also features the Newport Rising.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The 2016 <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">ITV</a> biographical TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Victoria_(TV_series)" title="Victoria (TV series)">Victoria</a></i> offers a fictionalized account of the Newport Rising in which <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> is depicted as ordering the drawing and quartering of the ringleaders to be commuted to <a href="/wiki/Deportation" title="Deportation">deportation</a> to Australia, after learning that one of the men is the brother of a member of her household staff. The commutation is intended as a signal to the people that her reign is to be a merciful one.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
