<p><b>Córdoba</b> (<small>Spanish pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[ˈkorðoβa]</a></span>) is a city located in the geographical center of <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, in the foothills of the <a href="/wiki/Punilla_Valley" title="Punilla Valley">Sierras Chicas</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Primero_River" title="Primero River">Suquía River</a>, about 700&#160;km (435&#160;mi) northwest of the <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Autonomous City of Buenos Aires</a>. It is the capital of <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba_Province,_Argentina" title="Córdoba Province, Argentina">Córdoba Province</a> and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_in_Argentina_by_population" title="List of cities in Argentina by population">second most populous</a> city in Argentina after Buenos Aires, with about 1.3&#160;million inhabitants according to the 2001 census. It was founded on 6 July 1573 by <a href="/wiki/Jer%C3%B3nimo_Luis_de_Cabrera" title="Jerónimo Luis de Cabrera">Jerónimo Luis de Cabrera</a>, who named it after <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Córdoba, Spain">Córdoba, Spain</a>. It was one of the first Spanish colonial capitals of the region that is now Argentina (the oldest city is <a href="/wiki/Santiago_del_Estero" title="Santiago del Estero">Santiago del Estero</a>, founded in 1553). The <a href="/wiki/National_University_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="National University of Córdoba">National University of Córdoba</a> is the oldest university of the country and the second to be inaugurated in <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>. It was founded in 1613 by the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Jesuit Order</a>. Because of this, Córdoba earned the nickname <i>La Docta</i> (roughly translated, "the learned one").</p>
<p>Córdoba has many historical monuments preserved from <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">Spanish colonial rule</a>, especially buildings of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>. The most recognizable is perhaps the <a href="/wiki/Jesuit_Block" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesuit Block">Jesuit Block</a> (Spanish: <i>Manzana Jesuítica</i>), declared in 2000 as a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> by UNESCO<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> which consists of a group of buildings dating from the 17th century, including the <a href="/wiki/Colegio_Nacional_de_Monserrat" title="Colegio Nacional de Monserrat">Colegio Nacional de Monserrat</a> and the colonial university campus. The campus belongs today to the historical museum of the <a href="/wiki/National_University_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="National University of Córdoba">National University of Córdoba</a>, which has been the second-largest university in the country since the early 20th century (after the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Buenos_Aires" title="University of Buenos Aires">University of Buenos Aires</a>), in terms of the number of students, faculty, and academic programs. Córdoba is also known for its historical movements, such as <a href="/wiki/Cordobazo" title="Cordobazo">Cordobazo</a> and La Reforma del '18 (known as <a href="/wiki/University_Revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="University Revolution">University Revolution</a> in English).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_settlement">First settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: First settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1570, Viceroy <a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Toledo" title="Francisco de Toledo">Francisco de Toledo</a> entrusted the Spanish settler <a href="/wiki/Jer%C3%B3nimo_Luis_de_Cabrera" title="Jerónimo Luis de Cabrera">Jerónimo Luis de Cabrera</a>, with the task of populating and founding a settlement in the <a href="/wiki/Punilla_Valley" title="Punilla Valley">Punilla Valley</a>. Cabrera sent an expedition of 48 men to the territory of the <a href="/wiki/Comechingones" class="mw-redirect" title="Comechingones">Comechingones</a>. He divided the principal column that entered through the north of the provincial territory at <a href="/wiki/Villa_Mar%C3%ADa,_C%C3%B3rdoba" class="mw-redirect" title="Villa María, Córdoba">Villa María</a>. The one hundred man expedition set foot on what today is Córdoba on 24 June 1573. Cabrera called the nearby river San Juan (today <a href="/wiki/Suqu%C3%ADa" class="mw-redirect" title="Suquía">Suquía</a>). The settlement was officially founded on 6 July of the same year and named Córdoba de la Nueva Andalucía, possibly in honor of ancestors of the founder's wife, originally from <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Córdoba, Spain">Córdoba, Spain</a>. The foundation of the city took place on the left bank of the river on Francisco de Torres' advice.</p>

<p>The settlement was inhabited by <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_people_of_the_Americas" class="mw-redirect" title="Indigenous people of the Americas">aboriginal people</a> called <a href="/wiki/Comechingones" class="mw-redirect" title="Comechingones">Comechingones</a>, who lived in communities called Ayllus. After four years, having repelled attacks by the aborigines, the settlement's authorities moved it to the opposite bank of the Suquía River in 1577. The Lieutenant Governor at the time, Don Lorenzo Suárez de Figueroa, planned the first layout of the city as a grid of seventy blocks. Once the city core had been moved to its current location, it acquired a stable population. Its economy blossomed due to trade with the cities in the north.</p>
<p>In 1599, the religious order of the <a href="/wiki/Jesuits" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesuits">Jesuits</a> arrived in the settlement. They established a <a href="/wiki/Novitiate" title="Novitiate">Novitiate</a> in 1608 and in 1610, the Colegio Maximo, which became the University of Córdoba in 1613 (today <a href="/wiki/National_University_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="National University of Córdoba">National University of Córdoba</a>), the fourth-oldest in the Americas. The local <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Jesuit</a> Church remains one of the oldest buildings in South America and contains the Monserrat Secondary School, a church, and residence buildings. To maintain such a project, the Jesuits operated five <i><a href="/wiki/Jesuit_Reductions" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesuit Reductions">Reducciones</a></i> in the surrounding, fertile valleys, including Caroya, <a href="/wiki/Jes%C3%BAs_Mar%C3%ADa,_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Jesús María, Córdoba">Jesús María</a>, Santa Catalina, <a href="/wiki/Alta_Gracia" title="Alta Gracia">Alta Gracia</a> and Candelaria.</p>
<p>The farm and the complex, started in 1615, had to be left by the Jesuits following the 1767 decree by King <a href="/wiki/Charles_III_of_Spain" title="Charles III of Spain">Charles III of Spain</a> that expelled them from the continent. They were then run by the <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscans</a> until 1853, when the Jesuits returned to the Americas. Nevertheless, the university and the high-school were nationalized a year later. Each <a href="/wiki/Estancia" title="Estancia">Estancia</a> has its own church and set of buildings, around which towns grew, such as <a href="/wiki/Alta_Gracia" title="Alta Gracia">Alta Gracia</a>, the closest to the Block.</p>

<p>In 1776, <a href="/wiki/King_Carlos_III" class="mw-redirect" title="King Carlos III">King Carlos III</a> created the <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_the_R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Viceroyalty of the Río de la Plata">Viceroyalty of the Río de la Plata</a>, in which Córdoba stays in 1785 as the Government Intendency of Córdoba, including the current territories of the provinces of Córdoba, <a href="/wiki/La_Rioja_Province_(Argentina)" class="mw-redirect" title="La Rioja Province (Argentina)">La Rioja</a> and the region of <a href="/wiki/Cuyo_(Argentina)" title="Cuyo (Argentina)">Cuyo</a>.</p>
<p>According to the 1760 census, the population of the city was 22,000 inhabitants. During the May Revolution in 1810, the widespread opinion of the most notable citizens was of continuing respecting the orders of <a href="/wiki/Fernando_VII" class="mw-redirect" title="Fernando VII">Fernando VII</a>, attitude assumed by the local authorities, which led to the <a href="/wiki/Liniers_Counter-revolution" title="Liniers Counter-revolution">Liniers Counter-revolution</a>. This position was not shared by the <a href="/wiki/Dean_Funes" class="mw-redirect" title="Dean Funes">Dean Gregorio Funes</a>, who was adhering to the revolutionary ideas, beside supporting contact with <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Belgrano" title="Manuel Belgrano">Manuel Belgrano</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juan_Jos%C3%A9_Castelli" title="Juan José Castelli">Juan José Castelli</a>.</p>
<p>In March 1816, the Argentine Congress met in <a href="/wiki/Tucum%C3%A1n_Province" title="Tucumán Province">Tucumán</a> for an independence resolution. Córdoba sent <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_P%C3%A9rez_Bulnes" title="Eduardo Pérez Bulnes">Eduardo Pérez Bulnes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Settler" title="Settler">Jerónimo Salguero de Cabrera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Antonio_Cabrera" title="José Antonio Cabrera">José Antonio Cabrera</a>, and to the Canon of the cathedral Michael Calixto of the Circle, all of them of autonomous position.</p>
<p>The 1820s belonged to <a href="/wiki/Caudillos" class="mw-redirect" title="Caudillos">caudillos</a>, since the country was in full process of formation. Until 1820 a central government taken root in Buenos Aires existed, but the remaining thirteen provinces felt that after 9 July 1816 what had happened it was simply a change of commander. Cepeda's Battle faced the commanders of the Littoral with the central power.</p>
<p>Finally, the Federales obtained the victory, for what the country remained since then integrated by 13 autonomous provinces, on the national government having been dissolved. From this way the period known like about the Provincial Autonomies began. From this moment the provinces tried to create a <a href="/wiki/Federal_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal government">federal</a> system that was integrating them without coming to good port, this mainly for the regional differences of every province.</p>
<p>Two Córdoba figures stood out in this period: Governor Juan Bautista Bustos, who was an official of the Army of the North and in 1820 was supervised by the troops quartered in Arequito, a town near Córdoba, and his ally and later enemy, General <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mar%C3%ADa_Paz" title="José María Paz">José María Paz</a>. In 1821, Bustos repelled the invasion of Córdoba on the part of Francisco Ramírez and his <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chilean</a> ally, General <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Miguel_Carrera" title="José Miguel Carrera">José Miguel Carrera</a>. The conflict originated in a dispute with the power system that included the provinces of <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires_Province" title="Buenos Aires Province">Buenos Aires</a>, Córdoba and <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe_Province" title="Santa Fe Province">Santa Fe</a>; according to the 1822 census the total population of Córdoba was of 11,552 inhabitants.</p>

<p>At the end of the 19th century the process of national industrialization began with the height of the economic agro-exporting model, principally of meats and cereals. This process is associated with the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_Argentina" title="Immigration to Argentina">European immigration</a> who began to settle the city, generally possessing the education and enterprising capacity appropriate for the development of industry. The majority of these European immigrants came from Italy (initially from <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">Piedmont</a>, <a href="/wiki/Veneto" title="Veneto">Veneto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a>; later from <a href="/wiki/Campania" title="Campania">Campania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">Calabria</a>), and Spain (mostly <a href="/wiki/Galicians" title="Galicians">Galicians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Basques" title="Basques">Basques</a>)</p>
<p>At the beginning of the 20th century the city had 90,000 inhabitants.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The city changed considerably its physionomy following the construction of new avenues, walks and public squares, as well as the installation of an electrified tram system, in 1909. In 1918, Córdoba was the epicentre of a movement known as the <a href="/wiki/University_reform_in_Argentina" class="mw-redirect" title="University reform in Argentina">University Reform</a>, which then spread to the rest of the Universities of the country, Americas and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The development of the domestic market, the <a href="/wiki/English_settlement_in_Argentina" class="mw-redirect" title="English settlement in Argentina">British investments</a> that facilitated European settlement, the development of the railways on the <a href="/wiki/Pampas" title="Pampas">pampas</a> rapidly industrialized the city. Córdoba's industrial sector first developed from the need to transform <a href="/wiki/Raw_materials" class="mw-redirect" title="Raw materials">raw materials</a> such as leather, <a href="/wiki/Meats" class="mw-redirect" title="Meats">meats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a> for export.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1927, the Military Aircraft Manufacturer (FMA) was inaugurated. The facility would become one of the most important in the world after the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World war">Second World war</a> with the arrival of German technical personnel. From 1952, its production began to diversify, to constitute the base of the former Institute Aerotécnico, the state-owned company Aeronautical and Mechanical Industries of the State (IAME). Córdoba was chosen as the site of The <i>Instituto Aerotécnico</i> that later became the <i>Fábrica Militar de Aviones</i>. It employed the Focke Wulf men until President <a href="/wiki/Juan_Per%C3%B3n" title="Juan Perón">Juan Perón</a> was ousted by a <a href="/wiki/Revoluci%C3%B3n_Libertadora" title="Revolución Libertadora">coup in 1955</a>. <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> purchased FMA in 1995.</p>
<p>Córdoba, according to the census of 1947, had almost 400,000 inhabitants (a quarter of the province's total). Subsequent industrial development led thousands of rural families to the city, doubling its population and turning Córdoba into the second largest city in Argentina, after Buenos Aires, by 1970. The city's population and economic growth moderated, afterwards, though living standards rose with the increase in the national consumption of Córdoba's industrial products, as well as the development of other sectors of economic activity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>At times rivaling Buenos Aires for its importance in national politics, Córdoba was the site of the initial mutiny leading to the 1955 <i><a href="/wiki/Revoluci%C3%B3n_Libertadora" title="Revolución Libertadora">Revolución Libertadora</a></i> that deposed President <a href="/wiki/Juan_Per%C3%B3n" title="Juan Perón">Juan Perón</a> and the setting for the 1969 <i><a href="/wiki/Cordobazo" title="Cordobazo">Cordobazo</a></i>, a series of violent labor and student protests that ultimately led to <a href="/wiki/Argentine_general_election,_March_1973" title="Argentine general election, March 1973">elections in 1973</a>. Córdoba's current economic diversity is due to a vigorous services sector and the demand for agro-industrial and railway equipment and, in particular, the introduction of U.S. and European automakers after 1954.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The city's geographic location is <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=C%C3%B3rdoba%2C_Argentina&amp;params=31_25_S_64_11_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">31°25′S</span> <span class="longitude">64°11′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">31.417°S 64.183°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-31.417; -64.183</span></span></span></a></span>, taking as a point of reference San Martín Square in downtown Córdoba. The relative location of the municipal common land, is in the south hemisphere of the globe, to the south of the <a href="/wiki/South_American" class="mw-redirect" title="South American">South American</a> subcontinent, in the geographical centre – west of Argentina and of the province of Córdoba; to a distance of 702&#160;km (436&#160;mi) from Buenos Aires and 401&#160;km (249&#160;mi) from the city of <a href="/wiki/Rosario" class="mw-redirect" title="Rosario">Rosario</a></p>
<p>As per the provincial laws No. 778 December 14, 1878, Not. 927 October 20, 1883, and Not. 1295 December 29, 1893, the limits of the city of Córdoba are delineated in the northern part, South, East and West located to 12&#160;km (7&#160;mi) from San Martín Square which means that the common land has 24&#160;km (15&#160;mi) from side. The city, adjoins in the northern territory with <a href="/wiki/Col%C3%B3n_Department,_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Colón Department, Córdoba">Colón Department</a> summarizing a total surface of 562.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city is located in the plain of the <a href="/wiki/Pampa" class="mw-redirect" title="Pampa">Humid Pampa</a>, to the east of the oriental cord of Córdoba Hills or Sierras Chicas, also known as the Sierras Cordobesas, which has an average height of 550 m.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It spreads at the foot of the mount, on both banks of the River Suquía, and flows into the <a href="/wiki/San_Roque_Lake" title="San Roque Lake">San Roque reservoir</a>; from there, the <a href="/wiki/Primero_River" title="Primero River">Primero River</a> goes east into the plains surrounding the city of Córdoba.</p>
<p>Once inside the city, the <i>La Cañada</i> stream meets the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Primero" class="mw-redirect" title="Rio Primero">Rio Primero</a> near the city centre area. Two kilometers to the east, <i>Isla de los Patos</i> (Ducks Island) was repopulated with ducks and swans in the 1980s. It was reported in March 2006 that a large number of ducks had died due to unspecified causes.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Pollution caused by chemical waste is suspected as the cause, but <a href="/wiki/Avian_influenza" title="Avian influenza">avian influenza</a> is also being investigated.</p>
<p>Beyond the city limits, the river flows towards the Algarrobos swamp and ends its course on the southern coast of the <a href="/wiki/Mar_Chiquita,_C%C3%B3rdoba" class="mw-redirect" title="Mar Chiquita, Córdoba">Mar Chiquita</a> (or Mar de Ansenuza) <a href="/wiki/Salt_lake_(geography)" class="mw-redirect" title="Salt lake (geography)">salt lake</a>. All in all, the river has a length of approximately 200&#160;km (124&#160;mi) and carries, on average, 9.7 m³/s, with minimum of 2 m³/s and maximum of 24 m³/s<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> with a peak during the summer months.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">Pollution of the water</a> and of the riverbank is a major environmental issue in Córdoba.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Periodic cleaning operations are carried out to increase the quality of the water and to preserve the viability of fishing, both in the San Roque reservoir area and downstream.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The climate of the city of Córdoba, as that of most of the province, is <a href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate" title="Humid subtropical climate">humid subtropical</a> (<i>Cwa</i>, according to the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a>), moderated by the Pampas winds, cold winds that blow from the South-western quadrant, originates in the <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.</p>
<p>There are four marked seasons. Summers run from late November till early March, and bring days between 28&#160;°C (82&#160;°F) and 33&#160;°C (91&#160;°F) and night between 15&#160;°C (59&#160;°F) and 19&#160;°C (66&#160;°F) with frequent thunderstorms. Heat waves are common, and bring days with temperatures over 38&#160;°C (100&#160;°F) and hot, sticky nights; however, Pampero winds are sure to bring relief with thunderstorms and a day or two of cool, crisp weather: nighttime temperatures can easily descend to 12&#160;°C (54&#160;°F) or less, but the heat starts building up right away the next day.</p>
<p>By late February or early March, nights start getting cooler, and in March, highs average 27&#160;°C (81&#160;°F) and lows 15&#160;°C (59&#160;°F); after cold fronts, lows below 10&#160;°C (50&#160;°F) and highs below 20&#160;°C (68&#160;°F) are recorded in this month. April is significantly drier already; highs attain 24&#160;°C (75&#160;°F) on average and lows 12&#160;°C (54&#160;°F), creating very pleasant conditions. On some years, temperatures can approach or even attain the freezing point in late April; however, heat waves of up to 33&#160;°C (91&#160;°F) are still possible, but nights are rarely as hot as in the summer. May usually brings the first frosts, and very dry weather, with under 20&#160;mm (1&#160;in) of rain expected. Highs average 21&#160;°C (70&#160;°F) and lows average 8&#160;°C (46&#160;°F); however, when cold waves reach the area, highs may stay below 8&#160;°C (46&#160;°F) and lows can be well below freezing.</p>
<p>Winter lasts from late May till early September, and bring average highs of 18&#160;°C (64&#160;°F) and lows of 4&#160;°C (39&#160;°F). However, strong northwesterly winds downsloping from the mountains can bring what is known as "Veranito" (little summer) with highs of up to 30&#160;°C (86&#160;°F) or more and dusty, windy weather (but dry, pleasant nights) for 2–3 days.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Conversely, when storms stall over the Atlantic coast, there may be several days of drizzle and cool weather, and when cold air masses invade the country from Antarctica (several times every winter), there may be one or two days with temperatures around 6&#160;°C (43&#160;°F), drizzle and high winds (which combined make it feel very cold), followed by dry, cold weather with nighttime lows between 0&#160;°C (32&#160;°F) and −5&#160;°C (23&#160;°F) and daytime highs between 8&#160;°C (46&#160;°F) and 15&#160;°C (59&#160;°F). Snowfall is very rare in the city, but more frequent in the outskirts where the Sierras begin<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>; sleet may fall every once in a while. The record low temperature for Córdoba is −8.2&#160;°C (17.2&#160;°F). In June, only 3.5&#160;mm (0.1&#160;in) of rain are expected, compared to 168&#160;mm (6.6&#160;in) in January.</p>
<p>Spring is extremely variable and windy: there may be long stretches of cool, dry weather and cold nights followed by intense heat waves up to 38&#160;°C (100&#160;°F), followed by the most severe thunderstorms with hail and high winds. It is not unusual to see temperatures drop 20&#160;°C (36&#160;°F) from one day to another, or to have frost following extreme heat. Drought is most common in this season, when the normal summer rainfall arrives later than expected. By October, days are warm at 26&#160;°C (79&#160;°F) but nights remain cold at 11&#160;°C (52&#160;°F), by late November, the weather resembles summer weather with cooler nights.</p>
<p>The wealthier suburbs west of the city are located at slightly higher altitudes, which allows cool breezes to blow in the summer, bringing drier, comfortable nights during hotter periods, and more regular frost in the winter. Generally speaking, Córdoba's daytime temperatures are very slightly warmer than Buenos Aires' but nighttime lows are usually cooler, especially in the winter. This, combined to a lower humidity and the possibility of fleeing to higher altitudes minutes away from the city centre, makes the climate a bit more comfortable than in the capital.</p>
<p>The variations or thermal extents are greater than in Buenos Aires, and lower in annual rainfall: 750&#160;mm (30&#160;in) / year. The annual average temperature calculated during the 20th century was 18&#160;°C. In January, the hottest month of the austral summer, the average maximum is 31&#160;°C and the minimum 17&#160;°C. In July, the coldest month of the year, the average temperatures are between 19&#160;°C and 3&#160;°C. In winter it is very frequent that temperatures rise above 30&#160;°C, due to the influence of the wind Zonda.</p>
<p>Due to the extension of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_area" title="Metropolitan area">metropolitan area</a>, there exists a difference of 5&#160;°C between the central area and the Greater Córdoba. The central district, a dense high-rise area is located in a depression, and it is the core of an important <a href="/wiki/Heat_island" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat island">heat island</a>. In addition the city presents a phenomenon of smog, but not so dense as to present health concerns.</p>

<p>The largest ethnic groups in Córdoba are <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italians</a>/<a href="/wiki/Italian_Argentine" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Argentine">Italian Argentine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spaniards" title="Spaniards">Spaniards</a>/<a href="/wiki/Spanish_Argentine" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish Argentine">Spanish Argentine</a> (mostly <a href="/wiki/Galicians" title="Galicians">Galicians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Basques" title="Basques">Basques</a>/<a href="/wiki/Basque_Argentine" class="mw-redirect" title="Basque Argentine">Basque Argentine</a>). Waves of immigrants from other European countries arrived in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. From the rest of <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a> came immigrants from <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>). <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europeans" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Europeans">Eastern Europeans</a> also arrived from nations such as <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Montenegro" title="Montenegro">Montenegro</a>). By the 1910s, 43 percent of the city population was non-native Argentine after immigration rates peaked.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most immigrants, regardless of origin, settled in the city or around <a href="/wiki/Conurbation" title="Conurbation">Greater Córdoba</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, in the first stages of immigration, some formed colonies (especially <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_colonies_in_Argentina" title="Agricultural colonies in Argentina">agricultural colonies</a>) in different parts of the city, often encouraged by the Argentine government and/or sponsored by private individuals and organizations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographic_distribution">Demographic distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Demographic distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Córdoba is the second largest city in the country in population and concentrates 40.9% of the Córdoba Province population of 3,216,993 inhabitants and represents almost 3.3% of the <a href="/wiki/Argentine" class="mw-redirect" title="Argentine">Argentine</a> population, which according to estimates to June 2008, reached 39,745,613 inhabitants. Driven by migration both domestic and from abroad, the city's rate of population growth was an elevated 3.2% annually from 1914 to 1960; but, it has been declining steadily since then, and has averaged around 0.4% a year, since the national census of 2001.</p>
<p>According to the last provincial census of 2008, the city has 1,315,540 inhabitants, representing an increase of 3.78% with regard to the 1,267,521 registered during the national census of 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-censo_2008_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-censo_2008-15">[14]</a></sup> Greater Córdoba is the metropolitan area of the city of Córdoba, a union of medium localities of the department Colón, from the north to the south. Greater Córdoba is the second-largest urban agglomeration in Argentina in both population and surface area.</p>
<p>The growth of the metropolitan area was not equal in all directions, it spreads approximately up to 50&#160;km (31&#160;mi) to the northwest of the Córdoba city centre in a thin succession of small localities. This is almost the maximum distance from the Buenos Aires city center to the most distant of its metropolitan area points; whereas in the rest of the cardinal points it comes to 15&#160;km (9&#160;mi).</p>
<p>The city receives a constant flow of students from the North-East, Southwest regions of Argentina and of other <a href="/wiki/South_American" class="mw-redirect" title="South American">South American</a> countries, owed principally to the National University of Córdoba, which increases gradually the city population. Córdoba grows constantly, expanding especially towards the southern areas of <a href="/wiki/Alta_Gracia" title="Alta Gracia">Alta Gracia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Villa_Carlos_Paz" title="Villa Carlos Paz">Villa Carlos Paz</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_structure">Urban structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Urban structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Area_segun_uso_del_suelo_cordoba_argentina.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Area_segun_uso_del_suelo_cordoba_argentina.svg/220px-Area_segun_uso_del_suelo_cordoba_argentina.svg.png" width="220" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Area_segun_uso_del_suelo_cordoba_argentina.svg/330px-Area_segun_uso_del_suelo_cordoba_argentina.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Area_segun_uso_del_suelo_cordoba_argentina.svg/440px-Area_segun_uso_del_suelo_cordoba_argentina.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="2386" data-file-height="1878" /></a>

<p>The use of the city soil is regulated by the municipality, which determines and destines 26,177 hectares to urban area (40.24%), 12,267 hectares to the industrial dominant area (21.3%), 16,404 hectares to rural area dominant (28.45%) and 5,750 hectares to other uses as military proposes, or institutional spaces (9.98%) of the total area of the city.<sup id="cite_ref-catastro02_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-catastro02-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Green spaces include different types of spaces, from squares, small squares, up to urban, green linear parks of different scales as the <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river Suquia</a>, bicycle pathways and highway s). The surface supported by the Municipality of Córdoba in character of green Urban adds approximately 1645 hectares.</p>
<p>The historical centre is shaped by quadrangular blocks of some hundred thirty meters of side. The disposition of the neighborhoods and principal avenues is radial. From the city centre district large avenues that lead to the most peripheral neighborhoods are born. In conformity with the demographic growth the city has expanded principally to the northwest and to the southeast, following the trace of the <a href="/wiki/National_Route_9_(Argentina)" title="National Route 9 (Argentina)">National Route 9</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Districts">Districts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Districts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Córdoba is home to one of the most important financial districts in South America. The district is home to the Bank of Córdoba and other private banking institutions. Sightseeing places include San Martín Square, the <a href="/wiki/Jesuit_Block_and_Estancias_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Jesuit Block and Estancias of Córdoba">Jesuit Block</a> (declared UNESCO <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>) and the Genaro Pérez Museum. The streets mostly follow a regular checkerboard pattern, and the main thoroughfares are Vélez Sarsfield, Colón, General Paz, Dean Funes Avenue, and 27 April Street. The point of origin of the city is the San Martin Square, surrounded by the Municipality and Central Post Office.</p>
<p>Downtown Córdoba is home of large shopping malls, notably <a href="/wiki/Patio_Olmos" title="Patio Olmos">Patio Olmos</a>. This mall is the result of a massive regeneration effort, recycling and refurbishing the west side old warehouses into elegant offices and commercial centres. An important cultural point of interest is the Palacio Ferreyra, a mansion built in 1916 based on plans by the <a href="/wiki/French_architecture" title="French architecture">French architect</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Sanson" title="Ernest Sanson">Ernest Sanson</a>. The Ferreyra palace was converted into the <a href="/wiki/Evita_Fine_Arts_Museum" title="Evita Fine Arts Museum">Evita Perón Museum of Fine Arts</a> (the city's second) in 2007. Located at the corner of Hipólito Yrigoyen and Chacabuco Avenues, it has now been restored and adapted to house the city's principal art gallery.</p>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_C%C3%B3rdoba&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="New Córdoba (page does not exist)">New Córdoba</a> has a number of important avenues such as <i>Yrigoyen</i> and <i>Vélez Sarsfield</i>. Most of the university students in this growing city live in this neighbourhood, and a recent construction boom has been transforming this upscale area into the fastest-growing section in the city.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Puente_parque_de_las_tejas.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Puente_parque_de_las_tejas.JPG/220px-Puente_parque_de_las_tejas.JPG" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Puente_parque_de_las_tejas.JPG/330px-Puente_parque_de_las_tejas.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Puente_parque_de_las_tejas.JPG/440px-Puente_parque_de_las_tejas.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4928" data-file-height="3264" /></a>

Tejas Park at New Córdoba.</div>

<p>Ciudad Universitaria is a district located in the southern area of the city, next to the 17 hectares (42 acres) <a href="/wiki/Sarmiento_Park" title="Sarmiento Park">Sarmiento Park</a>, the city's most important one. The <a href="/wiki/Universidad_Nacional_de_C%C3%B3rdoba" class="mw-redirect" title="Universidad Nacional de Córdoba">Universidad Nacional de Córdoba</a> (UNC) has most of its facilities in this area. The UNC was the first university built in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, founded by Jesuits around 1622. The <a href="/wiki/Universidad_Nacional_de_C%C3%B3rdoba" class="mw-redirect" title="Universidad Nacional de Córdoba">Universidad Nacional de Córdoba</a> is also famous for the "Reforma Universitaria", a student-led protest that started in March 1918 in the Medical School, in which the students rebelled against the prevailing university system. This was an old anachronic system in which professors were authoritarian and inefficient, with a religiously oriented curriculum. Eventually this revolt lead to a more secular curriculum and some significant re-structuring of the university government. The distinctive nature of the movement derived not only from its radical demands, but also from its extremist tactics, the level of sophistication of its organization, and its major continental impact. In fact, the Reform Movement rapidly spread from Córdoba to <a href="/wiki/Lima" title="Lima">Lima</a> (1919), Cuzco (1920), Santiago de Chile (1920), and Mexico (1921). Another important university, the <a href="/wiki/UTN" class="mw-redirect" title="UTN">UTN</a>, dedicated to the teaching of engineering sciences, is located in this part of the city. There are also a gym and football stadium and tennis courts for the students. The <i>Córdoba Zoo</i> is located in this district.</p>
<p>Located about 6&#160;km (4&#160;mi) from downtown Córdoba is the Cerro de Las Rosas. This very affluent neighborhood is famous for its schools, shops and educational institutions. This neighborhood's economic activity centers around the Rafael Núñez Avenue, a long wide road that stretches for a few kilometers and has restaurants, boutiques, banks and other shops. Over the last decade, this neighborhood has experienced steady growth; however, some of its most affluent inhabitants have moved to gated communities for security reasons. Some of these communities, such as "Las Delicias" and "Lomas de los Carolinos", are in the old Camino a La Calera.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Córdoba public transport system includes <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">trains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">buses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trolleybus" title="Trolleybus">trolleybuses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taxi" class="mw-redirect" title="Taxi">taxis</a>. Long-distance buses reach most cities and towns throughout the country.</p>
<p>The city is served by the nation's third largest airport <a href="/wiki/Ingeniero_Ambrosio_L.V._Taravella_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Ingeniero Ambrosio L.V. Taravella International Airport">Ingeniero Ambrosio L.V. Taravella International Airport</a>.</p>

<p>Rail transport in Córdoba has <a href="/wiki/Commuter_rail" title="Commuter rail">commuter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inter-city_rail" title="Inter-city rail">long-distance</a> services, all operated by state-owned <a href="/wiki/Operadora_Ferroviaria_Sociedad_del_Estado" title="Operadora Ferroviaria Sociedad del Estado">Trenes Argentinos</a>. From <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba_Mitre_railway_station" title="Córdoba Mitre railway station">the Mitre railway station</a> depart trains to <a href="/wiki/Villa_Mar%C3%ADa" title="Villa María">Villa María</a> <sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> while the <a href="/wiki/Tren_de_las_Sierras" title="Tren de las Sierras">Tren de las Sierras</a> connects the cities of <a href="/wiki/Alta_C%C3%B3rdoba_railway_station" title="Alta Córdoba railway station">Alta Córdoba</a> with <a href="/wiki/Cosqu%C3%ADn" class="mw-redirect" title="Cosquín">Cosquín</a>.</p>
<p>From <a href="/wiki/Retiro_Mitre_railway_station" title="Retiro Mitre railway station">Retiro station</a> of <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a> trains reach Córdoba twice a week with an estimated journey time of 18 hours.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tren_de_las_Sierras" title="Tren de las Sierras">Tren de las Sierras</a> is a tourist service that crosses the Valle de Punilla, Quebrada del Río Suquía and the Dique San Roque's Lake. It has two services per day with an additional service on weekends.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Córdoba has two <a href="/wiki/Railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway station">railway stations</a>, the <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba_Mitre_railway_station" title="Córdoba Mitre railway station">Córdoba (Mitre)</a> originally built by the <a href="/wiki/Central_Argentine_Railway" title="Central Argentine Railway">Central Argentine R.</a> in 1886. That station has been an intermediate stop for trains to <a href="/wiki/Tucum%C3%A1n_Mitre_railway_station" title="Tucumán Mitre railway station">Tucumán</a>, successively operated by <a href="/wiki/Ferrocarriles_Argentinos" title="Ferrocarriles Argentinos">Ferrocarriles Argentinos</a> and then by <a href="/wiki/Privately_held_company" title="Privately held company">private</a> consortiums such as <a href="/wiki/Ferrocentral" title="Ferrocentral">Ferrocentral</a>. The other station is <a href="/wiki/Alta_C%C3%B3rdoba_railway_station" title="Alta Córdoba railway station">Alta Córdoba</a>, built and operated by <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">British</a>-owned <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba_North_Western_Railway" title="Córdoba North Western Railway">Córdoba North Western</a> in 1891, and currently terminus of Tren de las Sierras.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High-speed_rail_project">High-speed rail project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: High-speed rail project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires%E2%80%93Rosario%E2%80%93C%C3%B3rdoba_high-speed_railway" title="Buenos Aires–Rosario–Córdoba high-speed railway">Buenos Aires–Rosario–Córdoba high-speed railway</a></div>
<p>The Argentine government had projected to build a <a href="/wiki/High-speed_rail" title="High-speed rail">high-speed train</a> between <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires-Rosario-C%C3%B3rdoba_high-speed_railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Buenos Aires-Rosario-Córdoba high-speed railway">Buenos Aires-Rosario-Córdoba</a>. It would eventually join Córdoba and Buenos Aires, with an intermediate stop in <a href="/wiki/Rosario,_Santa_Fe" title="Rosario, Santa Fe">Rosario</a>, in about 3 hours at speeds of up to 350&#160;km/h (220&#160;mph).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> Originally scheduled to be started in 2008, with its inauguration in 2010, the project was finally dismissed in December 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> The total cost of the rail had been estimated in <a href="/wiki/US_Dollar" class="mw-redirect" title="US Dollar">USD</a> 4,000,000,000. <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> company <a href="/wiki/Alstom" title="Alstom">Alstom</a>, that had won the tender to build <a href="/wiki/High-speed_rail" title="High-speed rail">high-speed rail</a>, admitted to have paid bribes to the Argentine authorities.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metro">Metro</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Metro">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba_Metro" title="Córdoba Metro">Córdoba Metro</a></div>
<p>On 10 December 2007 it was announced that a consortium of Iecsa/Gela companies was to build the US$1.1&#160;billion metro system in Córdoba. In April 2008, President <a href="/wiki/Cristina_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_Kirchner" title="Cristina Fernández de Kirchner">Cristina Fernández de Kirchner</a>, signed the project into law. The works, however, are currently "on hold" due to the world financial crisis.</p>

<p>Since World War II, Córdoba has been developing a versatile industrial base. The biggest sectors is car and car parts manufacturing: (<a href="/wiki/Renault" title="Renault">Renault</a> has a factory which produces a range of cars, <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen" title="Volkswagen">Volkswagen</a> has a factory specialized in the production of gearboxes, and <a href="/wiki/Fiat" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiat">Fiat</a>) has another car factory in the city. Many suppliers (both local and foreign) manufacture car parts for these operations. Additionally, starting in 2017-2018, <a href="/wiki/Nissan" title="Nissan">Nissan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz" title="Mercedes-Benz">Mercedes-Benz</a> will begin the production of their new pickup truck at the <a href="/wiki/Renault" title="Renault">Renault</a> factory. Railway construction (<a href="/wiki/Materfer" title="Materfer">Materfer</a>) and aircraft construction (<a href="/wiki/F%C3%A1brica_Militar_de_Aviones" class="mw-redirect" title="Fábrica Militar de Aviones">Fábrica Militar de Aviones</a>) were once significant employers, but their activities have greatly diminished. Furthermore, there are some textile, heavy and chemical industries (e.g. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Porta_Hermanos_S.A.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Porta Hermanos S.A. (page does not exist)">Porta</a> for alcohol).</p>
<p>Areas around Córdoba produce vast amounts of agricultural products, and some of these are processed around the city. Additionally, the province is one of the main producers of agricultural machinery in the country, although most of these operations are not in the city itself. Candy company <a href="/wiki/Grupo_Arcor" title="Grupo Arcor">Arcor</a> is headquartered in the city.</p>
<p>Córdoba has been considered the technological centre of Argentina. The Argentinian <i>spaceport</i> (Centro Espacial Teófilo Tabanera), where <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> are being developed and operated for <a href="/wiki/Comisi%C3%B3n_Nacional_de_Actividades_Espaciales" title="Comisión Nacional de Actividades Espaciales">CONAE</a>, is located in the suburb of Falda del Carmen. The software and electronic industries are advancing and becoming significant exporters; among the leading local employers in the sector are <a href="/wiki/Motorola" title="Motorola">Motorola</a>, Vates, <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Data_Systems" title="Electronic Data Systems">Electronic Data Systems</a>, and Santex América.</p>
<p>The city also has a service-based economy focused on retail, professional services (with companies like <a href="/wiki/Deloitte" title="Deloitte">Deloitte</a>) and financial services, where the main local player is credit card provider Tarjeta Naranja. It has recently emerged as a start-up hub with a growing number of angel investors, in part due to the availability of people with technology-oriented skills.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">Association football</a> is the most popular sport in Córdoba as well as in Argentina. Several leagues and divisions compete in the local championship annually. The city currently has one representative in the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_First_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="Argentine First Division">Argentine First Division</a>, <a href="/wiki/Club_Atl%C3%A9tico_Belgrano" title="Club Atlético Belgrano">Belgrano</a>, founded in 1905 and playing at the <a href="/wiki/Estadio_Gigante_de_Alberdi" class="mw-redirect" title="Estadio Gigante de Alberdi">Estadio Gigante de Alberdi</a>. Other lower-division sides in Córdoba include <a href="/wiki/Talleres_de_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Talleres de Córdoba">Talleres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Instituto_Atl%C3%A9tico_Central_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Instituto Atlético Central Córdoba">Instituto de Córdoba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Club_Atl%C3%A9tico_Racing_de_C%C3%B3rdoba" class="mw-redirect" title="Club Atlético Racing de Córdoba">Racing de Córdoba</a>.</p>
<p>Basketball is the second-most popular sport in Córdoba. <a href="/wiki/Asociaci%C3%B3n_Deportiva_Atenas" title="Asociación Deportiva Atenas">Asociación Deportiva Atenas</a> is the most popular club, and one of the most successful in Argentina, having won the National League (LNB) seven times, and being three times winner of the South American League. Córdoba was one of the host cities of the <a href="/wiki/1990_FIBA_World_Championship" title="1990 FIBA World Championship">1990 FIBA World Championship</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">Rugby union</a> is also a very popular sport in Córdoba, which has close to 20 teams with many divisions. <a href="/wiki/Tala_Rugby_Club" title="Tala Rugby Club">Tala Rugby Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/Club_La_Tablada" title="Club La Tablada">Club La Tablada</a>, <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba_Athletic_Club" title="Córdoba Athletic Club">Córdoba Athletic Club</a> (one of the oldest clubs in Argentina and founded by the British who worked in the building of the Argentine Railroads around 1882), <a href="/wiki/Jockey_Club_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Jockey Club Córdoba">Jockey Club Córdoba</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Club_Universitario_de_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Club Universitario de Córdoba">Club Universitario de Córdoba</a> are some of the most prestigious teams. Córdoba is one of the strongest rugby places in Argentina, and is the home of many international players. Many of the great players in Argentina and Italy began their careers in the Córdoba's rugby clubs.</p>
<p>Golf and tennis are also very popular; notable players that started playing in Córdoba include <a href="/wiki/%C3%81ngel_Cabrera" title="Ángel Cabrera">Ángel "Pato" Cabrera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Romero" title="Eduardo Romero">Eduardo "Gato" Romero</a> in golf.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rally_Argentina" title="Rally Argentina">Argentine stage</a> of the <a href="/wiki/World_Rally_Championship" title="World Rally Championship">World Rally Championship</a> has been run near Córdoba since 1984.</p>

<p>Córdoba has long been one of Argentina's main educational centers, with 6 universities and several postsecondary colleges. Students from the entire country, as well as neighbouring countries attend the local universities, giving the city a distinct atmosphere.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_University_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="National University of Córdoba">National University of Córdoba</a>, established since 1613, is the 4th oldest in the Americas and the first in Argentina. It has about 105,000 students, and offers degrees in a wide variety of subjects in the sciences, applied sciences, social sciences, humanities and arts.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Technological_University_%E2%80%93_C%C3%B3rdoba_Regional_Faculty" title="National Technological University – Córdoba Regional Faculty">Córdoba Regional Faculty</a> is a branch of the <a href="/wiki/National_Technological_University" title="National Technological University">National Technological University</a> in Córdoba, offering undergraduate degrees in engineering (civil, electrical, electronic, industrial, mechanical, metallurgy, chemical and information), as well as master's degrees in engineering and business, and a PhD program in engineering and materials.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Catholic_University_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic University of Córdoba">Catholic University of Córdoba</a> is the oldest <a href="/wiki/Private_university" title="Private university">private university</a> in Córdoba, it has nearly 10,000 students.</p>
<p>The Aeronautic University Institute, run by the Argentine Air Force, offers degrees in aeronautical, telecommunications and electronic engineering, as well as information systems, accounting, logistics and administration.</p>
<p>Furthermore, the Universidad Siglo 21 and Universidad Blas Pascal are private universities in the city.</p>
<p>The Air Force Academy and the Air Force NCOs School are both located in the city outskirts.</p>
<p>There is an Italian international school, <a href="/wiki/Escuela_Dante_Alighieri" title="Escuela Dante Alighieri">Escuela Dante Alighieri</a>.</p>
<p>The area once had a German school, Deutsche Schule Cordoba.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Literary activity flourished in the city at the beginning of the last century. Córdoba was the city of <a href="/wiki/Leopoldo_Lugones" title="Leopoldo Lugones">Leopoldo Lugones</a>, Arturo Capdevila and Marcos Aguinis, among many other prestigious writers. Among the city's best-known museums are the <a href="/wiki/Caraffa_Fine_Arts_Museum" title="Caraffa Fine Arts Museum">Caraffa Fine Arts Museum</a>, founded in 1916, and the <a href="/wiki/Evita_Fine_Arts_Museum" title="Evita Fine Arts Museum">Evita Fine Arts Museum</a>, founded in 2007. The <a href="/wiki/Paseo_del_Buen_Pastor" title="Paseo del Buen Pastor">Paseo del Buen Pastor</a>, a cultural center opened in 2007, features an art museum, as well as a shopping gallery devoted to local vintners, cheese makers, leather crafters and other artisans.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The typical music in Córdoba is the <i><a href="/wiki/Cuarteto" title="Cuarteto">cuarteto</a></i>, heard in many parties and pubs. Among the most popular cuarteto singers are <a href="/wiki/La_Mona_Jim%C3%A9nez" title="La Mona Jiménez">Carlos <i>La Mona</i> Jiménez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rodrigo_(musician)" title="Rodrigo (musician)">Rodrigo</a>, La Barra and Jean Carlos. The places they usually sing are named <i>bailes</i> (dances). One of the first groups was <i>Cuarteto de Oro</i>.</p>
<p>Other music styles popular with the youth are <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic music</a> (or <i>electro</i>), as well as <a href="/wiki/Reggaeton" title="Reggaeton">reggaeton</a>. These are commonly played at <i>boliches</i>, as night clubs are known in Argentina. Córdoba is sometimes referred to as "the night life city (or "the city that never sleeps"), because of its wide range of clubs and teenage <i>matinées</i> (dancing clubs).</p>
<p>Córdoba's rich musical culture also encompasses <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, rock and pop, in a variety of venues.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Libertador_Theatre" class="mw-redirect" title="Libertador Theatre">Teatro Libertador San Martín</a> regularly features concerts, operas, folk music, and plays.</p>

<p>Córdoba has many historical monuments left over from the colonial era. In the centre, near the <i>Plaza San Martín</i> square, is the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Jesuit</a> <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba_Cathedral" class="mw-redirect" title="Córdoba Cathedral">Cathedral</a>, whose altar is made of stone and silver from <a href="/wiki/Potos%C3%AD" title="Potosí">Potosí</a>. Every ornament inside is made of gold and the roof is all painted with different images from the Bible. Another important historic building is the <i><a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba_Cabildo" title="Córdoba Cabildo">Cabildo</a></i> (colonial government house), located next to the church. The Jesuit Block, the Monserrat School, the University and the church of the Society of Jesus are also located in Córdoba.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Festivals">Festivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Festivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first festival of the year is in February, the <i><a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">Carnival</a></i>, where children enjoy throwing water balloons at each other on the street.</p>
<p>Then in the middle of the year, on 20 July, <i>Friends Day</i> is celebrated. Usually, most of the teenagers meet at <i>Parque de las Naciones</i> or <i>Parque Sarmiento</i> and spend the afternoon there. At night, they go dancing to different places, and enjoy a drink.</p>
<p>The last festival is <i>Spring Day</i>, held on 21 September, which is Students' Day. Many go to the park or spend the day in the nearby city of <a href="/wiki/Villa_Carlos_Paz" title="Villa Carlos Paz">Villa Carlos Paz</a>. There they can enjoy lots of activities like concerts, dancing, going down town or visiting the river bank.</p>
