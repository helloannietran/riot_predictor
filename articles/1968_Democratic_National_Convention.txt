<p>The <b>1968 Democratic National Convention</b> of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a> was held at the <a href="/wiki/International_Amphitheatre" title="International Amphitheatre">International Amphitheatre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago, Illinois</a>, from August 26 to August 29, 1968. As <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a> had announced he would not seek re-election, the purpose of the convention was to select a new presidential nominee to run as the Democratic Party's candidate for the office.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Keynote_speaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Keynote speaker">keynote speaker</a> was Senator <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Inouye" title="Daniel Inouye">Daniel Inouye</a> (D-Hawaii).<sup id="cite_ref-AP19680827_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AP19680827-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> <a href="/wiki/Hubert_Humphrey" title="Hubert Humphrey">Hubert H. Humphrey</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Muskie" title="Edmund Muskie">Edmund S. Muskie</a> of <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> were nominated for President and Vice President, respectively.</p>
<p>The convention was held during a year of violence, political turbulence, and civil unrest, particularly <a href="/wiki/Mass_racial_violence_in_the_United_States" title="Mass racial violence in the United States">riots</a> in more than 100 cities<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-3">[3]</a></sup> following the assassination of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King,_Jr" class="mw-redirect" title="Martin Luther King, Jr">Martin Luther King, Jr</a>. on April 4.<sup id="cite_ref-Bailey_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bailey-4">[4]</a></sup> The convention also followed the assassination of Democratic presidential hopeful Senator <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" class="mw-redirect" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Assassination of Robert F. Kennedy">on June 5</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Schlesinger.2C_JR._1968_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schlesinger.2C_JR._1968-5">[5]</a></sup> Both Kennedy and Senator <a href="/wiki/Eugene_McCarthy" title="Eugene McCarthy">Eugene McCarthy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a> had been running for the Democratic Nomination at the time.</p>

<p>In 1968 the Democratic Party was divided. Senators Eugene McCarthy and Robert F. Kennedy had entered the campaign in March, challenging Johnson for the Democratic nomination. Johnson, facing dissent within his party, had dropped out of the race on March 31.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Vice President Hubert Humphrey then entered into the race, but did not compete in any primaries, compiling his delegates in caucus states that were controlled by party leaders. After <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Assassination of Robert F. Kennedy">Kennedy's assassination on June 5</a>, the Democratic Party's divisions grew.<sup id="cite_ref-Schlesinger.2C_JR._1968_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schlesinger.2C_JR._1968-5">[5]</a></sup> At the moment of Kennedy's death the delegate count stood at Humphrey 561.5, Kennedy 393.5, McCarthy 258.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Kennedy's murder left his delegates uncommitted.</p>
<p>Support within the party was divided between Senator McCarthy, who ran a decidedly anti-war campaign and was seen as the peace candidate,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and Vice President Humphrey, who was seen as the candidate representing the Johnson point of view.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the end, the Democratic Party nominated Humphrey. Even though 80 percent of the primary voters had been for anti-war candidates, the delegates had defeated the peace plank by 1,567¾ to 1,041¼.<sup id="cite_ref-Gitlin_1987:_331_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gitlin_1987:_331-10">[10]</a></sup> The loss was perceived to be the result of President Johnson and Chicago Mayor <a href="/wiki/Richard_J._Daley" title="Richard J. Daley">Richard Daley</a> influencing behind the scenes.<sup id="cite_ref-Gitlin_1987:_331_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gitlin_1987:_331-10">[10]</a></sup> Humphrey, who had not entered any of 13 state primary elections, won the Democratic nomination, and went on to lose the election to the Republican Richard Nixon.<sup id="cite_ref-Jennings_.26_Brewster_1998:_413_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jennings_.26_Brewster_1998:_413-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>Chicago's mayor, <a href="/wiki/Richard_J._Daley" title="Richard J. Daley">Richard J. Daley</a>, intended to showcase his and the city's achievements to national Democrats and the news media. Instead, the proceedings became notorious for the large number of demonstrators and the use of force by the Chicago police during what was supposed to be, in the words of the <a href="/wiki/Youth_International_Party" title="Youth International Party">Yippie</a> activist organizers, "A Festival of Life."<sup id="cite_ref-Bailey_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bailey-4">[4]</a></sup> Rioting took place between the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Police_Department" title="Chicago Police Department">Chicago Police Department</a>, who were assisted by the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_National_Guard" title="Illinois National Guard">Illinois National Guard</a>, and peaceful demonstrators. The disturbances were well publicized by the mass media, with some journalists and reporters being caught up in the violence. Network newsmen <a href="/wiki/Mike_Wallace" title="Mike Wallace">Mike Wallace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dan_Rather" title="Dan Rather">Dan Rather</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Newman" title="Edwin Newman">Edwin Newman</a> were assaulted by the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Police_Department" title="Chicago Police Department">Chicago police</a> while inside the halls of the Democratic Convention.<sup id="cite_ref-Gitlin_1987:_335_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gitlin_1987:_335-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Democratic Presidential Nominating Convention had been held in <a href="/wiki/1956_Democratic_National_Convention" title="1956 Democratic National Convention">Chicago 12 years earlier</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Farber_1988:_115_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farber_1988:_115-15">[15]</a></sup> Chicago Mayor Richard J. Daley had played an integral role in the election of John F. Kennedy in 1960.<sup id="cite_ref-Farber_1988:_115_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farber_1988:_115-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1968, however, it did not seem that Daley had maintained the clout which would allow him to bring out the voters again to produce a Democratic victory as he had in 1960.</p>
<p>On October 7, 1967, Daley and Johnson had a private meeting at a fundraiser for President Johnson's reelection campaign with an entry fee of one thousand dollars per plate (approximately $7,200.00 in 2016 dollars). During the meeting, Daley explained to the president that there had been a disappointing showing of Democrats in the 1966 congressional races, and the president might lose the <a href="/wiki/Swing_state" title="Swing state">swing state</a> with its twenty-seven electoral votes if the convention were not held in Illinois.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Johnson's pro-war policies had already created a great division within the party, Johnson hoped the selection of Chicago for the convention would eliminate further conflict with opposition.<sup id="cite_ref-Farber_1988:_117_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farber_1988:_117-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Committee head for selecting the location, New Jersey Democrat <a href="/wiki/David_Wilentz" class="mw-redirect" title="David Wilentz">David Wilentz</a>, gave the official reason for choosing Chicago as, "It is centrally located geographically which will reduce transportation costs and because it has been the site of national conventions for both Parties in the past and is therefore attuned to holding them." The conversation between Johnson and Daley was leaked to the press and published in the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> and several other papers.<sup id="cite_ref-Farber_1988:_117_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farber_1988:_117-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dan_Rather_incident">Dan Rather incident</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1968_Democratic_National_Convention&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dan Rather incident">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While trying to interview a Georgia delegate being escorted out of the building, <a href="/wiki/CBS_News" title="CBS News">CBS News</a> correspondent <a href="/wiki/Dan_Rather" title="Dan Rather">Dan Rather</a> was grabbed by security guards and roughed up.<sup id="cite_ref-rather68_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rather68-18">[18]</a></sup> While Rather was reporting from the convention floor,<sup id="cite_ref-rather68_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rather68-18">[18]</a></sup> CBS News anchor <a href="/wiki/Walter_Cronkite" title="Walter Cronkite">Walter Cronkite</a> turned his attention towards the area from where Rather was reporting. Rather was grabbed by security guards after he walked towards a delegate who was being hauled out, and asked him "what is your name, sir?" Rather, who was wearing a microphone headset, was then heard on national television repeatedly saying to the guards "don't push me" and "take your hands off me unless you plan to arrest me".<sup id="cite_ref-rather68_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rather68-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the guards let go of Rather, he then told Cronkite, "Walter... we tried to talk to the man and we got violently pushed out of the way. This is the kind of thing that has been going on outside the hall, this is the first time we've had it happen inside the hall. We... I'm sorry to be out of breath, but somebody belted me in the stomach during that. What happened is a Georgia delegate, at least he had a Georgia delegate sign on, was being hauled out of the hall. We tried to talk to him to see why, who he was, what the situation was, and at that instant the security people, well as you can see, put me on the deck. I didn't do very well."<sup id="cite_ref-rather68_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rather68-18">[18]</a></sup> Cronkite replied, "I think we've got a bunch of thugs here, Dan."</p>

<p>In 1968, the <a href="/wiki/National_Mobilization_Committee_to_End_the_War_in_Vietnam" title="National Mobilization Committee to End the War in Vietnam">National Mobilization Committee to End the War in Vietnam</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Youth_International_Party" title="Youth International Party">Youth International Party (Yippies)</a> had already begun planning a youth festival in Chicago to coincide with the Democratic National Convention. They were not alone, as other groups such as <a href="/wiki/Students_For_a_Democratic_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="Students For a Democratic Society">Students For a Democratic Society</a> would also make their presence known.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> When asked about anti-war demonstrators, Daley repeated to reporters that "no thousands will come to our city and take over our streets, our city, our convention."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> 10,000 demonstrators gathered in Chicago for the convention, where they were met by 23,000 police and National Guardsmen.<sup id="cite_ref-Jennings_.26_Brewster_1998:_413_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jennings_.26_Brewster_1998:_413-11">[11]</a></sup> Daley also thought that one way to prevent demonstrators from coming to Chicago was to refuse to grant permits which would allow for people to protest legally.<sup id="cite_ref-Gitlin_1987:_319_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gitlin_1987:_319-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the violence at the Chicago convention, Daley said his primary reason for calling in so many Guardsmen and police was reports he received indicating the existence of plots to assassinate many of the leaders, including himself.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>While several protests had taken place before serious violence occurred, the events headed by the Yippies were not without satire. Surrounded by reporters on August 23, 1968, Yippie leader <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Rubin" title="Jerry Rubin">Jerry Rubin</a>, folk singer <a href="/wiki/Phil_Ochs" title="Phil Ochs">Phil Ochs</a>, and other activists held their own presidential nominating convention with their candidate <a href="/wiki/Pigasus_(politics)" title="Pigasus (politics)">Pigasus</a>, an actual pig. When the Yippies paraded Pigasus at the Civic Center, ten policemen arrested Ochs, Rubin, Pigasus, and six others. This resulted in a great deal of media attention for Pigasus.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Chicago_police_riot">The Chicago police riot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1968_Democratic_National_Convention&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Chicago police riot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chicago_Police_helmet_%26_billy-club.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Chicago_Police_helmet_%26_billy-club.jpg/200px-Chicago_Police_helmet_%26_billy-club.jpg" width="200" height="134" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Chicago_Police_helmet_%26_billy-club.jpg/300px-Chicago_Police_helmet_%26_billy-club.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Chicago_Police_helmet_%26_billy-club.jpg/400px-Chicago_Police_helmet_%26_billy-club.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3872" data-file-height="2592" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chicago_Police_helmet_%26_billy-club.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Chicago Police helmet and billy club circa 1968 (photographed 2012)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On August 28, 1968, around 10,000 protesters gathered in <a href="/wiki/Grant_Park_(Chicago)" title="Grant Park (Chicago)">Grant Park</a> for the demonstration. At approximately 3:30&#160;p.m., a young man lowered the American flag that was there.<sup id="cite_ref-Gitlin_1987:_331_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gitlin_1987:_331-10">[10]</a></sup> The police broke through the crowd and began beating the young man, while the crowd pelted the police with food, rocks, and chunks of concrete.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The chants of the protesters shifted from "hell no, we won't go" to "pigs are whores".<sup id="cite_ref-Gitlin_1987:_332_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gitlin_1987:_332-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tom_Hayden" title="Tom Hayden">Tom Hayden</a>, one of the leaders of <a href="/wiki/Students_for_a_Democratic_Society_(1960_organization)" class="mw-redirect" title="Students for a Democratic Society (1960 organization)">Students for a Democratic Society</a>, encouraged protesters to move out of the park to ensure that if the police used tear gas on them, it would have to be done throughout the city.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The amount of tear gas used to suppress the protesters was so great that it eventually made its way to the Hilton Hotel, where it disturbed Hubert Humphrey while in his shower.<sup id="cite_ref-Gitlin_1987:_332_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gitlin_1987:_332-25">[25]</a></sup> The police sprayed demonstrators and bystanders with <a href="/wiki/Mace_(spray)" title="Mace (spray)">mace</a> and were taunted by the protesters with chants of "kill, kill, kill".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The police assault in front of the Hilton Hotel the evening of August 28 became the most famous image of the Chicago demonstrations of 1968. The entire event took place live under television lights for seventeen minutes with the crowd chanting, "<a href="/wiki/The_whole_world_is_watching" title="The whole world is watching">The whole world is watching</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Gitlin_1987:_332_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gitlin_1987:_332-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In its report <i>Rights in Conflict</i> (better known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Dan_Walker_(politician)#Early_life_and_career" title="Dan Walker (politician)">Walker Report</a></i>), the Chicago Study Team that investigated the violent clashes between police and protesters at the convention stated that the police response was characterized by "unrestrained and indiscriminate police violence on many occasions, particularly at night. That violence was made all the more shocking by the fact that it was often inflicted upon persons who had broken no law, disobeyed no order, made no threat. These included peaceful demonstrators, onlookers, and large numbers of residents who were simply passing through, or happened to live in, the areas where confrontations were occurring.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Connecticut Senator <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Ribicoff" class="mw-redirect" title="Abraham Ribicoff">Abraham Ribicoff</a> used his nominating speech for <a href="/wiki/George_McGovern" title="George McGovern">George McGovern</a> to report the violence going on outside the convention hall. He reportedly said that "With George McGovern as President of the United States, we wouldn't have to have Gestapo tactics in the streets of Chicago!"<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Mayor Daley responded to his remark with something unintelligible through the television sound, and Ribicoff replied, "How hard it is to accept the truth!" That night, <a href="/wiki/NBC_News" title="NBC News">NBC News</a> had been switching back and forth between images of the violence to the festivities over Humphrey's victory in the convention hall, highlighting the division in the Democratic Party.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the Chicago protests, the demonstrators believed the majority of Americans would side with them over what had happened in Chicago, especially because of police behavior. The controversy over the war in Vietnam overshadowed their cause.<sup id="cite_ref-Gitlin_1987:_335_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gitlin_1987:_335-14">[14]</a></sup> Daley shared he had received 135,000 letters supporting his actions and only 5,000 condemning them. Public opinion polls demonstrated that the majority of Americans supported the Mayor's tactics.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> It was often commented through the popular media that on that evening, America decided to vote for <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Chicago_Eight">The Chicago Eight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1968_Democratic_National_Convention&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Chicago Eight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Seven" title="Chicago Seven">Chicago Seven</a></div>
<p>After Chicago, the Justice Department meted out charges of conspiracy and incitement to riot in connection with the violence at Chicago. This created the Chicago Eight, consisting of protesters <a href="/wiki/Abbie_Hoffman" title="Abbie Hoffman">Abbie Hoffman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hayden" title="Tom Hayden">Tom Hayden</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Dellinger" title="David Dellinger">David Dellinger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rennie_Davis" title="Rennie Davis">Rennie Davis</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Froines" title="John Froines">John Froines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Rubin" title="Jerry Rubin">Jerry Rubin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lee_Weiner" title="Lee Weiner">Lee Weiner</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Seale" title="Bobby Seale">Bobby Seale</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gitlin_1987:_342_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gitlin_1987:_342-33">[33]</a></sup> Demonstrations were held daily during the trial, organized by the <a href="/wiki/National_Mobilization_Committee_to_End_the_War_in_Vietnam" title="National Mobilization Committee to End the War in Vietnam">National Mobilization Committee to End the War in Vietnam</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Young_Lords" title="Young Lords">Young Lords</a>, and the local <a href="/wiki/Black_Panther_Party" title="Black Panther Party">Black Panther Party</a> led by Chairman <a href="/wiki/Fred_Hampton" title="Fred Hampton">Fred Hampton</a>. In February 1970, five of the remaining seven Chicago Conspiracy defendants (Seale's charges had been separated from the rest) were convicted on the charge of intent to incite a riot while crossing state lines, but none were found guilty of conspiracy.</p>
<p>Judge <a href="/wiki/Julius_Hoffman" title="Julius Hoffman">Julius Hoffman</a> sentenced the defendants and their attorneys to unprecedented prison terms ranging from two-and-a-half months to four years for contempt of court. The convictions were eventually reversed on appeal, and the government declined to bring the case to trial again.<sup id="cite_ref-Gitlin_1987:_342_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gitlin_1987:_342-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_McGovern.E2.80.93Fraser_Commission">The McGovern–Fraser Commission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1968_Democratic_National_Convention&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The McGovern–Fraser Commission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/McGovern%E2%80%93Fraser_Commission" title="McGovern–Fraser Commission">McGovern–Fraser Commission</a></div>
<p>In response to the party disunity and electoral failure that came out of the convention, the party established the <b>Commission on Party Structure and Delegate Selection</b> (informally known as the <b>McGovern–Fraser Commission</b>),<sup id="cite_ref-Justice_Learning_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Justice_Learning-34">[34]</a></sup> to examine current rules on the ways candidates were nominated and make recommendations designed to broaden participation and enable better <a href="/wiki/Representation_(politics)" title="Representation (politics)">representation</a> for minorities and others who were underrepresented. The Commission established more open procedures and <a href="/wiki/Affirmative_action" title="Affirmative action">affirmative action</a> guidelines for selecting <a href="/wiki/Delegate" title="Delegate">delegates</a>. In addition the commission made it so that all delegate selection procedures were required to be open; <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">party</a> leaders could no longer handpick the convention delegates in secret.<sup id="cite_ref-ThisNation_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ThisNation-35">[35]</a></sup> An unforeseen result of these rules was a large shift toward state <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_primary" title="United States presidential primary">presidential primaries</a>. Prior to the reforms, <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrats</a> in two-thirds of the states used state conventions to choose convention delegates. In the post-reform era, over three-quarters of the states use primary elections to choose delegates, and over 80% of convention delegates are selected in these primaries.<sup id="cite_ref-kauf_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kauf-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
