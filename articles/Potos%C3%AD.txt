<p><b>Potosí</b> is a city and the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Potos%C3%AD_Department" title="Potosí Department">department of Potosí</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>. It is one of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_highest_cities_in_the_world" title="List of highest cities in the world">highest cities</a> in the world by elevation at a nominal 4,090 metres (13,420&#160;ft)<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> For centuries, it was the location of <a href="/wiki/National_Mint_of_Bolivia" title="National Mint of Bolivia">the Spanish colonial mint</a>.</p>
<p>Potosí lies at the foot of the <i><a href="/wiki/Cerro_Rico" title="Cerro Rico">Cerro de Potosí</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>—sometimes referred to as the <i><a href="/wiki/Cerro_Rico" title="Cerro Rico">Cerro Rico</a></i> ("rich mountain", a <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountain</a> popularly conceived of as being "made of" <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> ore that dominates the city. The Cerro Rico is the reason for Potosí's historical importance, since it was the major supply of silver for Spain during the period of the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a>.</p>
<p>The silver was taken by <a href="/wiki/Llama" title="Llama">llama</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mule_train_(transport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mule train (transport)">mule train</a> to the Pacific coast, shipped north to <a href="/wiki/Panama_City" title="Panama City">Panama City</a>, carried by mule train across the isthmus of Panama to <a href="/wiki/Nombre_de_Dios,_Col%C3%B3n" title="Nombre de Dios, Colón">Nombre de Dios</a> or <a href="/wiki/Portobelo,_Col%C3%B3n" title="Portobelo, Colón">Portobelo</a> whence it was taken to Spain on the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_treasure_fleets" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish treasure fleets">Spanish treasure fleets</a>.</p>
<p>Cerro de Potosí's peak is 4,824 metres (15,827&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above mean sea level">above sea level</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cunningham_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cunningham-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:376</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potos%C3%AD&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Located in the Bolivian Tin Belt, Cerro Rico de Potosi is the world's largest silver deposit and has been mined since the sixteenth century, producing up to 60,000 <a href="/wiki/Tonnes" class="mw-redirect" title="Tonnes">tonnes</a> by 1996. Estimates are that much silver still remains in the mines. Potosi became the second largest city, and the site of the first mint, in the Americas. By 1891, low silver prices prompted the change to mining tin, which continued until 1985. At peak production in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, the <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a> contained up to 40% silver.<sup id="cite_ref-Cunningham_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cunningham-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:374</sup></p>
<p>The ore deposits reside in <a href="/wiki/Vein_(geology)" title="Vein (geology)">veins</a> present in the <a href="/wiki/Dacite" title="Dacite">dacite</a> <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_dome" class="mw-redirect" title="Volcanic dome">volcanic dome</a>. The hill is "honeycombed" with underground workings, reaching from the summit to depths of 1,150 metres (3,770&#160;ft). The conical hill has a reddish-brown <a href="/wiki/Gossan" title="Gossan">gossan</a> cap of iron-oxides and quartz, with grayish-blue altered dacite and many <a href="/wiki/Mine_dump" class="mw-redirect" title="Mine dump">mine dumps</a> below.<sup id="cite_ref-Cunningham_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cunningham-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:374–376</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Basement_rock" class="mw-redirect" title="Basement rock">Basement rocks</a> consist of <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> <a href="/wiki/Clastic_sediment" class="mw-redirect" title="Clastic sediment">clastic sediments</a> consisting of <a href="/wiki/Phyllite" title="Phyllite">phyllite</a> with some <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> <a href="/wiki/Interbedding" title="Interbedding">interbedding</a>. At about 13.8 <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">Ma</a>, the dome was <a href="/wiki/Extrusive_(geology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Extrusive (geology)">extruded</a>. During the explosive process, the Venus <a href="/wiki/Breccia" title="Breccia">breccia</a> formed when the ascending dacite <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> reacted with <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> to produce a <a href="/wiki/Phreatic_eruption" title="Phreatic eruption">phreatic eruption</a>. The released pressure allowed the formation of the Caracoles <a href="/wiki/Tuff_ring" class="mw-redirect" title="Tuff ring">tuff ring</a> on top of the breccia. The magma then extruded outward from a <a href="/wiki/Dike_(geology)" title="Dike (geology)">dike</a> to form a <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_dome" class="mw-redirect" title="Volcanic dome">volcanic dome</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Tuff" title="Tuff">tuff</a>. The dacite dome is 1,700 metres (5,600&#160;ft) by 1,200 metres (3,900&#160;ft) at the surface and narrows down to the 100 metres (330&#160;ft) wide dike at depth. <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_circulation" title="Hydrothermal circulation">Hydrothermal circulation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fracture_(geology)" title="Fracture (geology)">fracturing</a> soon followed, altering the dacite and depositing ore minerals and <a href="/wiki/Gangue" title="Gangue">gangue</a> in the veins.<sup id="cite_ref-Cunningham_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cunningham-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:375,381,384</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="16th_century_silver_boom">16th century silver boom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potos%C3%AD&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 16th century silver boom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Real_Situado" title="Real Situado">Real Situado</a> and <a href="/wiki/Global_silver_trade_from_the_16th_to_18th_centuries" title="Global silver trade from the 16th to 18th centuries">Global silver trade from the 16th to 18th centuries</a></div>
<p>Founded in 1545 as a mining town, it soon produced fabulous wealth, and the population eventually exceeded 200,000 people. The city gave rise to a <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> expression, still in use: <i>vale un Potosí,</i> ("to be worth a Potosí") meaning "to be of great value". The rich mountain, <a href="/wiki/Cerro_Rico" title="Cerro Rico">Cerro Rico</a>, produced an estimated 60% of all silver mined in the world during the second half of the 16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Potosí's naturally rich deposits, along with technological advancements in production like the <a href="/wiki/Patio_process" title="Patio process">Patio process</a>, allowed silver production costs to remain extremely low. In fact, Spanish American mines were the world's cheapest sources of silver during this time period. Spanish America's ability to supply a great amount of silver and China's strong demand for this commodity resulted in a spectacular mining boom. The true champion of this boom in the silver industry was indeed the Spanish crown. By allowing private-sector entrepreneurs to operate mines and placing high taxes on mining profits, the Spanish empire was able to extract the greatest benefits. An example of a tax that was levied includes the <i>quinto</i>, a 20% severance tax on gross value.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> From the raw materials extracted from the mines, the Potosí mint called <a href="/wiki/Spanish_dollar" title="Spanish dollar">pieces of eight</a> were fashioned.</p>
<p>For Europeans, Peru–<a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a> was part of the <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_Peru" title="Viceroyalty of Peru">Viceroyalty of Peru</a> and was known as <i><a href="/wiki/Upper_Peru" title="Upper Peru">Alto Perú</a></i> before becoming independent as part of <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>. Potosi was a mythical land of riches, it is mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Cervantes" title="Miguel de Cervantes">Miguel de Cervantes</a>' famous novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Quixote" title="Don Quixote">Don Quixote</a></i> (second part, chap. LXXI) as a land of "extraordinary richness". One theory holds that the <a href="/wiki/Mint_mark" title="Mint mark">mint mark</a> of Potosí (the letters "PTSI" superimposed on one another) is the origin of the <a href="/wiki/Dollar_sign#Potosi_mint_mark" title="Dollar sign">dollar sign</a>, although the likelier origin of the symbol is the $-shaped scroll-wrapped columns on the reverse of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_dollar" title="Spanish dollar">Spanish dollar</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forced_labor">Forced labor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potos%C3%AD&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Forced labor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Native-American laborers were conscripted and forced to work in Potosí's silver mines through the traditional Incan <i><a href="/wiki/Mita_(Inca)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mita (Inca)">mita</a></i> system of contributed labor. Many of them died due to the harsh conditions of the mine life and natural gases. At such a high altitude, pneumonia was always a concern and mercury poisoning took the lives of many involved in the refining process.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> According to Noble David Cook, "A key factor in understanding the impact of the Potosi mita on the Indians is that mita labor was only one form of work at the mines. A 1603 report stated that of 58,800 Indians working at Potosi, 5100 were mitayos, or less than one in ten. In addition to the mitayos there were 10,500 <i>mingas</i> (contractual workers) and 43,200 free wage earners. Yet mitayos were required to do the work others refused: predominantly the transport of the ore up the shafts to the mouth of the mine."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>From around 1600, the death rate skyrocketed among the local Indian communities. To compensate for the diminishing indigenous labor force, the colonists made a request in 1608 to the Crown in Madrid to begin allowing the importation of 1,500 to 2,000 African slaves per year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> An estimated total of 30,000 African slaves were taken to Potosí during the colonial era. Like the native laborers, they too died in large numbers. African slaves were also forced to work in the <a href="/wiki/National_Mint_of_Bolivia" title="National Mint of Bolivia">Casa de la Moneda</a> (mint) as <i>acémilas humanas</i> (human mules). Since mules would die after a couple of months pushing the mills, the colonists replaced the four mules with twenty African slaves.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Independence_era">Independence era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potos%C3%AD&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Independence era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Bolivian_War_of_Independence" title="Bolivian War of Independence">Bolivian War of Independence</a> (1809–1825), Potosí frequently passed between the control of Royalist and Patriot forces. Major leadership mistakes came when the First Auxiliary Army arrived from <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a> (under the command of <a href="/wiki/Juan_Jos%C3%A9_Castelli" title="Juan José Castelli">Juan José Castelli</a>), this led to an increased sense that Potosi required its own independent government.</p>
<p>When the second auxiliary army arrived, it was received well and the commander, <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Belgrano" title="Manuel Belgrano">Manuel Belgrano</a>, did much to heal the past wounds inflicted by the tyrannical Castelli. When that army was forced to retreat, Belgrano took the calculated decision to blow up the <a href="/wiki/Casa_de_la_Moneda_de_Bolivia" class="mw-redirect" title="Casa de la Moneda de Bolivia">Casa de la Moneda</a>. Since the locals refused to evacuate, the explosion would have resulted in many casualties. The fuse was lit, but disaster was averted by locals who put the fuse out. Two more expeditions from Buenos Aires would seize Potosí.</p>

<p>There is no authoritative etymology for the word "Potosí". According to legend, in about 1462, <a href="/wiki/Huayna_Capac" title="Huayna Capac">Huayna Capac</a>, the eleventh <a href="/wiki/Sapa_Inca" title="Sapa Inca">Sapa Inca</a> of what by then was known as the <a href="/wiki/Inca_Empire" title="Inca Empire">Inca Empire</a> "set out for Ccolque Porco and Andaccaua, the location of his mines from which were taken innumerable <a href="/wiki/Arroba" title="Arroba">arrobas</a> of silver" (an arroba is a Spanish unit of weight equivalent to approximately 25 pounds (11&#160;kg)). Before leaving there, he saw Potosí, and admiring its beauty and grandeur, he said (speaking to those of his Court):</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"This doubtless must have much silver in its heart"; whereby he subsequently ordered his vassals to go to Ccolque Porco ... and work the mines and remove from them all the rich metal. They did so, and having brought their tools of flint and reinforced wood, they climbed the hill; and after having probed for its veins, they were about to open those veins when they heard a frightening thunderous noise which shook the whole hill, and after this, they heard a voice which said: "Do not take the silver from this hill, because it is destined for other masters." Amazed at hearing this reasoning, the Incan vassals desisted in their purpose and returned to Porco and told the king what had happened; relating the occurrence in their own language, on coming to the word noise, they said "Potocsí" which means there was a great thunderous noise, and from that later was derived (corrupting a letter) the name of potosí.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>It is believed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (September 2013)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> that "Potosí" is a <a href="/wiki/Quechua_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Quechua languages">Quechua</a> word.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, in Quechua the phoneme <i>p'otoj</i> does not refer to a thunderous noise, whereas it does in <a href="/wiki/Aymara_language" title="Aymara language">Aymara</a>. Thus, if Potosí encompasses the idea of a thunderous noise, the locution would have an Aymaran root rather than a Quechuan.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The actual sharp structure of the term is contrary to the nature of both Aymara and Quechua. Another explanation, given by several Quechua speakers,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="Statement needs to be more specific about the content to which it refers. (September 2010)">specify</span></a></i>]</sup> is that potoq is an <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeic" class="mw-redirect" title="Onomatopoeic">onomatopoeic</a> word that reproduces the sound of the hammer against the ore, and oral tradition has it that the town derived its name from this word.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potos%C3%AD&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Potosí features a rare climate for a city of its size, due to its extreme elevation at over 4000m. <a href="/wiki/Semiarid" class="mw-redirect" title="Semiarid">Semiarid</a> and with average temperatures in its warmest month sitting right on the 10&#160;°C threshold, the cities climate straddles that of the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_highland_climate" class="mw-redirect" title="Subtropical highland climate">subtropical highland climate</a> (<i>Cwc</i>, according to the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a>), with <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate#Subpolar_variety_.28Cfc.29" title="Oceanic climate">subpolar oceanic</a> characteristics and an <a href="/wiki/Alpine_climate" title="Alpine climate">alpine</a> climate (E). Summers are cool and wet with daily highs rarely rising above 20&#160;°C, while winters feature cooler days with much colder nights averaging −4&#160;°C. These low temperatures are a result of the extreme precipitation deficit during the winter months with the resulting aridity leading to an increased diurnal temperature variation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potos%C3%AD&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Potosí is home to football team <a href="/wiki/Club_Bamin_Real_Potos%C3%AD" class="mw-redirect" title="Club Bamin Real Potosí">Club Bamin Real Potosí</a>, who play their matches at the 32,000-capacity multi-purpose stadium <a href="/wiki/Estadio_V%C3%ADctor_Agust%C3%ADn_Ugarte" title="Estadio Víctor Agustín Ugarte">Estadio Víctor Agustín Ugarte</a>, one of the highest stadia in the world.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potos%C3%AD&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city is served by <a href="/wiki/Captain_Nicolas_Rojas_Airport" title="Captain Nicolas Rojas Airport">Aeropuerto Capitán Nicolas Rojas</a>, with commercial airline flights by <a href="/wiki/Boliviana_de_Aviaci%C3%B3n" title="Boliviana de Aviación">Boliviana de Aviación</a>, Bolivia's flag air carrier.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potos%C3%AD&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city of <a href="/wiki/San_Luis_Potos%C3%AD,_San_Luis_Potos%C3%AD" class="mw-redirect" title="San Luis Potosí, San Luis Potosí">San Luis Potosí</a> in Mexico was named after Potosí in Bolivia. In the United States, the name Potosi was optimistically given to lead-mining towns of <a href="/wiki/Potosi,_Wisconsin" title="Potosi, Wisconsin">Potosi, Wisconsin</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Potosi,_Missouri" title="Potosi, Missouri">Potosi, Missouri</a>, and also to the silver-mining town of <a href="/wiki/Potosi,_Nevada" title="Potosi, Nevada">Potosi, Nevada</a>.</p>
