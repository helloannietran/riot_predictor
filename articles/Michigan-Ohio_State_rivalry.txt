<p>The <b>Michigan–Ohio State football rivalry</b>, known as <b>The Game</b> by some followers,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> is an American <a href="/wiki/College_football" title="College football">college football</a> <a href="/wiki/College_rivalry" title="College rivalry">rivalry</a> game played annually by the <a href="/wiki/Michigan_Wolverines_football" title="Michigan Wolverines football">Wolverines</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_Buckeyes_football" title="Ohio State Buckeyes football">Buckeyes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University" title="Ohio State University">Ohio State University</a>. It has attracted particular national interest over the last four decades as most of the games have determined the <a href="/wiki/Big_Ten_Conference" title="Big Ten Conference">Big Ten Conference</a> title and the resulting <a href="/wiki/Rose_Bowl_Game" title="Rose Bowl Game">Rose Bowl</a> match ups, and many have influenced the outcome of the <a href="/wiki/College_football_national_championships_in_NCAA_Division_I_FBS" title="College football national championships in NCAA Division I FBS">national college football championship</a>. The game was ranked by <a href="/wiki/ESPN" title="ESPN">ESPN</a> in 2000 as the greatest North American sports rivalry.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The two <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwest</a> state schools first met in 1897, and the rivalry has been renewed annually since 1918. The game has been played at the end of the regular season since 1935 (with exceptions in 1942, 1986, and 1998). Since 1918, the game's site has alternated between <a href="/wiki/Columbus,_Ohio" title="Columbus, Ohio">Columbus, Ohio</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ann_Arbor,_Michigan" title="Ann Arbor, Michigan">Ann Arbor, Michigan</a> (Michigan hosts it in odd years and Ohio State in even years), and has been played in <a href="/wiki/Ohio_Stadium" title="Ohio Stadium">Ohio Stadium</a> since 1922 and <a href="/wiki/Michigan_Stadium" title="Michigan Stadium">Michigan Stadium</a> since 1927. Through 2010, Ohio State and Michigan have decided the <a href="/wiki/Big_Ten_Conference" title="Big Ten Conference">Big Ten Conference</a> championship between themselves on 22 different occasions, and have affected the determination of the conference title an additional 27 times.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>For many years, the game has aired on <a href="/wiki/ESPN_College_Football_on_ABC" title="ESPN College Football on ABC">ABC</a>, often in the 12 PM time slot.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Series_history">Series history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michigan%E2%80%93Ohio_State_football_rivalry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Series history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years_.281897.E2.80.931949.29">Early years (1897–1949)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michigan%E2%80%93Ohio_State_football_rivalry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years (1897–1949)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When the University of Michigan and Ohio State met for the first time in 1897, the <a href="/wiki/Toledo_War" title="Toledo War">Toledo War</a> was within the memories of some still living, and the short-lived border war fueled the rivalry between the two opposing teams. The inaugural game, held at Ann Arbor, resulted in a lopsided victory for Michigan, with the Wolverines posting a 34–0 win over Ohio State's Buckeyes.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> The teams did not meet in 1898 or 1899, but played again in 1900.</p>
<p>The first game foretold a long Michigan winning streak, with Michigan winning or tying every match from 1897 to 1912 and thereby compiling a 12–0–2 record before the contest was postponed for several years. The Ohio State <a href="/wiki/Alma_Mater" class="mw-redirect" title="Alma Mater">Alma Mater</a> "<a href="/wiki/Carmen_Ohio" title="Carmen Ohio">Carmen Ohio</a>" was written on the train ride home to Columbus following the 1902 contest, which saw Ohio State losing to Michigan 86–0. The lyrics and melody (Spanish Chant) have remained largely unchanged since its conception.</p>
<p>Ohio State became a member of the Big Ten Conference in 1912. In 1917, Michigan rejoined the conference after a ten-year absence. In 1918, the teams played their first conference matchup, with Michigan prevailing 14–0 and lodging its eleventh shutout over the Buckeyes. The rivalry has been renewed annually every year since then. In 1919, the Buckeyes (led by legendary halfback <a href="/wiki/Chic_Harley" title="Chic Harley">Chic Harley</a>) won their first game in the series, beating the Wolverines 13–3. The Buckeyes won the following two contests as well, to bring the series record to 13–3–2.</p>

<p>Harley's prowess spurred the university to campaign to build a stadium for Ohio State football. The stadium was completed in 1922, and the first of many historic games in <a href="/wiki/Ohio_Stadium" title="Ohio Stadium">Ohio Stadium</a> took place on October 21, 1922, the day the stadium was dedicated in Columbus. In front of a record 71,000 fans, the Wolverines posted another shutout of the home team Buckeyes, 19–0. According to lore, there was a wager on the outcome of this game, and yellow flowers on a blue background still exist today in the upper part of the stadium's rotunda.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> Michigan won the next five games before OSU picked up the final two victories of the decade. At the end of the 1920s, the series stood at 19–5–2 in favor of Michigan.</p>

<p>Michigan won three of four contests between 1930 and 1933, claiming the national championship twice. In 1934, <a href="/wiki/Francis_Schmidt" title="Francis Schmidt">Francis Schmidt</a> came on as the head coach for Ohio State. The team had lost nine of the previous 12 Michigan-OSU contests, and when a reporter asked Schmidt if Ohio State could beat Michigan that year, he replied, "Of course we can win, Michigan puts their pants on one leg at a time just like we do". The Buckeyes thereupon ran off four straight shutout victories against Michigan, outscoring the Wolverines 114–0 from 1934 to 1937. Schmidt's quote spawned an OSU tradition—since 1934, every Ohio State player receives a gold pants pendant after a victory against Michigan.</p>
<p>Michigan won the three games from 1938 to 1940. The 1940 game, won by Michigan, 40–0, was the benchmark performance of what some consider to be the greatest Michigan team in history, and was the final collegiate game of the legendary <a href="/wiki/Halfback_(American_football)" title="Halfback (American football)">tailback</a> tandem of <a href="/wiki/Tom_Harmon" title="Tom Harmon">Tom Harmon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Kromer" title="Paul Kromer">Paul Kromer</a>.</p>
<p>In 1941, Michigan and Ohio State met for the first time with each team ranked in the <a href="/wiki/AP_Poll" title="AP Poll">AP Poll</a>, which had started in 1936. The 14th-ranked Buckeyes played the 5th-ranked Wolverines to a 20–20 tie in Ann Arbor. Five more times during the 1940s, the teams were both ranked for their annual matchup. Michigan won five of the next seven games before playing to their second tie of the decade in 1949. The series record stood at 30–12–4 at the mid-century mark.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Snow_Bowl.22_and_Woody_Hayes_.281950.E2.80.9368.29">"Snow Bowl" and Woody Hayes (1950–68)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michigan%E2%80%93Ohio_State_football_rivalry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: &quot;Snow Bowl&quot; and Woody Hayes (1950–68)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the more famous games in the rivalry is the 1950 contest, colloquially known as the <a href="/wiki/Snow_Bowl_(1950)" title="Snow Bowl (1950)">Snow Bowl</a>. Eighth-ranked Ohio State, coached by <a href="/wiki/Wes_Fesler" title="Wes Fesler">Wes Fesler</a>, was scheduled to host the game on November 25 in Columbus amidst one of the worst blizzards on Ohio record. The Buckeyes, who led the Big Ten, were granted the option to cancel the game against Michigan, which would have, by default, given the Buckeyes the Big Ten title outright. Ohio State refused, and the game was set to be played. Amid howling snow and wind, in a famous example of a "field position" game, the teams exchanged 45 <a href="/wiki/Punt_(gridiron_football)" title="Punt (gridiron football)">punts</a>, often on <a href="/wiki/First_down" class="mw-redirect" title="First down">first down</a>, in hopes that the other team would fumble the ball near or into their own end zone. Ohio State's <a href="/wiki/Vic_Janowicz" title="Vic Janowicz">Vic Janowicz</a>, who would claim the <a href="/wiki/Heisman_Trophy" title="Heisman Trophy">Heisman Trophy</a> that year, punted 21 times for 685 yards and also kicked a field goal in the first quarter for the Buckeyes' only points. Michigan capitalized on two blocked punts, booting one out of the back of the end zone for a <a href="/wiki/Safety_(American_football_score)" class="mw-redirect" title="Safety (American football score)">safety</a> and recovering another one in the end zone for a <a href="/wiki/Touchdown" title="Touchdown">touchdown</a> just before halftime. Despite failing to gain a single first down or complete a single <a href="/wiki/Forward_pass" title="Forward pass">forward pass</a>, Michigan gained a 9–3 victory, securing the Big Ten title and a Rose Bowl berth. Heavy criticism of Fesler's play calling led to his resignation and the hiring of <a href="/wiki/Woody_Hayes" title="Woody Hayes">Woody Hayes</a> as his successor.</p>
<p>Between 1951 and 1968 under Hayes, the Buckeyes won 12 of 18 contests, including a 1957 victory in Michigan Stadium, the first game in the series attended by over 100,000 fans. In 1958, Ohio State had a 20–14 lead towards the end of the game. On the final play, Michigan fullback Gene Sisinyak ran the ball from the one-yard line for what might have been a game winning touchdown, but Ohio State defensive tackle <a href="/wiki/Dick_Schafrath" title="Dick Schafrath">Dick Schafrath</a> hit Sisinyak, forcing a fumble. In the 1968 game, Ohio State won 50–14, outscoring its foe 29–0 in the second half and attempting an unsuccessful two-point conversion attempt on its final touchdown. When asked why he went for two points with an already insurmountable 50–14 lead, Woody Hayes is rumored to have said, "Because I couldn't go for three." The victory gave top-ranked Ohio State the Big Ten title for the first time in seven years en route to an AP <a href="/wiki/College_football_national_championships_in_NCAA_Division_I_FBS" title="College football national championships in NCAA Division I FBS">national championship</a>. The Buckeyes had also narrowed the series margin to 37–24–4.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Ten-Year_War.22:_Hayes_vs._Bo_Schembechler_.281969.E2.80.9378.29">"Ten-Year War": Hayes vs. Bo Schembechler (1969–78)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michigan%E2%80%93Ohio_State_football_rivalry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: &quot;Ten-Year War&quot;: Hayes vs. Bo Schembechler (1969–78)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Ten_Year_War" title="The Ten Year War">The Ten Year War</a></div>
<p>Wolverines coach <a href="/wiki/Bump_Elliott" title="Bump Elliott">Bump Elliott</a> resigned after the 1968 loss and Michigan hired <a href="/wiki/Miami_RedHawks_Football" class="mw-redirect" title="Miami RedHawks Football">Miami (Ohio)</a> head coach <a href="/wiki/Bo_Schembechler" title="Bo Schembechler">Bo Schembechler</a>, who had previously been an assistant at Ohio State under Hayes, to revitalize its football program. On <a href="/wiki/1969_Michigan_vs._Ohio_State_football_game" class="mw-redirect" title="1969 Michigan vs. Ohio State football game">November 22, 1969</a>, Hayes led his top-ranked Buckeyes into Michigan Stadium to face Schembechler's Wolverines in the first matchup between two coaches who would come to define the rivalry between the two programs. The Buckeyes brought a 22-game winning streak into Ann Arbor, but behind an inspiring 60-yard punt return by Barry Pierson that set up a Wolverine touchdown in the second quarter, and a defense that intercepted Ohio State six times (three by Pierson), the Wolverines won a defensive battle (both teams were scoreless in the second half) for a 24–12 upset.</p>
<p>The contest was the first in the famous "Ten-Year War" between Hayes and Schembechler, which pitted some of OSU's and UM's strongest teams against one another. Four times between 1970 and 1975, Ohio State and Michigan were both ranked in the top five of the AP Poll before their matchup. The Wolverines entered every game during those years undefeated and won only once, a 10–7 victory in Ann Arbor on November 20, 1971. The Michigan graduating class of 1975 shared or won the Big Ten championship every season, yet went to the Rose Bowl only once, in 1972. They only lost or tied with Ohio State during the regular season in that period.</p>
<p>In 1973, both teams entered undefeated, with the winner guaranteed a trip to the Rose Bowl. The rivals played to a 10–10 tie in Ann Arbor on November 24, and the athletic directors of the other Big Ten institutions were forced to vote on the Big Ten representative for the bowl game. In a secret ballot, Ohio State won the vote, to the outrage of Michigan athletic officials and fans. Schembechler argued that Michigan was robbed of its on-field achievements, and for months afterward, Ohio State newspapers were flooded with angry Wolverine letters and threats of lawsuits.</p>
<p>Woody coined the phrase "That state up north" and "That team up north", so he would not have to say the word "Michigan". He was famous for his intense hatred of all things Michigan and according to legend, once refused to get gas in an empty tank, saying: "No, goddammit! We do NOT pull in and fill up. And I'll tell you exactly why we don't. It's because I don't buy one goddam drop of gas in the state of Michigan! We'll coast and PUSH this goddam car to the Ohio line before I give this state a nickel of my money!"<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the "Ten-Year War," Ohio State and Michigan shared the Big Ten title six times. Between 1976 and 1978, Michigan won the game each year, and Ohio State failed to score a touchdown in each of those contests. Woody Hayes was fired at the end of the 1978 season after punching an opposing player during the <a href="/wiki/1978_Gator_Bowl" title="1978 Gator Bowl">Gator Bowl</a>, which ended the "War." The 1978 game was won by Michigan, 14–3, giving Schembechler a record of 5–4–1 against Hayes. At the end of the Hayes tenure, the series stood at 42–28–5.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Schembechler_vs._Earle_Bruce_.281979.E2.80.9387.29">Schembechler vs. Earle Bruce (1979–87)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michigan%E2%80%93Ohio_State_football_rivalry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Schembechler vs. Earle Bruce (1979–87)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Earle_Bruce" title="Earle Bruce">Earle Bruce</a> took over for Hayes and led the Buckeyes to a 5–4 record against Schembechler's Wolverines between 1979 and 1987, perhaps the most balanced stretch of the rivalry, during which neither team won more than two consecutive games. In 1987, Bruce was fired in the week before the Michigan game due to a poor season record, but was allowed to coach anyway, and the inspired Buckeyes (each wearing a sweatband labeled "Earle") won an upset over the heavily favored Wolverines. After the game, Bo Schembechler told Bruce, "I always mind losing to Ohio State but I didn't mind so much today."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> After 1987, the series stood at 46–33–5 in favor of UM.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="John_Cooper_era_.281988.E2.80.932000.29">John Cooper era (1988–2000)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michigan%E2%80%93Ohio_State_football_rivalry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: John Cooper era (1988–2000)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 13 games during <a href="/wiki/John_Cooper_(American_football)" title="John Cooper (American football)">John Cooper</a>'s tenure as Buckeye coach were dominated by Michigan, as the Wolverines went 10–2–1 during the stretch. Schembechler coached Michigan through the 1989 season and then turned over the reins to one of his assistants, <a href="/wiki/Gary_Moeller" title="Gary Moeller">Gary Moeller</a>, who led the team for five seasons before another longtime Michigan assistant, <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_Carr" title="Lloyd Carr">Lloyd Carr</a>, became the head coach in 1995.</p>
<p>The most notorious matchups of the era took place in 1993, 1995, and 1996, in which Ohio State entered the game each year undefeated. The Buckeyes had a 9–0–1 record heading into the 1993 game and were looking to claim an outright Big Ten title against a Michigan team that had already lost four times. Michigan receiver <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Hayes" title="Mercury Hayes">Mercury Hayes</a> and running backs <a href="/wiki/Jon_Ritchie" title="Jon Ritchie">Jon Ritchie</a>, Che Foster, and Ed Davis each scored a touchdown as the Wolverines shocked the Buckeyes, 28–0. After the game, Cooper said: "This is one of the most embarrassing games I've ever been involved with." "They outplayed us on offense, on defense, and in the kicking game. If you'd told me we would come up here and get beat 28–0, I'd have probably stayed home."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1995, #2 Ohio State was led by eventual <a href="/wiki/Heisman_Trophy" title="Heisman Trophy">Heisman Trophy</a> winner <a href="/wiki/Eddie_George" title="Eddie George">Eddie George</a> and future <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> (NFL) stars <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Pace" title="Orlando Pace">Orlando Pace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terry_Glenn" title="Terry Glenn">Terry Glenn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Vrabel" title="Mike Vrabel">Mike Vrabel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shawn_Springs" title="Shawn Springs">Shawn Springs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rickey_Dudley" title="Rickey Dudley">Rickey Dudley</a>. Glenn insisted there wasn't anything special about the Wolverines: "Michigan's nothing," he said.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> Perhaps inspired by this remark, the Wolverines manhandled the Buckeyes at the line of scrimmage from the very first play. Michigan senior running back <a href="/wiki/Tim_Biakabutuka" title="Tim Biakabutuka">Tim Biakabutuka</a> amassed 313 yards rushing in Michigan's 31–23 upset.</p>
<p>The Buckeyes had high expectations again entering the 1996 contest. They boasted an unblemished 10–0 record and were ranked #2 in the nation as they entered the finale with 7–3 Michigan. When Ohio State jumped to a 9–0 halftime lead, the OSU crowd sensed a special finish and perhaps a rise to #1. The Wolverines' defense shut the Buckeyes out in the second half while <a href="/wiki/Brian_Griese" title="Brian Griese">Brian Griese</a> replaced the struggling <a href="/wiki/Scott_Dreisbach" title="Scott Dreisbach">Scott Dreisbach</a> and led Michigan to 13 unanswered points and another victory over their rivals, 13–9. The game would turn out to be the Buckeyes' only loss of the season and ended up costing them a chance of the national championship.</p>
<p>In 1997, Ohio State hoped to return the favor: the 10–0 Wolverines sat atop the AP Poll entering their matchup with the 10–1 Buckeyes, who were ranked #4. Spearheaded by the play of eventual Heisman winner <a href="/wiki/Charles_Woodson" title="Charles Woodson">Charles Woodson</a>, who ran a punt back for a touchdown, intercepted a pass in the Ohio State end zone, and caught a 37-yard pass that set up freshman running back <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Thomas_(American_football)" title="Anthony Thomas (American football)">Anthony Thomas</a>' touchdown run, the Wolverines prevailed, 20–14. The Wolverines then defeated <a href="/wiki/1997_Washington_State_Cougars_football_team" title="1997 Washington State Cougars football team">Washington State</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1998_Rose_Bowl" title="1998 Rose Bowl">Rose Bowl</a> by a 21–16 score, winning their first national championship since 1948.</p>
<p>Ohio State came back with a win in the 1998 contest, but Michigan went on to win in 1999 and 2000. Senior quarterback <a href="/wiki/Tom_Brady" title="Tom Brady">Tom Brady</a> hit sophomore receiver <a href="/wiki/Marquise_Walker" title="Marquise Walker">Marquise Walker</a> for the game-winning touchdown pass with five minutes to go to for a 24–17 victory in 1999. In the 2000 game, Michigan grabbed a 31–12 lead and held on to win, 38–26. Michigan junior quarterback <a href="/wiki/Drew_Henson" title="Drew Henson">Drew Henson</a> went 14 for 25 passing for 303 yards and three scores and added a touchdown run. At the end of the 2000 season, Cooper was fired. While he consistently fielded strong teams, his 2–10–1 record against Michigan, including his failure to ever win in Ann Arbor, was, along with disciplinary problems and a losing record in bowl games, a major contributor to his dismissal. Michigan students held a "John Cooper Day" celebration in Ann Arbor on February 10, 2001 in mock celebration of Cooper's record in the rivalry.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enter_Jim_Tressel_.282001.E2.80.9307.29">Enter Jim Tressel (2001–07)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michigan%E2%80%93Ohio_State_football_rivalry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Enter Jim Tressel (2001–07)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 2001, <a href="/wiki/Youngstown_State_Penguins" title="Youngstown State Penguins">Youngstown State</a> head football coach <a href="/wiki/Jim_Tressel" title="Jim Tressel">Jim Tressel</a> took over as Buckeye head coach. Unlike his predecessor John Cooper, Tressel put a special emphasis on the rivalry. In his introductory speech at halftime of a January basketball game, against Michigan, he said "I can assure you that you will be proud of our young people, in the classroom, in the community, and most especially in 310 days in Ann Arbor, Michigan on the football field." In his first year, Tressel registered the Buckeyes' first defeat of the Wolverines in Ann Arbor in 14 years, by a 26–20 score. Led by senior running back Jonathan Wells, the Buckeyes raced to a 23–0 halftime lead. With Wells out, Michigan mounted a second half comeback that fell just short. The next year, Tressel achieved what Cooper could not: Beating Michigan in consecutive years with a 14–9 victory. The game was decided on the last play when defensive back <a href="/wiki/Will_Allen_(American_football_safety)" class="mw-redirect" title="Will Allen (American football safety)">Will Allen</a> intercepted a pass at the two yard line as time expired to clinch the victory. The Buckeyes were led by freshman running back <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Clarett" title="Maurice Clarett">Maurice Clarett</a>, who ran for 119 yards and one touchdown. He also had a key reception to set up Maurice Hall's game-winning score. The Buckeyes went on to win the national championship that season, as they defeated <a href="/wiki/2002_Miami_Hurricanes_football_team" title="2002 Miami Hurricanes football team">Miami</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2003_Fiesta_Bowl" title="2003 Fiesta Bowl">Fiesta Bowl</a>.</p>
<p>In 2003, Michigan struck back and won the 100th meeting between the historical rivals by a score of 35–21 in Ann Arbor. Senior running back <a href="/wiki/Chris_Perry_(American_football)" title="Chris Perry (American football)">Chris Perry</a>, a Heisman finalist, had 154 yards rushing and two touchdowns to lead the Wolverines to the victory. <a href="/wiki/Braylon_Edwards" title="Braylon Edwards">Braylon Edwards</a> contributed seven catches for 130 yards and two big touchdowns. The game's attendance was 112,118, the largest crowd ever for an NCAA football game.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> In 2004, the 6–4 Buckeyes defeated the heavily favored 9–1 Wolverines, 37–21, behind the leadership of quarterback <a href="/wiki/Troy_Smith" title="Troy Smith">Troy Smith</a> and true freshman receiver <a href="/wiki/Ted_Ginn,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Ted Ginn, Jr.">Ted Ginn, Jr.</a> The Buckeyes added another win in the 2005 game by overcoming a 21–12 deficit with less than eight minutes in the game. In the closing minutes of the game, the Buckeye offense scored two touchdowns to claim a 25–21 victory. Smith threw for 300 yards and completed 73% of his passes. Ginn had a game high nine catches for 89 yards.</p>

<p>On November 18, 2006, Ohio State and Michigan met for their annual showdown, each carrying an 11–0 record. For the first time in the history of the rivalry, the two rivals faced off while holding the top two spots in the <a href="/wiki/Bowl_Championship_Series" title="Bowl Championship Series">Bowl Championship Series</a> rankings. Ohio State won the game by a score of 42–39 and became the outright Big Ten champion, earning the right to play for a national championship at the <a href="/wiki/BCS_National_Championship_Game_2007" class="mw-redirect" title="BCS National Championship Game 2007">BCS National Championship Game</a> in <a href="/wiki/Glendale,_Arizona" title="Glendale, Arizona">Glendale, Arizona</a>. Michigan struck first with a touchdown run by junior running back <a href="/wiki/Mike_Hart_(American_football)" title="Mike Hart (American football)">Mike Hart</a>, but the Buckeyes then scored 21 unanswered points, and at halftime, they were up 28–14. Thanks to an interception and a fumble recovery by junior defensive tackle <a href="/wiki/Alan_Branch" title="Alan Branch">Alan Branch</a>, Michigan made it 35–31 Ohio State with 14 minutes to go in the fourth quarter. But after appearing to have forced Ohio State into a fourth-down situation with six minutes to go, junior outside linebacker <a href="/wiki/Shawn_Crable" title="Shawn Crable">Shawn Crable</a> was called for <a href="/wiki/Roughing_the_passer" title="Roughing the passer">roughing the passer</a>, giving the Buckeyes a fresh set of downs. Ohio State quarterback Troy Smith then passed to <a href="/wiki/Brian_Robiskie" title="Brian Robiskie">Brian Robiskie</a> for a touchdown, increasing the Buckeyes' lead to 42–31 with five minutes remaining in the game. After Ohio State was called for pass interference on a failed fourth-down attempt, giving Michigan an automatic first down, junior quarterback <a href="/wiki/Chad_Henne" title="Chad Henne">Chad Henne</a> found senior tight end <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyler_Ecker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tyler Ecker (page does not exist)">Tyler Ecker</a> for a 16-yard touchdown with two minutes to go to cut the OSU lead to 42–37. Senior wide receiver <a href="/wiki/Steve_Breaston" title="Steve Breaston">Steve Breaston</a> caught the two-point conversion to bring the Wolverines within a field goal. Michigan needed to recover the ensuing onside kick, but they failed to do so. The Buckeyes ran out the clock for the victory, and a trip to the BCS national championship game. Troy Smith completed 71% of his passes for 316 yards and four touchdowns, essentially clinching the Heisman trophy. Ginn caught eight passes for 104 yards and a touchdown. Ohio State running back <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Pittman" title="Antonio Pittman">Antonio Pittman</a> ran for 139 yards on 18 carries for a 7.7 yards-per-carry average. Michigan running back Mike Hart carried the ball 23 times for 142 yards and three touchdowns against a stout Buckeye defense. Chad Henne had 267 yards, two touchdowns, and no turnovers on a 60% completion percentage. Neither performance was, however, sufficient to turn the tide in favor of the Wolverines. The game was highly touted by ESPN/ABC (there was a game countdown clock for a week before kickoff) and was viewed by the largest television audience for a regular-season college football game since 1993, averaging 21.8 million viewers.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> The victory marked the first time in 43 years that the Buckeyes had won three consecutive games in the series. The game gained even more significance when, on the eve of the meeting, Michigan head coach and former Ohio State assistant coach Bo Schembechler died. Schembechler was honored with a video tribute at Ohio Stadium as well as a moment of silence before kickoff.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> Half an hour after the game ended, the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_Lottery" title="Ohio Lottery">Ohio Lottery</a> PICK 4 evening drawing was 4–2–3–9, matching the final score of the game and paying out up to $5,000 per winner, for a total payout of $2.2 million.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the game, there was a chance of a rematch in the <a href="/wiki/2007_BCS_National_Championship_Game" title="2007 BCS National Championship Game">2007 BCS National Championship Game</a>, but <a href="/wiki/2006_Florida_Gators_football_team" title="2006 Florida Gators football team">Florida</a> was chosen over Michigan to be Ohio State's opponent.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea_change_at_Michigan_.282008.E2.80.9310.29">Sea change at Michigan (2008–10)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michigan%E2%80%93Ohio_State_football_rivalry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sea change at Michigan (2008–10)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:20081122_Brandon_Graham_and_Terrell_Pryor.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/20081122_Brandon_Graham_and_Terrell_Pryor.jpg/220px-20081122_Brandon_Graham_and_Terrell_Pryor.jpg" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/20081122_Brandon_Graham_and_Terrell_Pryor.jpg/330px-20081122_Brandon_Graham_and_Terrell_Pryor.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/20081122_Brandon_Graham_and_Terrell_Pryor.jpg/440px-20081122_Brandon_Graham_and_Terrell_Pryor.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2323" data-file-height="1857" /></a>

<p>Lloyd Carr retired as coach at Michigan following the 2007 season and another loss to Ohio State. Tressel had compiled a 6–1 record against Carr's Michigan teams, leaving Carr with a 6–7 career record against Ohio State. In December 2007, Michigan hired <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia_Mountaineers_football" title="West Virginia Mountaineers football">West Virginia</a> head coach <a href="/wiki/Rich_Rodriguez" title="Rich Rodriguez">Rich Rodriguez</a> to take over the football program. Rodriguez, known for his expertise in the spread offense, represented a significant departure from the Wolverines' traditional offensive style. Furthermore, both Carr and his predecessor Gary Moeller had been apprenticed by the now-legendary Schembechler, and Rodriguez's hiring marked the first time in 40 years that a Michigan football team would not be coached by a member of the "Schembechler school."</p>
<p>The 2008 game, Rodriguez's first against the Buckeyes, featured an Ohio State team that needed a win to secure at least a share of the Big Ten championship for the fourth straight year. Michigan by contrast entered the game with a 3–8 record, having already suffered more losses than in any other season in its history. The Buckeyes posted a 42–7 win. OSU scored its largest margin of victory over Michigan since 1968 and pushed its winning streak against the Wolverines to five games, their longest in the rivalry's history.</p>
<p>Ohio State beat Michigan, 21–10, in the 2009 game to extend their winning streak against Michigan to six games and improve Jim Tressel's record versus Michigan to 8–1. Ohio State wore <a href="/wiki/Throwback_uniform" title="Throwback uniform">throwback uniforms</a> to commemorate their <a href="/wiki/1954_Ohio_State_Buckeyes_football_team" title="1954 Ohio State Buckeyes football team">1954 national championship team</a>. The 2009 meeting also saw Buckeye <a href="/wiki/Guard_(American_football)" class="mw-redirect" title="Guard (American football)">guard</a> <a href="/wiki/Justin_Boren" title="Justin Boren">Justin Boren</a>, who had transferred to Ohio State from Michigan in 2008, become the third player in school history to play for both teams (<a href="/wiki/J._T._White" title="J. T. White">J. T. White</a> and <a href="/wiki/Howard_Yerges" title="Howard Yerges">Howard Yerges, Jr.</a> being the others)<sup id="cite_ref-LwlMtpfOSi2_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LwlMtpfOSi2-22">[21]</a></sup> and only the second to play for both teams in the rivalry game. In 2010, Ohio State again prevailed, 37–7.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A_Carousel_of_Coaches_.282011.E2.80.93present.29">A Carousel of Coaches (2011–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michigan%E2%80%93Ohio_State_football_rivalry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: A Carousel of Coaches (2011–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ohio_State_vs._Michigan_football_2011_04_(Michigan_on_offense).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Ohio_State_vs._Michigan_football_2011_04_%28Michigan_on_offense%29.jpg/220px-Ohio_State_vs._Michigan_football_2011_04_%28Michigan_on_offense%29.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Ohio_State_vs._Michigan_football_2011_04_%28Michigan_on_offense%29.jpg/330px-Ohio_State_vs._Michigan_football_2011_04_%28Michigan_on_offense%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Ohio_State_vs._Michigan_football_2011_04_%28Michigan_on_offense%29.jpg/440px-Ohio_State_vs._Michigan_football_2011_04_%28Michigan_on_offense%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a>

<p>Rodriguez was fired following Michigan's 2010 season, ending Michigan's flirtation with the spread—and with non-"Michigan Man" coaches. Rodriguez was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Brady_Hoke" title="Brady Hoke">Brady Hoke</a>, who served as Michigan's defensive line coach from 1995 to 2002. Hoke held head coaching positions at <a href="/wiki/Ball_State_Cardinals_football" title="Ball State Cardinals football">Ball State</a> from 2003 to 2008 and <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_State_Aztecs_football" title="San Diego State Aztecs football">San Diego State</a> from 2009 to 2010 before returning to Michigan. Meanwhile, in Columbus, the Ohio State football program came under NCAA investigation in early 2011 for an incident in which several prominent players were discovered to have traded memorabilia for tattoos. Evidence surfaced that Tressel had known about the situation but had not reported it to school compliance officials, and that the abuses were more widespread and longstanding than originally reported.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> On May 30, 2011, Tressel resigned as head coach and former Buckeye player and assistant coach <a href="/wiki/Luke_Fickell" title="Luke Fickell">Luke Fickell</a> was appointed interim head coach for the <a href="/wiki/2011_Ohio_State_Buckeyes_football_team" title="2011 Ohio State Buckeyes football team">2011 season</a>. In July 2011, in response to the ongoing NCAA investigation, Ohio State vacated all wins from the 2010 season, including the win over Michigan, leaving Tressel with a final record against Michigan of 8–1, with a win streak of six.<sup id="cite_ref-Dispatch_Vacated_2010_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dispatch_Vacated_2010-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Michigan won the 2011 meeting, 40–34, which was the first between two first-year coaches since the 1929 match-up of <a href="/wiki/Harry_Kipke" title="Harry Kipke">Harry Kipke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sam_Willaman" title="Sam Willaman">Sam Willaman</a>. The Wolverines were led by junior quarterback <a href="/wiki/Denard_Robinson" title="Denard Robinson">Denard Robinson</a> who accounted for 337 total yards rushing and passing, and five touchdowns.</p>
<p>On November 28, 2011, <a href="/wiki/Urban_Meyer" title="Urban Meyer">Urban Meyer</a>, who had served as an assistant at Ohio State from 1986 to 1987, was named the 24th head coach for the Buckeyes, replacing Fickell.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> In the 2012 matchup, OSU's junior running back <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Hyde" title="Carlos Hyde">Carlos Hyde</a> ran for 146 yards, and the fourth-ranked Buckeye's defense shut out No. 20 Michigan in the second half to prevail, 26–21, capping a 12–0 season for OSU.</p>
<p>In 2013, Ohio State came to Michigan Stadium with an 11–0 record (7–0 Big Ten) and ranked #3 in the BCS standings. Michigan was a disappointing 7–4 (3–4 Big Ten). In spite of the seeming mismatch, the game remained close and with 32 seconds remaining Michigan scored to bring the score to 42–41. Michigan attempted a two-point conversion for the lead, but <a href="/wiki/Devin_Gardner" title="Devin Gardner">Devin Gardner</a>'s pass was intercepted in the end zone by Tyvis Powell, and Ohio State escaped with the win.</p>
<p>Michigan entered the 2014 contest with a 5–6 record and needed a win against Ohio State to become bowl eligible. Ohio State had a 10–1 record (7–0 in the Big Ten), and was holding onto hope that they would be selected as one of the top four teams in the nation and gain a spot in the first ever College Football Playoff. The game was tied at 21 until the fourth quarter when Ohio State scored three touchdowns (two on offense and one on defense) to go up 42–21. The Game ended 42–28. During the contest, OSU quarterback <a href="/wiki/J._T._Barrett" title="J. T. Barrett">J. T. Barrett</a> suffered a broken ankle and left the game in favor of third-string sophomore quarterback, <a href="/wiki/Cardale_Jones" title="Cardale Jones">Cardale Jones</a>.</p>
<p>Three days after the game, Michigan head coach Brady Hoke was fired and Michigan began searching for Hoke's successor. On December 30, 2014, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Harbaugh" title="Jim Harbaugh">Jim Harbaugh</a>, former quarterback for Michigan from 1983 to 1986, was introduced by the University of Michigan as the school's new head football coach.</p>
<p>The 2015 game, in Ann Arbor, marked Jim Harbaugh's first game as a head coach in the rivalry. His Michigan team was 9–2 coming into the game and Ohio State was 10–1. For the first time since 2006, both teams were ranked in the top 10, with Ohio State at #8 and Michigan at #10.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> The first half was close, with Michigan scoring a touchdown shortly before halftime to narrow Ohio State's lead to 14–10. Ohio State, however, scored touchdowns on each of its first 4 possessions in the second half, while Michigan could only muster a single field goal. The final score was 42–13, in favor of Ohio State.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Big_Ten_expansion">Big Ten expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michigan%E2%80%93Ohio_State_football_rivalry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Big Ten expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9313_Big_Ten_Conference_realignment" title="2010–13 Big Ten Conference realignment">2010–13 Big Ten Conference realignment</a></div>
<p>In 2010, the Big Ten Conference announced that the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Nebraska%E2%80%93Lincoln" title="University of Nebraska–Lincoln">University of Nebraska</a> would be joining the Big Ten the following year, and that the Big Ten would be split into two divisions. When rumors surfaced that Ohio State and Michigan would be placed in different divisions, concerns arose that the teams might no longer play the last game of the regular season to avoid potential back-to-back games if each team won their division and earned the right to play in the <a href="/wiki/Big_Ten_Football_Championship_Game" title="Big Ten Football Championship Game">Big Ten Championship Game</a>. Fans bombarded the athletic directors of both schools, as well as the Big Ten commissioner with emails, creating pressure to keep The Game as the regular season finale. Ultimately, Michigan was placed in the Legends Division and Ohio State in the Leaders Division. The Michigan–Ohio State game became a "protected crossover" game, to be played every year at the end of the regular season.</p>
<p>The game became a division matchup in 2014 under the Big Ten's geographical realignment. Michigan and Ohio State were both placed in the East Division.</p>

<p>During the mid-2000s, <a href="/wiki/ESPN" title="ESPN">ESPN</a> aired several commercials describing certain situations that would be "normal, if it wasn't for sports". One commercial featured a man in an Ohio State shirt <a href="/wiki/Making_out" title="Making out">making out</a> with a woman woman wearing a Michigan shirt.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/ESPNU" title="ESPNU">ESPNU</a> commercial air portrayed a couple on a <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blind_date" class="extiw" title="wikt:blind date">blind date</a> that appears to be successful until it becomes clear that she is from Michigan and he is a fan of Ohio State.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, as part of their "Midwest Midterm Midtacular", <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Show" title="The Daily Show">The Daily Show</a></i> visited The Ohio State University and made fun of the rivalry on the final night by having correspondent <a href="/wiki/Rob_Riggle" title="Rob Riggle">Rob Riggle</a> report while wearing a Michigan sweatshirt. This brought boos, jeers, and a few laughs from the OSU audience. After the sketch was over, Jon Stewart compared the rivalry to the <a href="/wiki/Shia%E2%80%93Sunni_relations" title="Shia–Sunni relations">disputes between Sunni and Shiite Muslims</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Weareredyouareblueweallmustide_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weareredyouareblueweallmustide-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2007 <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> documentary <i>Michigan vs. Ohio State: The Rivalry</i> focused on the rivalry, chiefly the "Ten-Year War" through the present.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The book <i>Myth Directions</i> by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Asprin" title="Robert Asprin">Robert Asprin</a>, who attended Michigan, features a thinly veiled version of the Ohio State vs. Michigan game, parodying both sides and their fanaticism regarding the event.</p>
<p>The 1942 movie <i><a href="/wiki/The_Male_Animal" title="The Male Animal">The Male Animal</a></i>, based on a play of the same name by Ohio State alumni <a href="/wiki/James_Thurber" title="James Thurber">James Thurber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elliott_Nugent" title="Elliott Nugent">Elliott Nugent</a>, also features a version of the game.</p>
<p>In 2011, 10-year-old Grant Reed of Columbus was diagnosed with a malignant <a href="/wiki/Brain_tumor" title="Brain tumor">brain tumor</a>. Not liking to use the word "<a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>", Reed (whose parents are both Ohio State alums and himself is an Ohio State fan) decided to call his cancer "Michigan", since "Ohio State is always going to beat Michigan". The move of calling his cancer "Michigan" got the support of his parents, Ohio State head coach <a href="/wiki/Urban_Meyer" title="Urban Meyer">Urban Meyer</a> (who Reed later got to meet), and even some Michigan fans, including Michigan head coach <a href="/wiki/Brady_Hoke" title="Brady Hoke">Brady Hoke</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup> After his last <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy" title="Chemotherapy">chemotherapy</a> treatment in 2013 which placed him in <a href="/wiki/Cure#Remission" title="Cure">remission</a>, Reed now tells people that he "beat Michigan".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Big_Ten_games">Big Ten games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michigan%E2%80%93Ohio_State_football_rivalry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Big Ten games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Big_Ten_Conference" title="Big Ten Conference">Big Ten Conference</a> play, Ohio State leads the series 47–46–4 through the 2015 meeting.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup> Michigan was a charter member of the Big Ten in 1896 before leaving the league after the 1906 football season. Ohio State began league play with the 1913 season, and Michigan returned for the 1917 season.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> The 1918 matchup between the two schools was the first between them with both as Big Ten members.</p>
